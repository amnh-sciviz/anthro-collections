Curatorial Notes: Navigating between distant islands in the Pacific is both an art and a science. In the Caroline Islands of Micronesia, people require the skills of master navigators to get from place to place. A master navigator (palu or paliuw) relies on his extensive knowledge of not only the surrounding islands but also the sun and stars, sea swells and ocean currents, cloud and wind patterns, and animal behavior. Navigating can also be dangerous, and sudden storms can endanger the lives of an entire crew. In order to prevent bad weather, master navigators use weather magic. Charms like these, known as hos, are used for protection on voyages to ward off storms, bad weather, and malevolent spirits. This hos was collected from Eurapik Atoll by John H. Brandt, who bartered for it in the late 1950s or early 1960s. According to Brandt, master navigators throughout the Caroline Islands at the time still used hos, although many traditional navigation practices were beginning to disappear. It was purchased by the museum in 1962.

The term hos refers to the spiritually-charged stingray spines that make up the legs of these charms. The carved wooden figure is mainly something for the master navigator to grasp while employing weather magic. Stingray spines are usually attached to the wooden base with coral limestone. Coconut fibers are tied around the figures waist to help summon the aid of spirits. Any Caroline Islands carver can create the figures, which are almost always in the shape of a single- or Janus-faced human torso. However, only a master navigator is allowed to use the charm.
Before a voyage, a master navigator blows a conch shell and chants to invoke the spirits associated with the sea (yalulawie) asking for good weather and a safe journey. He often conducts these chants near a coconut tree given to him by the master navigator who trained him. If necessary, he can ward off bad weather during the voyage by thrusting the charm into the wind and chanting. Because of their power, hos have usually been stored away from dwellings in canoe houses when not in use. The basket attached to this figure contains a piece of coral. The purpose of this basket remains something of a mystery to contemporary researchers. According to Brandt (1963:27), master navigators carried special arm bags woven of [sennit (coconut fiber)] cord, with a small charm of seeds and leaves attached to help remember their training. These charms were sometimes tied around a master navigators wrist (Brandt 1963).

Items like this basket are found throughout the Caroline Islands, which is made up of about 500 small coral islands. Numerous navigation schools developed throughout the islands but, due to gradual loss of navigation practices, only two of these schools remain active today. On the island of Yap-from where the museum has a significant collection of materials-shells are collected from large coral heads, and red dirt and the feather of a frigate bird are placed within it (Lin personal communication). These are called ragoyog. The red dirt is used to paint the charm before the voyage. Kjellgren (2007) also notes that hos were often accompanied by offerings such as turmeric, coconut oil, or textiles. Further research into the context of Eurapik Atoll will hopefully shed light on this baskets function.
Traditional navigation practices, including the use of hos for weather magic, are in a state of revival in the Caroline Islands. In 1990, master navigator Jesus Urupiy resurrected the Pwo navigation ceremony on Lamotrek Atoll to initiate five men into the Weriyeng navigation school. This ceremony had not been done since famed master navigator Mau Piailug was initiated in 1951. Although Urupiy passed away in 2003, the Pwo ceremony and traditional navigation practices continue in the Caroline Islands today.
