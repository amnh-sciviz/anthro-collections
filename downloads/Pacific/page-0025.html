Curatorial Notes: Largely created from nephrite jade this pendant, known as hei-tiki, has evolved into a national symbol for contemporary New Zealanders. As New Zealand's first inhabitants, the Maori arrived in double-hulled canoes from their ancestral home of Hawaiki, thought to be located among the Cook Islands or the Society Islands. Known in Maori as Aotearoa, New Zealand is the westernmost point of the Polynesian triangle reaching to Hawai’i and Easter Island, and located some 1,400 miles from Australia. The Maori people trace their lineage through tribes (iwi) that are descended from the ancestors of these maiden voyages (waka). Chiefs (rangatira) hold political authority that are empowered by knowledge of their genealogy (whakapapa) as this was integral to leadership and aristocracy. The land is significant to the people for it represented the collective power (mana) of a tribal group (iwi) and held great importance as a source of food, home and livelihood.

The hei-tiki, believed to symbolize fertility, is referred by some anthropologists as the "Polynesian Adam" and may take on a feminine or androgynous form. The Maori word hei is translated as "pendant" while tiki means "man." These symbolic objects were worn around the neck suspended from a flax cord and the eyes were usually inlaid with abalone shell and after European contact, red sealing wax as well. Considered ancestral treasures (taonga), the hei-tiki were believed to absorb the power of those who wore them, "passing" this power from one family member to another as these pendants were bestowed as gifts. Indeed, the figure itself is an ancient one that predated Maori arrival in New Zealand.

Curatorial Notes: The above is one of eight fish hooks in the Pacific Ethnology Collection from Aotearoa, the Maori word for New Zealand, and was purchased in 1905. Although hooks were made using a wide range of materials, this one was crafted using cord, wood & bone.
After migrating from their Pacific island homelands, around Ra’iatea (a part of the colonially-renamed French Polynesia known as the Society Islands), the Maori people first settled in Aotearoa around AD 1200-1300 (Newell 32-33; 35). In Maori spiritual history, the islands were created when the god, Maui, pulled Te Ika-a-Maui (Maui’s fish, The North Island) from the sea, while Te-Waka-a-Maui (Maui’s boat, The South Island) was the waka (boat) from which the god caught his fish (Orbell 115). The diverse ocean life surrounding them was a great source of food for the Maori of Aotearoa, as evidenced by the wealth of fish bones and shells found on the shores of the island. When the Maori migrated to Aotearoa, they adapted generations of fishing practices to the new materials and fish available to them. Most Maori fishing hooks were not designed to pierce the flesh like J-shaped hooks, rather, they had a matau (a circle profile) that, “rotated and locked inside the fish’s jaw,” (The Fishing Website).
The construction of fish hooks amongst the Maori changed over time due to changes in resources on the island. While the Maori historically primarily used bone or stone to construct lure shanks and one-piece bait hooks, as the moa, a native 12-ft flightless bird whose bones were primarily used to carve fish hooks, became extinct from over-hunting, the one-piece hook structure was replaced with two-piece hooks, with a separate shank and point connected by a strong, fine fiber (Starzecka 17). Other resources for fishing hooks included sea mammal bone, ivory, and even dog bones and teeth (Starzecka 17). In the nineteenth century, with the British colonization of Aotearoa, metal points replaced bone ones in both kinds of lures, while large iron hooks were modelled on customary styles of wooden and bone two-piece bait hooks (Starzecka 18). Furthermore, the Maori would often reshape the J-shaped metal hook into their own characteristic matau (The Fishing Website). They relied on a traditional fishing calendar to stay attuned to the sea, and used a mix of hooks, sinkers, and nets to catch the large quantities of fish needed to support their community.
Many spiritual traditions in the Maori culture focus on fishing, such as the Maori legends of the god, Maui, pulled Te Ika-a-Maui from the bottom of the sea using a fish hook carved from the jaw of his grandmother (Jade). Within Maori spiritual belief, the god of the sea and father of the fish, Tangaroa, is an important figure, whose energy is represented by the many moods of the sea (Orbell 182; Royal). Furthermore, Maori describe wai (water) with many names, such as waikino for stormy waters & waitapu for sacred water used (Royal). Before dangerous expeditions, Maori fishermen would honor Tangaroa with karakia (incantations) to request an abundance of fish & protection from the ocean’s waters (Orbell 182). Even the fishhook is revered; aside from its utilitarian use, the hook held symbolic meaning. Wearing a decorative fish hook around one's neck would show respect for Tangaroa, thus ensuring prosperity & safe travel over the water (Jade).

NATIVE DYEING COLOR
PACIFIC ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 80.0/ 107 A
Locale: SAMOA
Region: POLYNESIA
Country: SAMOA
Date: Early 20th Century A.D.
Material: PIGMENT
Dimensions: L:9.7 W:9 H:7.5 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1906 [PURCHASE]
Donor: ZIMMERMANN, F.L.
Keywords: DYE
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: This red dye is clay, or soft earth, that has been dug out of the ground with wooden digging sticks. The clay is then scraped directly onto a piece of tapa that is laid over a wooden upeti or design board. The dye is then rubbed into the tapa with a rag, which transfers the design on the upeti to the piece of tapa.

Researcher: Shelby Pykare. 2012

Curatorial Notes: This yellow dye is turmeric (curcuma longa), which was brought and cultivated by those who populated the islands of Polynesia. Turmeric is one of multiple natural dyes used in the process of decoration tapa. Other common dyes included the bark from the ‘o’a tree, candlenut tree or lama, mangrove or togo, and red earth.
