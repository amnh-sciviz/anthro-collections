Curatorial Notes: This carved wooden bowl descends from a long lineage of craft from Te Enana (Land of the People) that fostered the interest of traders and tourists since early contact between the Marquesas Islands and Europe. In this Eastern Pacific nation, carved bowls have historically been functional used in the home and to adorn ceremonial sites. As relations with European nations brought commerce to the islands, woodcarvings took on new lives as commodity items and art for wealthy Western travelers. By the mid-19th century, disease and inter-tribal warfare left the islands devastated. As a result, many traditions were infrequently practiced. By the 1880s, new trade relations with the United States sparked cultural revival in the islands, throughout which woodcarvers reconnected with ancestral traditions and a market once again emerged for Marquesan art (Ivory 1999, 320). During this time, full-surface low relief carvings became more feasible as trade brought metal tools to the islands. According to correspondence in the bowl's accession file, the donor was a traveler and was given the bowl in Tahiti by a woman whom he referred to as the princess of Tahiti. This woman would have been part of either the colonial government or, more likely, a member of the Pomare lineage that held power in the islands between 1791 and 1842 (Kjellgren 2007, 298). Its low relief surface ornamentation date its making to the 20th century, when it was collected and accessioned to AMNH.

While colonial woodcarving was contingent on trade with the West and economic conditions in the Marquesas, many design elements have remained constant throughout the lifespan of this tradition. In Marquesan culture, objects, people and places may contain mana (supernatural power). Acts of art-making, carried out by tuhuka (artisans) are recognized as sacred. Often art-making is accompanied by the recitation of chants and incantations, which are integral to the creative process (Kjellgren and Ivory 2005, 5). This sacredness is manifested physically in the objects through the elaborate carvings that decorate their surfaces. On this bowl, anthropomorphic and geometric patterns make up the relief pattern. These carvings depict tiki, human-like figures that recall ancestral deities. Tiki are ubiquitous in Marquesan art and are often defined by their wide goggle-like eyes, stylized limbs and robust bodies (4).

As the tradition of woodcarving has transitioned from a functional practice to an artform, Marquesan artists continue to use motifs that identify with this creative process in their islands. This means combining traditional designs with new techniques, balancing the demands of the market, and the sacred conventions that make this craft intrinsically Marquesan (Ivory 1999, 333). In the last thirty years, ties between the Marquesas and tourist destinations in Tahiti have created an outlet for woodcarvers to sustain themselves and for this cultural practice to travel throughout the world. Today, woodcarvers collectively celebrate and continue their practice through artist cooperatives, shared gallery spaces, and web platforms that promote Marquesan woodcarving as a means of livelihood, a tradition in the islands and a well-respected artform. Its low relief surface ornamentation date its making to the 20th century, when it was collected and accessioned to AMNH.

While colonial woodcarving was contingent on trade with the West and economic conditions in the Marquesas, many design elements have remained constant throughout the lifespan of this tradition. In Marquesan culture, objects, people and places may contain mana (supernatural power). Acts of art-making, carried out by tuhuka (artisans) are recognized as sacred. Often art-making is accompanied by the recitation of chants and incantations, which are integral to the creative process (Kjellgren and Ivory 2005, 5). This sacredness is manifested physically in the objects through the elaborate carvings that decorate their surfaces. On this bowl, anthropomorphic and geometric patterns make up the relief pattern. These carvings depict tiki, human-like figures that recall ancestral deities. Tiki are ubiquitous in Marquesan art and are often defined by their wide goggle-like eyes, stylized limbs and robust bodies (4).

As the tradition of woodcarving has transitioned from a functional practice to an artform, Marquesan artists continue to use motifs that identify with this creative process in their islands. This means combining traditional designs with new techniques, balancing the demands of the market, and the sacred conventions that make this craft intrinsically Marquesan (Ivory 1999, 333). In the last thirty years, ties between the Marquesas and tourist destinations in Tahiti have created an outlet for woodcarvers to sustain themselves and for this cultural practice to travel throughout the world. Today, woodcarvers collectively celebrate and continue their practice through artist cooperatives, shared gallery spaces, and web platforms that promote Marquesan woodcarving as a means of livelihood, a tradition in the islands and a well-respected artform. (399).

Curatorial Notes: This ancestor carving is made from wood, rubbed with red ochre pigment and is marked by slight scarifications across the surface. It depicts three human figures, one on top of another. Purchased in the Asmat territory in the late 1950s, this object was donated to the Museum in 1995. Originally made for trade, this ancestor carving was not intended for ceremonial use; however its form reflects certain dance rituals of Asmat people and their deep ties with nature, creating connections between contemporary people and their ancestors and traditions.

In Asmat cosmology, Fumeripitsj-- the god of creation-- first formed human beings from wood figures carved from trees. Linked at the elbows and knees, these carvings came to life as Fumeripitsj played music (Smidt 1993). In this carving, the bottom figure resembles a squatting man, the middle figure is depicted with his legs slightly bent, and the figure on the top is standing upright with straight legs. This gradual standing motion is seen in Asmat choreography, during which dancers show how the Asmat people were transformed to human beings. Like sculptures, the dancers first sit squatting on the ground. They slowly start moving their wooden limbs and then gradually stand up like the figures in the myth. The dance shows that a squatting position is associated with lifeless existence, whereas standing indicates the renewal of life. (Zee 2009, 32).

The Asmat people live in a tropical area on the southwestern coast of New Guinea. Their home is a land of swamp forest within the territory of the Republic of Indonesia. In Asmat culture, all living creatures, as well as swamps and rivers that share the land have a spiritual life. This relationship between nature and spirituality contextualizes much of Asmat art. According to the Asmat myth of creation, Mans feet are the roots of the tree, the trunk of the tree is the human trunk, its branches are his arms, its fruits the human head, and ultimately man is tree and tree is man. (Gerbrands 1967, 14). These ideologies are depicted in this wooden sculpture, as it illustrates the wood-to-flesh motion using the material from which the Asmat originated. In this way, man and tree are interchangeable entities. As the Asmat people work to preserve their wetlands, they are mindful of the ecological balance between the human and natural world and the roots that they continue to share.

Moreover, as one of the most important forms of art in Asmat culture, woodcarving is closely linked to peoples communication with the ancestors. Woodcarvings such as shields and large pieces bare special meanings and are traditionally crafted by the master carvers-- wowipits. Wow in the Asmat language means woodcarving, and ipits means man (Smidt 1993, ix). It is their belief that by creating art and a spiritual world, woodcarvers are making connections between the living and their ancestors. Most wood carvings are named for ancestors, or reference ancestors through their motifs and designs (Smidt 1993). Sculptures such as this allow the Asmat to make the spiritual world tangibile.

Curatorial Notes: Feather boxes like the one shown above hold the highly valued items of a Maori chief and are known as papahuia when they are shaped flat and rectangular, and called wakahuia when boxes take on a canoe-form. As New Zealand's first inhabitants, the Maori arrived in double-hulled canoes from their ancestral home of Hawaiki, thought to be located among the Cook Islands or the Society Islands. Known in Maori as Aotearoa, New Zealand is the westernmost point of the Polynesian triangle reaching to Hawaii and Easter Island, and located some 1,400 miles from Australia. The Maori people trace their lineage through tribes (iwi) that are descended from the ancestors of these maiden voyages (waka). Chiefs (rangatira) hold political authority that are empowered by knowledge of their genealogy (whakapapa) as this was integral to leadership and aristocracy. The land is significant to the people for it represented the collective power (mana) of a tribal group (iwi) and held great importance as a source of food, home and livelihood.

The items stored within these boxes are regarded sacred because they were worn on the owner's head, which was believed to be the body's most sacred part. The sanctity of the objects such as white heron feathers, jade and combs, made them dangerous to anyone not considered royalty thus feather boxes protect these items when not in use. Indeed, Maori decorative artwork never "interfered" with an object's purpose; its goal was to enhance the box's design and the carvings made outside of a box were most likely created by a chief. In fact, feather boxes were deemed the highest caliber of carving: No carved objects in the field of Maori art surpass them for originality, vigor, or as a demonstration of the local style (312). Thus the bases of boxes were elaborately decorated as well because they were suspended from the rafters of homes, allowing them to be admired from below.

Curatorial Notes: This tapa (bark cloth) painting was made by contemporary Marquesan artist Hina Tiaho in the 1990s. Depicting a fully tattooed warrior and complex geometric designs, this contemporary tapa speaks to the relationship between tattooing and other mediums in the Marquesas, as well as the persistence of a tradition that is grounded in a long history of changing social, political, and spiritual contexts.
This particular cloth comes from an aoa (banyan tree), and is marked by its reddish brown appearance. Typically, these cloths were considered sacred and were used to make garments for warriors and priests (Kjellgren and Ivory 2005, 12). Painted tapa was actually quite rare, and was created by men, as Marquesan men usually created art related to sacred activities. Before trade between Western nations brought cotton cloth to the islands, bark cloth was used widely used throughout the Marquesas, where textiles were considered a womens medium and a form of womens wealth. Tapa made by women was used for clothing, bedding, or domestic use (Klarr). Interestingly, it is primarily women that have fostered the tradition of tapa painting as it lives on as an artform today (Ivory 1999, 330).
In the 1950s, women in villages Omoa and Hanavave in Fatuiva revived the tradition of tapa painting inspired by scenes and designs utilized in Marquesan woodcarving and tattooing (Ivory 1999, 330). In some cases, as with this painting, contemporary artists choose to reimagine a historic image using illustrations from the past. This work offers a striking comparison to a copperplate engraving in AMNHs library collections entitled A Young Nukahiwan Not Completely Tattooed, made by German artist and scholar Wilhelm Gottlieb Tilesius von Tilenau during the Krusenstern Expedition of 1804 (Kjellgren and Ivory 2005, 55). In both the tapa and the engraving, a young warrior is depicted from behind, decorated with elaborate body tattoos, and holding trophies that mark his successes in battle. Working from such illustrations allows contemporary Marquesan artists to assert ownership of their cultural history and to accurately depict the motifs used in historic Marquesan art and tattooing.
The motifs employed in this painting are central to many art mediums in the Marquesas. With this in consideration it was brought into AMNHs collections in 1999, donated by Pacific scholar Carol S. Ivory who purchased Hina Tiaho's work in Omoa, Fatu Hiva. In this painting, Tiaho depicts a warrior that was perhaps member of the elite Marquesan society, as he would have been able to afford the time and expense to tattoo most of his body (Kjellgren and Ivory 2005, 56). While men were often fully covered in body art, women were tattooed on their hands, arms, wrists, feet, ears and lips (Schildkrout 2001). Using the medium of painted bark cloth as it continues to be practiced today, Tiahos work materializes and preserves the tradition of body art. As Marquesan art continues to gain recognition in the world, the islands designs have made their way into popular culture and, in some ways, have become widely emblematic of Polynesia, even the Pacific Islands as a whole (Kjellgren and Ivory 2005, 25).
