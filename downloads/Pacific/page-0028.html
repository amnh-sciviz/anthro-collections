Curatorial Notes: A tekoteko is carved wooden figure adorning the front of the wharenui (meeting house) above the door near the roof. Traditionally, the wharenui belonged to a hapū or whānau, communities and family groups (Moorfield). This object was donated by Major General Horatio Gordon Robley, who was known for his collection of pacific objects containing tekoteko.

A tekoteko often consists of two separate figures, one placed on top of the other. The top figure is often naturalistic in form, while the base figure is usually stout and wide. The base of the figure is where the carving is attached to the gable of the house. The large and wheku-style head, which is wider with exaggerated features, occurs frequently. This is because the head is the source of mana, sacred life energy and vitality. Also common is the haka pose; an assertive stance where the figure leans forward with its tongue protruding. In figures like these, the prominent phallus represents the fertility of the tupuna (ancestor) and the ongoing continuation of family for generations (Moore 1977). The origin of these figures comes from a story where Tangaroa, the sea god, used the body of Te Manuhauturuki as the tekoteko of his wharenui (Royal 2006).

The tekoteko at the apex of the wharenui represents an ancestor or progenitor of the people to whom the house belongs. Ancestors serve as some of the most important figures in Maori culture, helping to define culture and identity. They also are reminders of the culture’s roots and values providing guidance, wisdom, and strength to leaders in the present (Moore 1977).

Archaeological evidence such as unearthed tattoo equipment dating from early settlement times has revealed that the practice of tattooing came to Aotearoa when the Maori migrated from their Pacific island homelands in Eastern Polynesia (Starzecka 40). The moko (tattoo) is a very important, sacred aspect of Maori culture. Acting as a major component of both adornment and identity, the moko was considered a mark indicating status, rank, and mana (power) of an individual (Starzecka 61). The sites and design of moko vary between men and women, though in both sexes it represents rites of passage, significant events in ones life, and a statement of self (Starzecka 40). Men have been tattooed between the waist and knees with swirling double spirals on either flank, stretching down to the knees in a more angular design. Men also often receive face moko, a more time-consuming and exacting practice.
Women have had their moko on the upper lip and chin, with deep coloring of the lips; as well as between the eyebrows, on the forehead, and even sometimes on their breasts. The motif of the moko worn by women often includes linked crescents and spirals balanced symmetrically on either side of a blank, narrow median (Starzecka 41). Though the practice declined in the 20th century with the 1907 Tohunga Suppression Act by the government of Aotearoa which outlawed the spiritual and educational role of tohunga (Maori experts), it has experienced a resurgence in Maori culture with the political Maori renaissance of the 1970s (New Zealand).
Originally, men were only adorned with painted patterns, not permanent ones. However, according to Maori spiritual belief, Mataora, a young man from Earth, fell in love with Niwareka, a princess of the underworld, and visited her there after she fled to escape his beatings. When Mataora arrived in the underworld Uetonga, Niwarekas father, saw his painted face, wiped away the paint, and permanently tattooed Mataoras face. After Uetonga tattooed his face, Mataora returned to Earth with Niwareka and taught the art of tattooing (Starzecka 111).
The tattooing process is extensive. Different regions used slightly different techniques though all utilized the tattooing chisel. Only a few tonguna were able to carry out the process. Maori use two tattooing techniques including the puncture technique, which involves piercing the skin with a serrated bone chisel, tapped rhythmically with a wooden mallet, and another technique which involves a finely-honed chisel primarily made of either bone or nephrite jade used to create textured scarification over the skin, into which the pigment was applied by a separate serrated implement. Though this particular chisel is made of whale bone, many chisels were made of bird bone. The pigment for the moko was produced by mixing soot with light fish or dog oil, or spring water. The mixture was prepared by the burning of resin from the sacred kauri tree or aawheto (vegetable caterpillar), a native fungus, producing a sticky indigo blue-black substance that was kept in highly prized containers (Starzecka 40).
Ta moko (process of tattooing) comes with a risk for infection and unintended skin changes, thus the Maori consider the practice very tapu (sacred). For those receiving face tattoos, Maori invented the koorere, a funnel to nourish those being tattooed. In order to hasten healing, Maori placed slit leaves of the karaka tree over the incisions, played the bullroarer, a whirling musical instrument, in order to change the energy currents of the air, and recited chants (Starzecka 40).

Curatorial Notes: This musical instrument created from wood, shell, bead, and string, is one of two in the Pacific Ethnology collection from Aotearoa, the Maori word for the islands of New Zealand, and was gifted to the museum in 1907 by Morris K. Jesup.

After migrating from their Pacific island homelands, around Raiatea (a part of the colonially-renamed French Polynesia dubbed Society Islands), the Maori people first settled in Aotearoa around AD 1200-1300 (Newell, 32-33; 35). Music played a very important role in Maori culture. Traditional Maori music draws from the emotions of Maori spiritual ancestors (Flintoff). Different instruments were used for ceremonies, healing, sending messages, or marking the stages of life (Flintoff). Taonga puoro (traditional musical instrument) players are treated with great respect due to the instruments spiritual functions (Flintoff). A koaua (flute) could aid in childbirth or healing by summoning spirits (Flintoff). Accompanying a rangi (tune) with the right words could express sorrow or love, loss or joy (Flintoff).

Each taonga puoro was unique. The Maori believe in the personification of all things, so inanimate objects like taonga puoro are believed to have their own personality (Starzecka 83). To accommodate this, each instrument was made slightly differently; the finger holes would be spaced based on the owners knuckles or a favorite pattern of stars (Flintoff).

Maori music is rooted in their spiritual traditions. Rangi, short for the Sky Father Ranginui, is the word for melody in Maori language; while Papa, short for the Earth Mother Papatuanuku, provides the basis for musical rhythms (Flintoff). Melodies are believed to be carried up to Ranginui, while rhythms are representative of the heartbeats of Papatuanuku (Flintoff). Maori musical instruments are seen as gifts to its people from various gods, the descendants from Rangi and Papa (Flintoff).

Maori instruments were primarily idiophones (vibrating instruments) and aerophones (wind instruments), whereas membranophones (drums) and chordophones (string instruments) were historically uncommon in Maori culture prior to globalization (McLean 166). Flutes and trumpets make up the main Maori instruments. Maori flutes can vary between 7 and 700 millimeters in length, are commonly made with wood, human bone, or ivory; and depending on their size, and can be played with either the mouth or nose (McLean 184). The main flutes included the kooauau and the nguru (McLean 184). Trumpets were made with a variety of things, the most common being a conch-shell. There are four kinds of trumpets in Maori culture including the puu harakeke (flax trumpet), the puutaatara (shell trumpet), the puukaaea (long, wooden war trumpet), and the puutoorino (nasal trumpet) (McLean 177).

This particular musical artifact, commonly known as a bull-roarer, was used as a healing element in Maori culture. It is made of carved wood, shell, bead, and plant fiber string, making up a small elliptical disk attached to a cord (Starzecka 49). This instrument would be spun in the air while a member of the iwi (tribe) was being tattooed, both in order to calm them and to change the energy currents of the air to promote healing (Starzecha 49). Its healing properties were also extended to those suffering from bronchial illnesses and the elderly (Starzecka 49). Furthermore, the whirling instrument invoked the goddesses and gods, casted various incantments, and summoned desired weather (Starzecka 49).

Curatorial Notes: The vegetable caterpillar, also known as āwheto or āwhato, is actually a type of fungus- Ophiocordyceps robertsii (Moorfield). The caterpillars of some moth species in New Zealand, like Aoraia dinodes or Dumbletonius characterifer, become infected by this fungus when they eat the reproductive fungal spores while feeding on leaf litter. The fungus takes over the caterpillar’s body when it burrows underground to metamorphize. However, they are instead encased in a shell by the fungus. The caterpillar is dried out and the fungus feeds from its nutrients. The fungus then grows a small stem from the caterpillar’s head to the forest floor, where it releases spores to infect new caterpillars (Science Learning Hub 2015). This is one of three vegetable caterpillars found in the Pacific Ethnology Collection. They were acquired by the museum in 1907, this unique piece lends itself to a major cultural practice of the Māori during the 19th century.
This object was donated by Major General Horatio Gordon Robley. Robley’s collection contained dozens of toi moko; the preserved heads of tattooed Māori. The preservation of important figures and family members was an important ritual tradition for the Māori that was perversely turned into an object of trade during European colonization (Higgins 2013). Beyond the moko, his collection contained sections of tattooed thigh skin, the head of a child which he was aware had been tattooed after death, and the untattooed head of an infant (AMNH Division of Anthropology Manuscript Catalog, see link below this entry).
The fungus and mummified remains of the caterpillar are culturally significant to the Māori people. The āwheto were a popular source of pigmentation in traditional tā moko, Māori facial tattoos. To use the pigment, several āwheto were heated over a fire to from charcoal (Moorfield). They were then mixed with other ingredients, like muttonbird fat and charred wood. While this type of natural pigment is no longer used, the ritual tattooing is still present in modern New Zealand (Relph 1991).
Tā Moko is one of the most sacred forms of skin art. Tā Moko is specifically a facial art. Tā moko is a culturally significant, as it is meant to represent the family history of the wearer as well as Māori identity and culture. The traditional process involves cutting open the skin in a pattern, then using an uhi pure, a type of hand tool, to push the ink into the open wound to create the markings upon healing (Relph 1991).
These caterpillars were sold to New Zealand tourists at the beginning of the 20th century as curios, due to their antiseptic qualities. This is the reason for the relative commonality of the mummified hunks outside of New Zealand (Science Learning Hub 2015).
These vegetable caterpillars were put on display in 1999 in the American Museum of Natural History’s display, Body Art: Marks of Identity.
