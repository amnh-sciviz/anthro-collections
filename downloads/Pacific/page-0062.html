Curatorial Notes: This utik is a bird snare, one of over 700 objects collected from the Mountain Arapesh peoples by former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead during her 1931-33 expedition of New Guinea. Mead first encountered the Mountain Arapesh in the Torricelli Mountain range, along the Northern coast of Papua New Guinea in the East Sepik Province. Mead adapted the word “Arapesh,” which means “human” or “friend” in their language, to name the people she studied as well as the language itself (Roscoe 2004:680). Arapesh now refers to a family of languages spoken throughout East Sepik, as well as the diverse geographic and cultural groups that speak them.

Only men were permitted to make the cord used with the utik , although Mead observed that both Arapesh men and women knew how to make cord and string from sapling bark. Mead wrote, the Mountain Arapesh believed that “if a woman made [the cord], the animals would avoid the trap” (Mead 1938:268). The majority of hunting and trapping practices were done by men.

Generally, the utik consists of several yards of fine cord, tied into a slip knot to create a noose-like device. A tree branch with a desirable bird is chosen by the hunter to set up the trap, which consists of the knotted cord, a bait of berries tied to the branch, and a short releasing stick to stabilize the apparatus. The attached image below provides further detail (Figure 74, Mead 1938:293). The utik does not self-release and requires a watcher to remain concealed underneath the trap. The watcher imitates bird calls to attract prey and holds the end of the cord in his hand. He waits until the bird lands on the branch and subsequently pulls the cord in order to catch the bird’s foot in a tight loop (Mead 1938:294).

According to Mead, meat from animals, including birds, was not a substantial part of the daily Arapesh diet. However, it was an essential part of any communal gathering where feasting was involved. Special events, such as building a house or the conclusion of a mourning period, as well as a periodic surplus of special foods like meat, might initiate a feast (Mead 1938:225). Among the Arapesh, there was a general attitude that the hunter should not eat his own kill, but rather share it with others (Mead 1938:225).

Birds would have likely been used in preparations for such feasts, as well as for their feathers for trade. Birds of paradise and eagle feathers were often exported to Arapesh-speaking people on the Northern coast, whereas hornbill and yellow and white parrot feathers would be exchanged with the Plains Arapesh to the South. The Mountain Arapesh were engaged in less economic trade than bordering Arapesh groups and bird feathers were among their few exports (Mead 1938:329).

Curatorial Notes: This bird snaring cord is one of over 700 objects collected from the Mountain Arapesh by former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead during her 1931-33 expedition of New Guinea. Mead first encountered the Mountain Arapesh in the Torricelli Mountain range, along the Northern coast of Papua New Guinea in the East Sepik Province. Mead adapted the word “Arapesh,” which means “human” or “friend” in their language, to name the people she studied as well as the language itself (Roscoe 2004:680). Arapesh now refers to a family of languages spoken throughout East Sepik, as well as the diverse geographic and cultural groups that speak them.

This cord was created for an utik, a hunting trap designed to catch small to medium-sized birds. There is also an utik with cord similar to this in the museum’s collection (Catalog #80.0/ 6931). Only men were permitted to make cord used for these traps, although Mead observed that both Arapesh men and women knew how to make cord and string from sapling bark. Mead wrote, the Mountain Arapesh believed that “if a woman made it, the animals would avoid the trap” (Mead 1938:268). The majority of hunting and trapping practices were done by men.

Generally, the utik consists of several yards of this fine cord, tied into a slip knot to create a noose-like device. A tree branch with a desirable bird is chosen by the hunter to set up the trap, which consists of the knotted cord, a bait of berries tied to the branch, and a short releasing stick to stabilize the apparatus. The attached image below provides further detail (Figure 74, Mead 1938:293). The utik does not self-release and requires a watcher to remain concealed underneath the trap. The watcher imitates bird calls to attract prey and holds the end of the cord in his hand. He waits until the bird lands on the branch and subsequently pulls the cord in order to catch the bird’s foot in a tight loop (Mead 1938:294).

According to Mead, meat from animals, including birds, was not a substantial part of the daily Arapesh diet. However, it was an essential part of any communal gathering where feasting was involved. Special events, such as building a house or the conclusion of a mourning period, as well as a periodic surplus of special foods like meat, might initiate a feast (Mead 1938:225). Among the Arapesh, there was a general attitude that the hunter should not eat his own kill, but rather share it with others (Mead 1938:225).

Birds would have likely been used in preparations for such feasts, as well as for their feathers for trade. Birds of paradise and eagle feathers were often exported to Arapesh-speaking people on the Northern coast, whereas hornbill and yellow and white parrot feathers would be exchanged with the Plains Arapesh to the South. The Mountain Arapesh were engaged in less economic trade than bordering Arapesh groups and bird feathers were among their few exports (Mead 1938:329) According to anthropologists who revisited Mead’s field sites in 2007, the Mountain Arapesh continue to hunt more frequently than Plains Arapesh in recent years (Gibbs and Wailoni 2008:150).
