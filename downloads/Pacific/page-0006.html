Curatorial Notes: Malagan (also spelled malangan or malanggan) carvings are complex objects that resist easy description. Malagan traditions are unique to the peoples of New Ireland, located off the Northeast coast of Papua New Guinea. They are most commonly associated with New Ireland mortuary rites, but are also affiliated with the men’s initiation ceremonies. The term malagan refers to both the carvings and masks used in these ceremonies, as well as to the ceremonies themselves. The practices associated with malagan vary widely and the carving traditions are much less prevalent in New Ireland today. Thus, malagan are difficult to write about with certainty.

Louise Lincoln (1987:16) astutely describes malagan, saying:

"It is easy to assert that malagan sculptures and ceremonies are ‘about’ many things at once…Malagans are about the memory of the deceased, about families, hierarchies, power, and social organization. They are about the maintenance of social order and networks of interdependence; they are about myth and history, the economic status of their owners, about aesthetic preferences and cultural change. And at their most abstract level, they are concerned with philosophical speculation of a very high order; mirroring their own most visible characteristics, they are about complexity, subtlety, and change."

Much like a copyright system, individuals in New Ireland can acquire the rights to certain malagan images, which are carved and displayed at their funerals. Malagan carvings make up a visual compendium of the deceased person’s life and achievements. Horizontal friezes like this one were most commonly placed in men’s houses or on top of poles for the mortuary ceremonies (Lincoln 1987:16). Once the final funerary ceremonies have finished—often many months after the person has died—the malagan have fulfilled their purpose. The carvings are either left to rot or sold to outsiders.

Birds, snakes and fish are the most common animals carved on malagan, as they correspond to some of the most important totemic animals for the people of New Ireland. A large bird, likely a combination of both a rooster and a hornbill, dominates this carving. There are also two human figures carved at either end of the bird—one upside down below the beak and one upright beneath the tail feathers. Two fish meet at the bird’s feet. A section at the base near the feet of the bird has been hollowed out, meaning it was probably displayed on top of a pole or standing figure.

This particular carving was collected by Otto Finsch in 1882 from northern New Ireland during an expedition during the time in which he was appointed Imperial Commissioner of New Guinea for Germany. The precise location in New Ireland from which this carving was collected is unclear, but it was likely near the present-day city of Kavieng, the capital of New Ireland province. In his notes, Finsch wrote that it had been displayed in a men’s meeting house. According to Finsch, the local name for carvings like this one was kulap, which is likely one of the many hundreds of names for different types of malagan. Further research into this malagan's specific context may illuminate its creator or place of origin. Finsch sold it to the AMNH along with roughly 1,200 other pieces in 1898.
