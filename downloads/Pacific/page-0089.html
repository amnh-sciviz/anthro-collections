Curatorial Notes: This Maori bag, donated in 1952 by the Dominion Museum in Aotearoa (the Maori name of New Zealand), is called kete. It is one example of the seventy different varieties of Maori woven bags that range from large totes to small handbags (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:47). Each kete serves a specific purpose such as gathering, transporting, or storing foodstuff and personal items (Starzecka 1996:121). Usually made from New Zealand flax, the weaving style implemented in kete production is known as plaiting (raranga), a practice that involves diagonally knitting strips of flax fiber (Starzecka 1996:119). Variations on raranga patterns are specific to family and iwi (tribal lineages), passed from mother to daughter during the teaching process (Puketapu-Hetat 1989:53).
New Zealand flax is actually not part of the flax family but the lily family; its similarity to Irish flax could have caused this misnomer to emerge during the colonial era (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:9). There are two species of New Zealand flax: wharakiki, soft and only used for intricate weaves, and harakeke, strong and therefore the most common weaving flax (Starzecka 1996:116). Harakeke is prepared by first removing the edges and ribs of the leaves before splitting them into strips. These strips are then softened by knives, shells, or rocks (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:20-21).
Maori epistemology explains that kete originated from Tane, the creator of the world. Tane brought down three kete from the sky, each containing sacred knowledge of the worlds order and wisdom (Orbell 1998:49). Additionally, there are various weaving origins with a rich diversity of lore across regions and iwi. One such origin is that weaving came from patupaiarehe (fairy-like beings) who reside on hilltops and other secluded areas away from sunlight. Humans tricked the patupaiarehe by distracting them as they weaved throughout the night and into the day. As the sun rose they fled, leaving behind woven materials for humans to study (Orbell 1998:20).
Predominantly a womans task (Starzecka 1996:114), weavers are more than skillful artists; weavers possess a spiritual connection to the gods and ancestors (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:2). A weaver is initiated into Te Whare Pora, the House of Weaving, when she is ready to study the craft intensely. The popularity of these initiation ceremonies decreased during colonial times because European missionaries deemed that the rite was anti-Christian (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:24).
Colonization, urbanization, and dispossession of Maori communities in the twentieth century alongside introduction of European textiles and endangerment of native plants caused a decline in Maori weaving (Te Kanawa 2014). Although it was discouraged, the practice was never forgotten. Women, like Diggeress Te Kanawa, promoted weaving traditions in the 1950s by founding organizations like Te Roopu Raranga Whatu o Aotearoa (National Maori Weavers Collective of New Zealand) (Te Kanawa 2014). Weavings ability to incorporate new fibres, patterns, and techniques ensure its sustained practice; and, with the appointment of guardians, the customs of the long-standing tradition are maintained (Te Kanawa 2014).
