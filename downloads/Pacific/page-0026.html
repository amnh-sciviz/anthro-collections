Curatorial Notes: This comb, known as selu tuiga, originates from Samoa. Located in the middle of the Polynesian triangle, Samoa is comprised of two distinct nations: Independent Samoa (usually referred to as Samoa) and American Samoa (a U.S. territory). Over 200,000 people inhabit the major islands in these two nations, in addition to surrounding groups of islands, and a significant Samoan diaspora community occupy New Zealand. Samoan society is based upon chiefly (matai) titles in which each village is governed by a fono, a gathering of all chiefs as each family retained its own chiefly titles, and decisions made by the fono are binding for all members although Samoan court systems also play significant roles in maintaining social order now. Samoa's wealth is reflected in the fine mats and barkcloth produced by village women, and these objects are important in ceremonial exchanges during marriages, births and funerals, as well as at the validations of matai titles, which are known as fa’alavelave. Combs, such as the one on display, were made from dried, cleaned and rounded coconut leaf midribs, lashed together with fine sennit (coconut fiber) and were used solely as decorative items often adorned with multicolored glass beads that were likely acquired from trade with Europeans.

Curatorial Notes: The dancing girdle seen above originates from Samoa. It was created from hibiscus fiber and intricately decorated with rosettes around its waistband and dyed in bright colors. Located in the middle of the Polynesian triangle, Samoa is comprised of two distinct nations: Independent Samoa (usually referred to as Samoa) and American Samoa (a U.S. territory). Over 200,000 people inhabit the major islands in these two nations, in addition to surrounding groups of islands, and a significant Samoan diaspora community occupy New Zealand. Samoan society is based upon chiefly (matai) titles in which each village is governed by a fono, a gathering of all chiefs as each family retained its own chiefly titles, and decisions made by the fono are binding for all members although Samoan court systems also play significant roles in maintaining social order now. Samoa's wealth is reflected in the fine mats and barkcloth produced by village women, and these objects are important in ceremonial exchanges during marriages, births and funerals, as well as at the validations of matai titles, which are known as fa’alavelave. The girdle on display here was worn during performances as a component of a dancer's costume. Samoan dance differs from other Polynesian performances in that the individual is emphasized over the group and in fact, everyone is encouraged and expected to dance in Samoan society. According to anthropologist Margaret Mead, who did fieldwork in Samoa from 1925 to 1926 and worked at the American Museum of Natural History from 1926 until her death in 1978, dancing serves two purposes: It “offsets the rigorous subordination in which children are kept,” making them the center of attention while encouraging their individuality, and reduces the "threshold of shyness” which ensures that they will dance when encouraged.

Researcher: Shelby Pykare, 2012
