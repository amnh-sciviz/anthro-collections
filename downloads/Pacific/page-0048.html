Curatorial Notes: This drill was donated by Adolph Zukor and J.J Lasky, co-founders of Paramount Pictures, in 1926 (Rotha 1983). It was originally collected by David Flaherty, brother to the explorer turned filmmaker Robert Flaherty who first made his name with the release of Nanook of the North in 1922 (Zimmerman 2013). Impressed with his first film, Paramount commissioned Flaherty to travel to Samoa in 1923 and make the film Moana (1926) (Rotha 1983). David Flaherty accompanied his brother to Savai’i, an island in Samoa, and collected items like this one to take back with him.

Moana documents a Samoan chief’s son and family performing daily activities of fishing, hunting, and harvesting. While these scenes accurately portray the daily life on the island, some scenes, like the final tattooing scene, were staged (Zimmerman 2013). The tattooing tradition was once practiced in Samoa as a initiation rite, but at the time of Flaherty’s filming it was no longer implemented. However, to provide the film with a dramatic climax, this rite and several other scenes were included (Zimmerman 2013). Nevertheless, Flaherty’s works continue to be influential in both anthropology and media studies (Rotha 1983).

This drill, also known as a “pump drill”, consists of a wooden bar with a shaft passing through its center. A cord is attached from the top of the shaft to opposite ends of the bar. During use the cord is wrapped around the shaft so when the user presses down on the bar the shaft spins. This causes friction with the object resulting in a hole being drilled. Pump drills replaced earlier shaft drills, a more simple version of this tool (Hedley 1897).

A pump drill was depicted in Flaherty’s Moana during the turtle hunting scene. After capturing the sea turtle, the chief's son punctures a hole in the turtle’s shell in order for a rope to be tied to the turtle and then transported (Flaherty 1926).

Sea Turtles, laumei, have long been a special food source in Samoa. Turtles are usually caught during hunting expeditions that are arranged ahead of time and excitedly anticipated (Grattan 1985). Once a turtle is caught it is presented to the chief and orators who then divide the animal to distribute it amongst the community’s families. The cooking and distribution of the turtle is called i’a sa, meaning ‘sacred or forbidden fish’. This term also refers to sharks and other sea animals that require similar care (Grattan 1985). Sea turtle species found in Samoan waters are the hawksbill and green turtle, both are now endangered and protected by the U.S Endangered Species Act (Mederos 2009).

Curatorial Notes: This Savai’i cup is the only cup in the Moana Collection. This object was collected by David Flaherty and donated by two founders of Paramount Pictures, Adolph Zukor and Jesse L. Lasky, Jr. in 1926. In Moana, Flaherty’s film about daily life on the Samoan island of Savai’i, coconut cups like this one can be seen used for a variety of purposes. A coconut cup is used to help dampen sandalwood, a seed used to paint cloth made from mulberry tree bark. Another use depicted in the film is to ladle coconut cream in preparation for the feast. Cups are also used to hold coconut custard and Kava, a drink used in Moana’s coming of age ceremony . Throughout the film, coconuts can also be seen used as bowls, jugs, fire starters, and food (Flaherty 1926).

In Samoa, coconuts are one of six main crops (Watters 1958). One reason for their wide use is because of their abundance with it being rare to be further than 30 minutes away from a coconut tree. Some variety of coconut trees live up to 70 years baring 10-60 nuts a year, year round; thus, they are an abundant, replenishing resource. With this abundance, coconuts are essential not only because they are a staple in Samoan food, but also because of their many practical uses. The strong wood of a coconut tree is used to build houses, rollers for canoes, headrests, and firewood. The leaves are made into baskets, blinds, trays, fans, hats, mats, shoes, toys and roofs. The fibers on the outer layer of the coconut are used to make Sennit, a cord used in houses and boats. The coconut shell can be used to make utensils, bottles, dishes, game pieces, and fish hooks. Oil for the coconut kernel can be used for many cosmetic purposes including washing hair, oiling skin, and making perfume. The oil is also used on cuts and wounds as well as on the chest to help with coughs (Holmes 1974). In order to keep up with the demand for coconuts, Samoans planted coconut groves that tended to be around an acer in size to maintain a steady supply of coconuts (Watters 1958).

Formerly known as Western Samoa, the islands have since been renamed after gaining their independence from New Zealand in 1963. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. These trumpets are made from conch shells, usually Charonia tritonis or Cassis cornuta. In Samoa, these trumpets are used for signaling rather than as a musical instrument (Moyle). Some examples of their use are “evening curfews, village and church meetings, evening prayers, and the several stages of fishing expeditions” (McLean). They are also blown for shows, war signals (Williamson), calling children to school, and to announce the appearance of the Pleiades, a star formation that is one of many astrological and environmental signs the Samoans would look to (McLean; Lefale). In Flaherty’s Moana, a villager is seen blowing a conch trumpet to “gather the village to their ceremonial dance,” to celebrate Moana’s coming of age (Flaherty).

Important chiefs would have a conch trumpet blown to signify their arrival, while priests would blow it before a war (Williamson). In the case of war, depending on weather the trumpets sound was clear or rough was thought to predict the outcome (McLean). The Samoan chief system is called the Fa’amatai, with the head of each village being the matai. The matai can be either male or female and is the head of the aiga, extended family. Each village follows a similar structure and has a matai who forms a larger community amongst villages (Va’a).

Sandals (se’e vae) were not frequently used by Samoans, only being worn for protection when a foot was injured or when an individual was required to walk on sharp reefs (Hiroa 1930). Anthropologist Hiroa notes in his book Samoan Material Culture (1930) that Samoans claimed sandals to be both uncomfortable and unnecessary because of the increase in medical services on the islands.

There are three kinds of sandals worn in Samoa, each differing in material and design. This sandal specifically is the pula. The base of the shoe is a coconut husk and fau bast, or a soft and flexible plant fibre (Editors of Encyclopedia Britannica 2017), that wraps over the top of the foot to keep the sandal in place (Hiroa 1930).

Curatorial Notes: This Maori bag, donated to the American Museum of Natural History in 1926, is called kete. It is one example of the seventy different varieties of Maori woven bags that range from large totes to small handbags (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:47). Each kete serves a specific purpose such as gathering, transporting, or storing foodstuff and personal items (Starzecka 1996:121). Usually made from New Zealand flax, the weaving style implemented in kete production is known as plaiting (raranga), a practice that involves diagonally knitting strips of flax fiber (Starzecka 1996:119). Variations on raranga patterns are specific to family and iwi (tribal lineages), passed from mother to daughter during the teaching process (Puketapu-Hetat 1989:53).
New Zealand flax is actually not part of the flax family but the lily family; its similarity to Irish flax could have caused this misnomer to emerge during the colonial era (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:9). There are two species of New Zealand flax: wharakiki, soft and only used for intricate weaves, and harakeke, strong and therefore the most common weaving flax (Starzecka 1996:116). Harakeke is prepared by first removing the edges and ribs of the leaves before splitting them into strips. These strips are then softened by knives, shells, or rocks (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:20-21).
Maori epistemology explains that kete originated from Tane, the creator of the world. Tane brought down three kete from the sky, each containing sacred knowledge of the world’s order and wisdom (Orbell 1998:49). Additionally, there are various weaving origins with a rich diversity of lore across regions and iwi. One such origin is that weaving came from patupaiarehe (fairy-like beings) who reside on hilltops and other secluded areas away from sunlight. Humans tricked the patupaiarehe by distracting them as they weaved throughout the night and into the day. As the sun rose they fled, leaving behind woven materials for humans to study (Orbell 1998:20).
Predominantly a woman’s task (Starzecka 1996:114), weavers are more than skillful artists; weavers possess a spiritual connection to the gods and ancestors (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:2). A weaver is initiated into Te Whare Pora, the House of Weaving, when she is ready to study the craft intensely. The popularity of these initiation ceremonies decreased during colonial times because European missionaries deemed that the rite was anti-Christian (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:24).
Colonization, urbanization, and dispossession of Maori communities in the twentieth century alongside introduction of European textiles and endangerment of native plants caused a decline in Maori weaving (Te Kanawa 2014). Although it was discouraged, the practice was never forgotten. Women, like Diggeress Te Kanawa, promoted weaving traditions in the 1950’s by founding organizations like Te Roopu Raranga Whatu o Aotearoa (National Maori Weavers Collective of New Zealand) (Te Kanawa 2014). Weaving’s ability to incorporate new fibres, patterns, and techniques ensure its sustained practice; and, with the appointment of guardians, the customs of the long-standing tradition are maintained (Te Kanawa 2014).

Curatorial Notes: This Maori bag, donated to the American Museum of Natural History in 1927, is called kete. It is one example of the seventy different varieties of Maori woven bags that range from large totes to small handbags (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:47). Each kete serves a specific purpose such as gathering, transporting, or storing foodstuff and personal items (Starzecka 1996:121). Usually made from New Zealand flax, the weaving style implemented in kete production is known as plaiting (raranga), a practice that involves diagonally knitting strips of flax fiber (Starzecka 1996:119). Variations on raranga patterns are specific to family and iwi (tribal lineages), passed from mother to daughter during the teaching process (Puketapu-Hetat 1989:53).
New Zealand flax is actually not part of the flax family but the lily family; its similarity to Irish flax could have caused this misnomer to emerge during the colonial era (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:9). There are two species of New Zealand flax: wharakiki, soft and only used for intricate weaves, and harakeke, strong and therefore the most common weaving flax (Starzecka 1996:116). Harakeke is prepared by first removing the edges and ribs of the leaves before splitting them into strips. These strips are then softened by knives, shells, or rocks (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:20-21).
Maori epistemology explains that kete originated from Tane, the creator of the world. Tane brought down three kete from the sky, each containing sacred knowledge of the world’s order and wisdom (Orbell 1998:49). Additionally, there are various weaving origins with a rich diversity of lore across regions and iwi. One such origin is that weaving came from patupaiarehe (fairy-like beings) who reside on hilltops and other secluded areas away from sunlight. Humans tricked the patupaiarehe by distracting them as they weaved throughout the night and into the day. As the sun rose they fled, leaving behind woven materials for humans to study (Orbell 1998:20).
Predominantly a woman’s task (Starzecka 1996:114), weavers are more than skillful artists; weavers possess a spiritual connection to the gods and ancestors (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:2). A weaver is initiated into Te Whare Pora, the House of Weaving, when she is ready to study the craft intensely. The popularity of these initiation ceremonies decreased during colonial times because European missionaries deemed that the rite was anti-Christian (Puketapu-Hetet 1989:24).
Colonization, urbanization, and dispossession of Maori communities in the twentieth century alongside introduction of European textiles and endangerment of native plants caused a decline in Maori weaving (Te Kanawa 2014). Although it was discouraged, the practice was never forgotten. Women, like Diggeress Te Kanawa, promoted weaving traditions in the 1950’s by founding organizations like Te Roopu Raranga Whatu o Aotearoa (National Maori Weavers Collective of New Zealand) (Te Kanawa 2014). Weaving’s ability to incorporate new fibres, patterns, and techniques ensure its sustained practice; and, with the appointment of guardians, the customs of the long-standing tradition are maintained (Te Kanawa 2014).
