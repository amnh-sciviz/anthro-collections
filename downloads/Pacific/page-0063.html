Curatorial Notes: This tipakowgihe, or cassowary bone dagger, is one of over 600 objects collected from the Abelam peoples by former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead during her 1931-33 expedition of the East Sepik Province of Papua New Guinea. Mead encountered the Abelam in the grassy plains between the southern foothills of the Torricelli Range and the Sepik River. From their borders, Mead traded heavily for Abelam material culture throughout her fieldwork with the Mountain Arapesh to the North (see culture sheet for more details), believing it to be the finest craftsmanship in the region.

In particular, Mead considered Abelam incised bonework to be the most elaborate of the Sepik region (Mead 1938:309). Among many Sepik peoples, it was a common practice to carve cassowary tibiotarsi and tarsometatarsi (leg and foot bones), as well as, to some extent, human femurs, into intricately incised or engraved daggers. The cassowary, the third largest flightless bird in the world and a species native to New Guinea, is central to origin stories of many Sepik peoples. Their bones are a durable material and they also hold particular cosmological significance (see attached PDF for details).

The tipakowgihe is a style of dagger carved from a cassowary tibiotarsus. The distal end of the tibiotarsus forms the head of the dagger and the condyles are often cut so that the outer surfaces are in line with the shaft (Newton 1989). These outer surfaces are often pierced and plant fibers or feathers are attached, or small bird bones are inserted through both holes to attach the two condyles (Newton 1989). The sides of the cut joint are frequently engraved with a subspiral line to signify a parrot’s beak and head (Newton 1989). Below the carved joint, the posterior surface of the bone is removed to a point at the end and the anterior surface is incised with elaborate designs down the shaft (Newton 1989). Cassowary bone daggers do not generally have handles and are the length of the bone. Since they are intended to be used as stabbing weapons, they do not have sharpened edges but instead needle-pointed tips (Newton 1989). Those with duller tips were likely intended for ceremony, while sharper ones would have been used as weapons.

Among the Abelam, cassowary bone daggers played a vital role in ceremonial life, particularly in men’s cult initiation ceremonies. In stages of initiation, young Abelam males are introduced to increasingly powerful spiritual-beings embodied in carved figures, sounds, and paintings kept in the kurambu, or the male spirit house (Scaglion 2013). In some instances, cassowary daggers were displayed to new initiates among other ritual objects as cult secrets. Part of the legend and custom of this group has been passed down and it is said that upon seeing the powerful cassowary daggers in the past, younger initiates would be driven mad, seize the dagger, and slip outside the kurambu in search of a sacrificial victim from a rival group (Tuzin 1980:229). Cassowary daggers are also used as personal adornment, displayed and exchanged as tokens of peace and obligation between male members of the cult (Newton 1989:316).

Curatorial Notes: This tipakowgihe, or cassowary bone dagger, is one of over 600 objects collected from the Abelam peoples by former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead during her 1931-33 expedition of the East Sepik Province of Papua New Guinea. Mead encountered the Abelam in the grassy plains between the southern foothills of the Torricelli Range and the Sepik River. From their borders, Mead traded heavily for Abelam material culture throughout her fieldwork with the Mountain Arapesh to the North (see culture sheet for more details), believing it to be the finest craftsmanship in the region.

In particular, Mead considered Abelam incised bonework to be the most elaborate of the Sepik region (Mead 1938:309). Among many Sepik peoples, it was a common practice to carve cassowary tibiotarsi and tarsometatarsi (leg and foot bones), as well as, to some extent, human femurs, into intricately incised or engraved daggers. The cassowary, the third largest flightless bird in the world and a species native to New Guinea, is central to origin stories of many Sepik peoples. Their bones are a durable material and they also hold particular cosmological significance (see attached PDF for details).

The tipakowgihe is a style of dagger carved from a cassowary tibiotarsus. The distal end of the tibiotarsus forms the head of the dagger and the condyles are often cut so that the outer surfaces are in line with the shaft (Newton 1989). These outer surfaces are often pierced and plant fibers or feathers are attached, or small bird bones are inserted through both holes to attach the two condyles (Newton 1989). The sides of the cut joint are frequently engraved with a subspiral line to signify a parrot’s beak and head (Newton 1989). Below the carved joint, the posterior surface of the bone is removed to a point at the end and the anterior surface is incised with elaborate designs down the shaft (Newton 1989). Cassowary bone daggers do not generally have handles and are the length of the bone. Since they are intended to be used as stabbing weapons, they do not have sharpened edges but instead needle-pointed tips (Newton 1989). Those with duller tips were likely intended for ceremony, while sharper ones would have been used as weapons.

Among the Abelam, cassowary bone daggers played a vital role in ceremonial life, particularly in men’s cult initiation ceremonies. In stages of initiation, young Abelam males are introduced to increasingly powerful spiritual-beings embodied in carved figures, sounds, and paintings kept in the kurambu, or the male spirit house (Scaglion 2013). In some instances, cassowary daggers were displayed to new initiates among other ritual objects as cult secrets. Part of the legend and custom of this group has been passed down and it is said that upon seeing the powerful cassowary daggers in the past, younger initiates would be driven mad, seize the dagger, and slip outside the kurambu in search of a sacrificial victim from a rival group (Tuzin 1980:229). Cassowary daggers are also used as personal adornment, displayed and exchanged as tokens of peace and obligation between male members of the cult (Newton 1989:316).

Curatorial Notes: This suwega, or claw shaped cassowary dagger, is one of over 600 objects collected from the Abelam peoples by former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead during her 1931-33 expedition of the East Sepik Province of Papua New Guinea. Mead encountered the Abelam in the grassy plains between the southern foothills of the Torricelli Range and the Sepik River. From their borders, Mead traded heavily for Abelam material culture throughout her fieldwork with the Mountain Arapesh to the North.

Mead considered Abelam incised bonework to be the most elaborate of the Sepik region (Mead 1938:309). Among many Sepik peoples, it was a common practice to carve cassowary tibiotarsi and tarsometatarsi (leg and foot bones), as well as, to some extent, human femurs, into intricately incised or engraved daggers. The cassowary, the third largest flightless bird in the world and a species native to New Guinea, is central to origin stories of many Sepik peoples. Their bones are a durable material and they also hold particular cosmological significance.

The suwega is a type of cassowary bone dagger unique to the Abelam and carved from a cassowary tarsometatarsus. It appears claw shaped, but each “claw” is also likely meant to signify a pair of parrots’ heads. As art historian Douglas Newton contends, the suwega joint is notched vertically on its three ridges, each of which are carved into a pair of parrot heads that face the posterior side of the bone. Below the carved joint, the anterior surface of the bone is removed and squared off and the posterior surface is engraved with designs (Newton 1989). Cassowary bone daggers do not generally have handles and are the length of the bone. Since they are intended to be used as stabbing weapons, they do not have sharpened edges but instead needle-pointed tips (Newton 1989). Those with duller tips were likely intended for ceremony, while sharper ones would have been used as weapons. It is also possible that tarsometatarsal bones such as this may have been mislabeled as daggers, as they are relatively thinner than the tibiotarsi and show traces of being tools for peeling and splitting instead (Newton 1989:309).

Among the Abelam, cassowary bone daggers played a vital role in ceremonial life, particularly in men’s cult initiation ceremonies. In stages of initiation, young Abelam males are introduced to increasingly powerful spiritual-beings embodied in carved figures, sounds, and paintings kept in the kurambu, or the male spirit house (Scaglion 2013). In some instances, cassowary daggers were displayed to new initiates among other ritual objects as cult secrets. Part of the legend and custom of this group has been passed down and it is said that upon seeing the powerful cassowary daggers in the past, younger initiates would be driven mad, seize the dagger, and slip outside the kurambu in search of a sacrificial victim from a rival group (Tuzin 1980:229). Cassowary daggers are also used as personal adornment, displayed and exchanged as tokens of peace and obligation between male members of the cult (Newton 1989:316).
