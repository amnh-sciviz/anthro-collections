Curatorial Notes: Feather boxes like the one shown above hold the highly valued items of a Maori chief and are known as papahuia when they are shaped flat and rectangular, and called wakahuia when boxes take on a canoe-form. As New Zealand's first inhabitants, the Maori arrived in double-hulled canoes from their ancestral home of Hawaiki, thought to be located among the Cook Islands or the Society Islands. Known in Maori as Aotearoa, New Zealand is the westernmost point of the Polynesian triangle reaching to Hawaii and Easter Island, and located some 1,400 miles from Australia. The Maori people trace their lineage through tribes (iwi) that are descended from the ancestors of these maiden voyages (waka). Chiefs (rangatira) hold political authority that are empowered by knowledge of their genealogy (whakapapa) as this was integral to leadership and aristocracy. The land is significant to the people for it represented the collective power (mana) of a tribal group (iwi) and held great importance as a source of food, home and livelihood.

The items stored within these boxes are regarded sacred because they were worn on the owner's head, which was believed to be the body's most sacred part. The sanctity of the objects such as white heron feathers, jade and combs, made them dangerous to anyone not considered royalty thus feather boxes protect these items when not in use. Indeed, Maori decorative artwork never "interfered" with an object's purpose; its goal was to enhance the box's design and the carvings made outside of a box were most likely created by a chief. In fact, feather boxes were deemed the highest caliber of carving: No carved objects in the field of Maori art surpass them for originality, vigor, or as a demonstration of the local style (312). Thus the bases of boxes were elaborately decorated as well because they were suspended from the rafters of homes, allowing them to be admired from below.

Curatorial Notes: Feather boxes like the one shown above hold the highly valued items of a Maori chief and are known as papahuia when they are shaped flat and rectangular, and called wakahuia when boxes take on a canoe-form. As New Zealand's first inhabitants, the Maori arrived in double-hulled canoes from their ancestral home of Hawaiki, thought to be located among the Cook Islands or the Society Islands. Known in Maori as Aotearoa, New Zealand is the westernmost point of the Polynesian triangle reaching to Hawaii and Easter Island, and located some 1,400 miles from Australia. The Maori people trace their lineage through tribes (iwi) that are descended from the ancestors of these maiden voyages (waka). Chiefs (rangatira) hold political authority that are empowered by knowledge of their genealogy (whakapapa) as this was integral to leadership and aristocracy. The land is significant to the people for it represented the collective power (mana) of a tribal group (iwi) and held great importance as a source of food, home and livelihood.

The items stored within these boxes are regarded sacred because they were worn on the owner's head, which was believed to be the body's most sacred part. The sanctity of the objects such as white heron feathers, jade and combs, made them dangerous to anyone not considered royalty thus feather boxes protect these items when not in use. Indeed, Maori decorative artwork never "interfered" with an object's purpose; its goal was to enhance the box's design and the carvings made outside of a box were most likely created by a chief. In fact, feather boxes were deemed the highest caliber of carving: No carved objects in the field of Maori art surpass them for originality, vigor, or as a demonstration of the local style (312). Thus the bases of boxes were elaborately decorated as well because they were suspended from the rafters of homes, allowing them to be admired from below.

Researcher: Shelby Pykare, 2012

Source
