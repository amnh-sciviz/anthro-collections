Curatorial Notes: This figure is one of over 250 objects collected from the Murik peoples by the American anthropologist Louis Pierre Ledoux during his five months of fieldwork in Kaup, Papua New Guinea in 1935. Ledoux was sent to Kaup under the direction of Former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead. The village of Kaup is located 40 miles west of the Sepik River in the estuary on the northern coast of the East Sepik Province. The term Murik refers to the people living in Kaup and several other nearby villages, as well as the language they speak. Ledoux ultimately joined his family’s chemical business instead of pursuing a doctorate in anthropology. His two manuscripts and field notes from this work remain unpublished in the Museum’s archives.

Mead distinguishes the Sepik culture area by several “traits” including men’s houses (tok pisin: haus tambera) ceremonial masks (tumbuan) and diverse supernatural objects (tamberan) placed in the houses and used for initiation rites, most often taking the form of bamboo flutes (Lutkehaus 1990). Tamberan societies reinforce authority and social ties such as solidarity and loyalty (Lewis 1990), provide common ground, and are a unifying intercultural force in the region (Schindlbeck 1990). The process “strengthens” men, allowing them “to be motivated to the tasks appropriate to their gender and to possess the talents necessary for success in these tasks” (Roscoe 1990). Sepik river initiations have reacted in a diversity of ways to the intrusion of European powers, with the Murik now only occasionally performing a flute ceremony for firstborn sons (Roscoe & Scaglion 1990).

These intense and elaborate trials impart social distinction, strengthen bonds amongst peers, and encode collective memory, similar to “hazing” techniques among Western adolescents (Young 1985). According to Ledoux’s field notes, initiates are introduced to the powerful ngwaalndu, or ancestral clan spirits, embodied in the carvings and paintings lining the tamberan house. Only adult men can enter tamberan houses that store the secret paraphernalia of the cult (Mead 1938). The practices of tamberan societies differ among groups throughout the Sepik. The most prevalent external framework for understanding these rituals is that they formalize and publicize changes in social identity, often employing a metaphor of being eaten and reborn (Roscoe).

Ledoux describes the use of this specific object in the initiation: “To enter the ceremony the father pays [for] a pig to allow him to make, or repaint and ornament a Kandimbwag [figure]. This figure may be of any size, even a life size… the Kandimbwag are then installed in the House Tamberan.” (Ledoux)

Curatorial Notes: Tongan barkcloths are traditionally made from the inner bark of the paper mulberry tree, Broussonetia papyrifera, known in Tongan as hiapo. The bark is beaten with a hardwood mallet on an anvil until its fibres spread to form supple sheets. Women usually beat bark alone, but fall in to a rhythm with their audible - albeit unseen - neighbours. The ringing of their mallets and anvils creates a soundscape ubiquitous in Tonga; tapa beating ceases only on Sundays and during the observation of a period of mourning, and is akin to the beating heart of the Tongan nation. Groups of women come together to paste their sheets of beaten bark into double-layered fibrous cloths, and decorate them with motifs commemorating important events, people, places and things through naturalistic or figurative depictions and the important Tongan allusory technique called heliaki (to say or illustrate one thing but mean another).
