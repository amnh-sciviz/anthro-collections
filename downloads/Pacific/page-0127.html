Curatorial Notes: This carving was made from one piece of wood, including the small rat sitting on the octopus’s head; cowry shells are the only other material utilized, as the eyes of the octopus. The sculpture is fairly large given that it is considered ‘tourist art’—purchased by Dr. Paula Glick in 1972 from the New Guinea Primitive Arts Shop in Sydney, Australia and donated to AMNH in 2001 (AMNH Archives). Little information remains available on the shop that this piece was purchased in, the documentation only noting that it was run by a mission of some kind. The archives do provide the name of the artist, Kuki Sii, and reveal that this person was Tongan—a member of the Kingdom of Tonga. While more about this person is currently not known, looking at Tongan culture we are able to suggest a few things. Because carving has historically been considered a male-gendered occupation in Tonga, it is likely that Kuki Sii was male, and therefore probable that he was part of a family of carvers—with skills and knowledge passed down father to son. The fact that the piece was purchased in Australia in the 1970s also suggests that the artist was part of a substantial emigration period that was underway at that time and continues today—during which large groups of Pacific islanders moved to other countries, such as Australia and the United States.

While the diaspora of the Tongan people is tied to economic and environmental issues within the Kingdom of Tonga, it is also part of a long-standing tradition of cultural mobility. “Tongans were not confined to Tonga geographically”, Keith St. Cartmail states in his historical overview of Tongan culture, The Art of Tonga, (1997); and the statement is just as true for Tongan citizens today. The Kingdom of Tonga is comprised of three island groups within Western Polynesia in the South Pacific; its neighbors are Fiji and Samoa, two islands with which Tonga shares many cultural characteristics and historically, their complex relationships have involved exchanges of goods and marriage partners. While the island cultures may have points of connection, this does not discount specific island traditions and specializations—St. Cartmail notes, “The fact that many of these breastplates and other artifacts were actually made in Fiji by Tongan...artists, clearly this makes them Tongan, not Fijian, works of art” (20).

Cultural mobility can be facilitated through oral traditions—passing down folktales and legends through verbal storytelling. These stories help to teach, explain, and orient Tonga people to the world around them and to their “Tonga-ness” or their Tongan identity. This carving was sold with a printed card re-telling a legend about an octopus and a rat; a story that has been told in many different variations throughout the Pacific and is even printed and illustrated in children’s books today. In the version that came with this piece, a drowning rat receives a ride to land on a helpful octopus’s head, only to repay the sea creature by leaving dung on its head, sparking a feud that lasts to this very day. Folk tales such as these impart moral lessons, and relate to cultural traditions. For instance, Tongan people catch octopuses by creating a lure in the shape of a rat, called a ‘maka fake’—a process that works because, as explained in this story, the octopus is still looking for revenge on the rat. In another version retold by Eseta Aholelei in a “Sharing Stories from the Cultural Collections” video from the Australian Museum, it is explained that the dung from the rat created spots on the octopus’s head, which is why octopuses have spots today. These oral traditions mirror the close connections between the people of the Pacific and their natural environment.

Curatorial Notes: This storyboard originated in the Palau Islands. The scene depicted on the board takes place both on the water and land as is exemplary of the Palauan cultural attitude that life itself extends to the land and sea in equal measure. On the shore stands a bai, a traditional communal building, on which the Palauans recorded myths and legends. Sailing towards the shore, there is an open outrigger canoe, styled in the manner of a traditional war canoe.

This storyboard is one of two that was given to the museum by Dr. Solange Petit Skinner, in 2006. She donated them in memory of her husband Carlton Skinner, who had been the first civilian governor of Guam. They were acquired during his tenure as governor, at which time Dr. Solange Petit Skinner was also an anthropologist studying local cultures in the surrounding islands. The history of the storyboard in the islands begins in the 1930s with the Japanese occupation and the work of Hisakatsu Hijikata, who had come to the island to study art and culture. Through his work, the storyboards were adapted from the large interior beams in the bai- traditional high-gabled structures, used for ceremonies, as chief clubhouses and for men’s groups- to a smaller more portable form that could be sold to tourists. (Smith, 7) The histories and myths of Palauan communities were depicted on the interior and often the exterior gables of the bai. These buildings tend to form the center of traditional life for tribes, only four currently exist in their original architectural style. (Kaeppler, 146)

Since their beginning, the storyboards have undergone many changes in style. At first, they were painted as direct copies of the style and stories of the bai. Since Hijikata’s time, carvers have worked to incorporate the preferences of the market, alternately including and omitting facets like linear perspective, deeply carved images, different palettes and borders. The variations in storyboard reflect the tastes of the people coming to the island. After WWII, during the American administration of the island, carved storyboards became more popular than painted storyboards, signalling a shift in preference for American taste over Japanese. Today, it is possible to find boards that are painted and/or carved. Earl Wesley Jernigan has identified four styles of storyboard that continue to be used: melecheb itabori (deeply carved), omutk itabori (shallowly carved), chesbereber (painted), and ngdiak a chesbereber (unpainted) (Jernigan, 167). This storyboard is both painted and carved, and Dr. Solange Petit Skinner estimates that it was made during the middle of the twentieth century.

In the 1960s the Palauan government began to allow inmates in prisons to develop their skills at carving storyboards, creating a new kind of market for these works. This began with the work of Baris Sylvester, who was a former student of Hijikata. Today, the climate around the artistry of storyboards consists of a wide range of skill and artistic intention. Interestingly, because the bai were historically constructed as signs of wealth, placing a profit-driven value on storyboards for a burgeoning tourist art market does not serve to denounce the artistic or cultural significance of these works. The stories, iconography and visual language depicted on these boards continues to celebrate the Palauan cultural identity, and their production speaks to the relationship between Micronesian art, tourism and commodification.

Curatorial Notes: This navigational chart comes from the Marshall Islands, located North of New Zealand and to the West of the Philippines. Referred to colloquially as "stick charts," navigational charts are made from palm ribs, coconut fibers and cowries shells. Their form is meant to document the change in ocean swells and currents, typically induced by nearby islands or air (Akerblom 1968). Detecting such wave movements allowed sailors to navigate their ships towards land. There are three categories of Marshallese stick charts: Mattang, Meddo, and Rebbelib. (Spenneman 1998) As this navigational tool depicts the entire island chain, it is considered a Rebbelib chart. Alternatively, Mattang charts were used to teach new navigators concepts in wave formations, while Meddo charts were used to illustrate specific waves around one particular land mass (Akerblom 1968).

Historically constructed and used by Marshallese navigators, these charts were highly political objects, as the privileged few who controlled knowledge of navigation likely controlled resources and trade. (Davenport 1960) The value placed on these maps and navigation in the Marshall Islands led to separate familial schools of navigation, in which information was passed along father to son. The knowledge embedded in these charts was often considered to be quite sensitive, as each chart was individually crafted and was often difficult to interpret without the original maker (Davenport 1960). Generally, navigational charts functioned as physical pneumonic devices, and were memorized by navigators rather than taken aboard on voyages. An expert sailor could feel shifts in ocean currents during the course of his voyage, oftentimes by lying down in the center of a canoe blindfolded. (Spennemann 1998).

The use of stick charts as traditional sea-farring devices has widely fallen out of practice, especially as colonization and globalization have introduced new technology to the islands in the last few centuries. Because of the regard for navigation in Marshallese society, chiefs saw the inclusion of schooners, sextants, compasses, maps motorized boats and Western maritime practices as an opportunity to gain more political power over one another and more feasibly navigate changing seas. (Genz 2011)

This chart is one of six that was donated by Mrs. Solange P. Skinner, an anthropologist and the wife of the late Carlton Skinner--the first civilian governor of Guam. Dating to the early 1900s, this particular chart is a handicraft piece, and was most likely made for tourist trade. Diverging from traditional historic stick charts, it includes taped labels on the palm ribs that indicate each island’s name. Generally, handicraft charts include less coconut fiber string, and perhaps rely more heavily on glue to adhere pieces together. (AMNH Archives) Despite changes in sea-farring technology, continuing to construct these charts, even as collectibles and tourist souvenirs, allows Marshall islanders to revive historical navigation methodologies and to celebrate their environment and their ancestors as master navigators. Today, islanders take great pride in the history and making of these charts, buying and displaying them in their homes.

Curatorial Notes: This Toyota Net Bag, or Bilum was made by Louise Manusat, from Mendi in the Southern Highlands region of Papua New Guinea (PNG). PNG is in the southwest Pacific on the eastern part of the island New Guinea. When her mother, Cathy Randé sold this bilum (for 50 Kina, about 16 USD) Louise was a 17 year old high school student. Net bags, or bilums are tightly twisted looped bags made almost exclusively by women in PNG. Bilums across many regions of PNG have been interconnected to ritual events, significant life stages and cycles, spirit worlds, gender, tourism, and even high fashion. Many scholars agree that bilums play an important role in women’s exchange relationships and the commodity-based economy (West 2006). This role connects these bags with the history of PNG, global industries, national identity, and the natural environment.
Bilum is a Tok Pisin (or Melanesian Pidgin) word and is used to refer to net bags, as well as the marsupial pouch, the human placenta, and the afterbirth (Stewart 1997). The connection between bilum and the womb in Papua New Guinea occurs in many languages spoken on the island. When a woman has her first child, her mother may give her a bilum in which to carry the baby, thus a baby is carried in a woman’s physical womb and after birth is often carried in a bilum, or a net bag womb. Although bilums are worn by both men and women (in sometimes different fashions) the bilum has a specific connection to women in PNG, mostly because of the production traditions.
Traditional bilum making is a labor-intensive process. Plant fiber is collected from young tulip trees. The bark is stripped, dried, worked into string, and looped using various bilum techniques. Colors are made from local plants and kneaded into the string. Bilums are also woven from more accessible materials like acrylic yarn. As increased logging projects in PNG have threatened the growth of tulip trees, man-made fibers are now a more sustainable way to continue this tradition. Although making bilums from yarn is less laborious, it still requires a great skill to learn and master different patterns. Patterns are often associated with a particular region or group, and can circulate among skilled weavers and designers. More recent designs may have names that correlate to individual, local or global ideas, trends or styles.
Louise incorporated the Toyota logo and the motto of the local Toyota distributor, Ela Motors, in her work. In 1960 twelve Land Cruisers were the first Toyota products to arrive in PNG. This was only three years after Toyota first arrived in the United States (Toyota, is a Japanese automobile company that was founded in 1937). Louise’s choice to mix the globally recognized logo with her work may speak to her immediate surroundings, the popularity of the Toyota brand in PNG, as well as her engagement with global trends and technology. Some weavers from PNG have established international audiences. Bilumwear and designs are featured in national fashion shows and magazines, and similar looping techniques are used in clothing designs. Bilums Festivals also occur throughout the country.
Bilum fashion, and production is considered amongst some as a symbol of national identity (Garnier 2009). Garnier suggests that many people from PNG can identify themselves with this technique and expression, even if this practice was not part of their communities in the past.
