Curatorial Notes: During the course of her 1931-1932 fieldwork, Margaret Mead collected over six hundred artifacts and artworks from the Abelam tribe in the Sepik River region of Papua New Guinea. Of particular interest are objects related to the Abelam yam cult, which requires planting, harvesting, and decorating yams with feathers, ornaments, and painted basketry masks. Abelam men exhibit and exchange decorated yams between ceremonial partners within or between villages. A tense atmosphere of competition surrounds the exchanges, for a successful crop of the largest yams brings high prestige to both the grower and his clan.

Curatorial Notes: This is one of over six hundred Abelam artifacts collected by Margaret Mead during her 1931-1932 fieldwork in the Sepik River region of Papua New Guinea. This carving, representing an ancestor, would have been one of many used to decorate the interior of a ceremonial house. The face painting on this carving is similar to that worn by Abelam men and women in ritual contexts.
Exhibition History
• BODY ART: MARKS OF IDENTITY. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. November 1999 - May 2000
Exhibition Label: Abelam figure. Wood, vegetable fiber, paint. 20th century. Decorated figures found inside ceremonial houses represent the spirits of clan ancestors. The face painting on these images is similar to that worn by Abelam men and women in ritual contexts.
