Curatorial Notes: This burekalou (spirit house) model originates from the Republic of Fiji in the Melanesian region of the Pacific. Its structure emulates the full-size burekalou that were found in villages throughout Fiji until the 19th century, when ancestral spirit worship began to be displaced by Christianity. Typically each village had one or more burekalou, wherein a priest would reside and act as a medium through which village chiefs could connect with deities and the spirits of ancestors (Roth 406). During these times, ancestral spirits often occupied shrines, animals or inanimate objects. Not only were burekalou used as havens for consulting with ancestors but, they functioned as council-chambers, guesthouses, places of entertainment, and sleeping quarters for the head persons in the village (Williams and Calvert 175).

Measuring to 82 centimeters, this burekalou was created as a model of the treasured full-size spirit houses that can reach heights of up to twelve meters. Typically, the structures of burekalou and models like this one are comprised of wood, bamboo and sinnet, a material made up of braided coconut fibers. Wooden posts positioned at the corners of the structure formed the frame, and bamboo was fastened between the posts to shape the outer walls (Roth 406). Often, these structures were built within the vicinity of where a chief recently died or was killed (Williams and Calvert 174).

During construction, at the time when the posts were lowered into their holes and erected vertically, human sacrifices were sometimes offered to the temples deity. In some cases, men were placed standing in the post hole and buried alive by the side of the post. Feasts and celebrations were held when the first pair of rafters was put in place, and similarly when construction was completed. Such sacrifices and festivities speak to the significance of these structures in Fijian spiritual life.

According to missionary reports from the 19th century, and well as sources collected by the Polynesian Cultural Center, a strip of white masi or long bark tapa (cloth) hung from the ceiling to facilitate the arrival and department of deities into the burekalou. Carried out by priests, these moments of worship were often accompanied by offerings from the chief, such as food, hand-clubs, headwraps, necklaces, or sugar-cane. By petitioning the gods through priests, chiefs sought to improve conditions in their villages or serve their own personal interests (Williams and Calvert 175). Models like this were kept behind screens inside spirit houses and used by priests for worship, and to communicate with gods and ancestors (Roth 407). In some cases, tabua (sperm whales tooth) was carved into human figures, which were placed inside these models to facilitate spirit worship. In Fijian society, it was common for tabua to be used to make formal requests, to apologize, to give thanks, and to compensate, as community members might have intended in their interactions with deities (Seeman 219).

Because these models allowed priests to communicate with gods on behalf of their communities, it is not likely that Fijians would have traded or sold them to travelers or researchers when traditional ancestral worship was still active throughout the islands. Burekalou models more likely were collected at the onset of Christianity. After spending forty years on the island studying Fijian culture, John William Waters brought roughly 1800 artifacts to AMNH in the early 20th century, including this burekalou model.
