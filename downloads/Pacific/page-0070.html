SACRED CROCODILE FLUTE FIGURE
PACIFIC ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION

Material: WOOD, PLANT FIBER, SHELL, FEATHER, STRING, PIGMENT, BEAD, BARK?, FUR, BONE (CROCODILE SKULL), TOOTH (CROCODILE
Dimensions: FIGURE) L:61 W:27 H:23 OVERALL) L:204 W:27 H:23 (in CM)
Technique: CARVED, INLAY, PAINTING
Acquisition Year: 1933 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: MEAD, MARGARET, DR.
Keywords: FLUTE FIGURE

Curatorial Notes: Members of the Mundugumor tribe crafted this wooden flute for Margaret Mead during her 1932 fieldwork in the village of Kenakatem in the Yuat River region of northeastern Papua New Guinea. Flutes in this region are usually carved from hard, heavy brown wood and range in size from 8 to 38 inches. A figure depicting a human, animal, or bird is often carved at the top of the instrument (Fraser 1955). The attachment of decorative objects, such as shell, tusk, bone, beads, fur, hair, fiber, seeds and feathers, onto flutes and other objects is common for objects made throughout Papua New Guinea. This particular flute depicts an ancestral figure and is enhanced by natural materials such as feathers, fur, shells, hair and snake vertebrae (Mead 1938). According to Fraser and Mead, flutes like this are decorated with the same materials used in personal adornment, which represent elements that the flute’s possessor wishes to embody (Mead 1938, Fraser 1955).
Flutes have been a significant part of socioeconomic life in Mundugumor society. They have been used as the primary portion of a woman’s dowry to her husband’s family and are also used to pay the family of the bride (Fraser 1955). Once a flute is in a family, it is passed down through the generations and conveys prestige and fame to those who possess it. The flute is kept in the back of the house, only able to be seen by individuals who have undergone initiation and have earned the right to participate in certain ceremonies, including the birth of the flute and initiation rites for young people (Fraser 1955). The flute is honored and regarded as sacred in these ceremonies, which are less commonly celebrated now than in the past.
These flutes are only constructed by men in the Mundugamor tribe. When the flute is first created, it is referred to as a child of the crocodile spirit, which is a particularly powerful entity in Mundugumor traditions. The flute is given a ceremonial birth in which a water drum, representing the crocodile spirit, is beaten, and the flute is triumphantly displayed. After its birth, the flute is only seen at initiations. In the past, initiations depicted a symbolic birth ceremony, in which the youth of the tribe were “swallowed by the crocodile,” climbing into the mouth of a model of a crocodile and exiting out the tail (Fraser 1955).
Over time, this initiation ceremony has been transformed. Formerly used to introduce a young man to the community of adult males. It later became a ceremony focused on the prestige and fame of a “big man,” wealthy men with many wives, who sponsored and held initiations periodically (Mead 1935). The men who were to be initiated would have to demonstrate endurance, being subjected to cutting by crocodile teeth, burning, or beating in order to view the sacred flute (Mead 1935). Notably, women had their own initiation ceremony through which they could gain access to view the sacred flute. Women had to observe food taboos if they wished to be initiated, in contrast to the trials of physical scarification which men endured (Mead 1935).
By the time Margaret Mead arrived in the area, the initiation ceremony had transitioned from being an initiation into adulthood. It became an event promoting the exclusion of those who had not been initiated (Mead 1935). Although these ceremonies lost their role to initiate young people into the community, they still fostered awe for the sacred flutes, especially for a family’s own flutes. Mead wrote, “Each boy will henceforth honour the flute of his own line if it possesses one, or strive to obtain one if there is none in existence” (Mead 1935). The birth of the flute and the initiation ceremonies embodied the relationship of the human, crocodile and flute and presented the flute as an extension of the self (Fraser 1955). These initiation ceremonies are likely not widely practiced by contemporary Mundungamor groups (McDowell 2003).

Curatorial Notes: This cassowary bone worked into a bird’s head is one of over 700 objects collected from the Mountain Arapesh peoples by former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead during her 1931-33 expedition of New Guinea. Mead first encountered the Mountain Arapesh in the Torricelli Mountain range, along the Northern coast of Papua New Guinea in the East Sepik Province. Mead adapted the word “Arapesh,” which means “human” or “friend” in their language, to name the people she studied as well as the language itself (Roscoe 2004). Arapesh now refers to a family of languages spoken throughout East Sepik, as well as the diverse geographic and cultural groups that speak them.

This carved cassowary bone was likely meant to be a dagger, although its dulled tip suggests it may have been more decorative than functional. In the Sepik region, it was a common practice to carve cassowary tibiotarsi (leg bones), as well as, to some extent, human femurs, into intricately incised or engraved daggers. Cassowary-bone daggers do not generally have handles and are the length of the bone, usually between 33 to 40 centimeters. Since they are intended to be stabbing weapons, they do not have sharpened edges but instead a needle-pointed tip (Newton 1989:306).

As Mead suggests, the Mountain Arapesh were an importing culture and likely traded with neighboring groups for daggers such as these (Mead 1938). This particular cassowary-bone dagger most closely resembles those made by the Ilahita Arapesh. The Ilahita Arapesh manufactured cassowary-bone daggers without the elaborate incisions of other Sepik groups, but oftentimes carved the joint end of the bone into a parrot or cassowary head much like this one (Newton 1989:314).

The cassowary, the third largest flightless bird in the world and a species native to New Guinea, plays a key role in the cosmogony of many Arapesh-speaking peoples. A resource is included below that provides an overview of the role of the cassowary in the creation stories of the Arapesh-speaking peoples.

Cassowary daggers were most notably used in initiation ceremonies for young boys, as well as for the decoration of male spirit houses where these ceremonies were performed (Tuzin 1980:238). Among the Ilahita Arapesh, for instance, “fancy cassowary daggers, their hilts carved as parrots or hornbills, [were] given fresh paint and bedecked with leaves and fathers” in preparation for ritual dramas (Tuzin 230). Among the Mountain Arapesh, the cassowary served as the primary initiatory being for men’s cults, in which men imitating cassowaries would “devour” pre-adult boys to initiate them into men’s cult. In this ritual called “the swallowing by the cassowary,” a man “wearing a ferocious pair of cassowary-feather eye-pieces, and having suspended from his neck a shell-covered bag in which are stuck two sharpened cassowary bones” would perform penile incisions on the boy with daggers (Newton 1989 310).
