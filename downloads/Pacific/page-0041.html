Curatorial Notes: Feather boxes like the one shown above hold the highly valued items of a Maori chief and are known as papahuia when they are shaped flat and rectangular, and called wakahuia when boxes take on a canoe-form. As New Zealand's first inhabitants, the Maori arrived in double-hulled canoes from their ancestral home of Hawaiki, thought to be located among the Cook Islands or the Society Islands. Known in Maori as Aotearoa, New Zealand is the westernmost point of the Polynesian triangle reaching to Hawai’i and Easter Island, and located some 1,400 miles from Australia. The Maori people trace their lineage through tribes (iwi) that are descended from the ancestors of these maiden voyages (waka). Chiefs (rangatira) hold political authority that are empowered by knowledge of their genealogy (whakapapa) as this was integral to leadership and aristocracy. The land is significant to the people for it represented the collective power (mana) of a tribal group (iwi) and held great importance as a source of food, home and livelihood.

The items stored within these boxes are regarded sacred because they were worn on the owner's head, which was believed to be the body's most sacred part. The sanctity of the objects such as white heron feathers, jade and combs, made them dangerous to anyone not considered royalty thus feather boxes protect these items when not in use. Indeed, Maori decorative artwork never "interfered" with an object's purpose; its goal was to enhance the box's design and the carvings made outside of a box were most likely created by a chief. In fact, feather boxes were deemed the highest caliber of carving: “No carved objects in the field of Maori art surpass them for originality, vigor, or as a demonstration of the local style” (312). Thus the bases of boxes were elaborately decorated as well because they were suspended from the rafters of homes, allowing them to be admired from below.

Curatorial Notes: This Hawaiian necklace is known as le niho palaoa and has a carved sperm whale’s tooth amid strands of plaited human hair that can number from 200 to 1,000 strands. These necklaces were worn by Hawaiian chiefs who were members of the royalty (ali’i). Hawai’i was settled between 800-1,000 AD and is located at the northernmost point of the Polynesian triangle that stretches to New Zealand and Easter Island. Historically, Hawai’i was populated by ancestors of Polynesian descent who established the foundation for a highly stratified and complex society in which distinctions were made between the lower classes (nakanaka) and those of royal and chiefly rank (na li'i). Thus, the common people were expected to provide regular offerings from their crops to the na li’i or ali’i for the privilege to work land that was owned by the ali’i. The highest-ranking chiefs became known as sacred chiefs (ali’i kapu) and their power was on par with the gods. Indeed, it was the ali’i kapu who commissioned many of the art objects such as feathered capes and headdresses to serve as physical manifestations of their power. Thus, the ivory within these necklaces is an example of the scarce and valuable material, which include boar tusks and red feathers, that was reserved for use by the chiefs. The human hair stores the power (mana) of an individual and thereby the necklace displays the power of its wearer. "Hair comes from the head, the most sacred part of the body, hair is the embodiment of personal mana so by wearing another’s, an individual affirms and increases their own mana” (Neich & Pereira:160).

Curatorial Notes: The navigational skills of Micronesians were unsurpassed, for in two million square miles of Pacific Ocean, the total land area of the islands was less than 1,500 square miles. Navigators taught apprentices how to get from one island to another, using charts made of sticks and shells which represented currents and islands. This navigation chart belonged to Robert Louis Stevenson, the author of Treasure Island (1883), Kidnapped (1886), and The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1886). Stevenson and his family moved to Samoa in 1889, where he was called "Tusitala" (teller of tales), and where he convalesced until his death in 1894. The AMNH purchased Stevenson's navigation chart from his estate for $80 in 1914.
