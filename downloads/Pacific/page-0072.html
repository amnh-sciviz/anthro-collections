Curatorial Notes: This dagger is one of over 250 objects collected from the Murik peoples by the young American anthropologist Louis Pierre Ledoux during his five months of fieldwork in Kaup, Papua New Guinea in 1935. Ledoux was sent to Kaup under the direction of Former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead. The village of Kaup is located 40 miles west of the Sepik River in the estuary on the northern coast of the East Sepik Province. The term Murik refers to the people living in Kaup and several other nearby villages, as well as the language they speak. Ledoux ultimately joined his family’s chemical business instead of pursuing a doctorate in anthropology. His two manuscripts and field notes from this work remain unpublished in the Museum’s archives.

Mead distinguishes the Sepik culture area by several “traits” including men’s houses (tok pisin: haus tambera) ceremonial masks (tumbuan) and diverse supernatural objects (tamberan/tambaran) placed in the houses and used for initiation rites, most often taking the form of bamboo flutes (Lutkehaus 1990). Tamberan societies reinforce authority and social ties such as solidarity and loyalty (Lewis 1990), provide common ground, and are a unifying intercultural force in the region (Schindlbeck 1990). The process “strengthens” men, allowing them “to be motivated to the tasks appropriate to their gender and to possess the talents necessary for success in these tasks” (Roscoe 1990). Sepik River initiations have reacted in a diversity of ways to the intrusion of European powers, with the Murik now only occasionally performing a flute ceremony for firstborn sons (Roscoe & Scaglion 1990)

According to Ledoux’s field notes, a flying fox wing bone such as this would have been used in Murik tamberan initiation ceremonies. These intense and elaborate trials impart social distinction, strengthen bonds amongst peers, and encode collective memory, similar to “hazing” techniques among Western adolescents (Young 1985). Initiates are introduced to the powerful ngwaalndu, or ancestral clan spirits, embodied in the carvings and paintings lining the tamberan house. Only adult men can enter tamberan houses that store the secret paraphernalia of the cult (Mead 1938). The practices of tamberan societies differ among groups throughout the Sepik. The most prevalent external framework for understanding these rituals is that they formalize and publicize changes in social identity, often employing a metaphor of being eaten and reborn (Roscoe).

Ledoux wrote that “There is some contradiction here as to whether the bones are human femur, or cassowary. The human leg bone daggers are only used in boys’ initiatory ceremony. These bones are found on the beach as the cemetery is worn away. They do not know whose bones are used and they are not saved from burial” (58)

Curatorial Notes: This flying fox wing bone is one of over 250 objects collected from the Murik peoples by the American anthropologist Louis Pierre Ledoux during his five months of fieldwork in Kaup, Papua New Guinea in 1935. Ledoux was sent to Kaup under the direction of Former Pacific Ethnology Curator Margaret Mead. The village of Kaup is located 40 miles west of the Sepik River in the estuary on the northern coast of the East Sepik Province. The term Murik refers to the people living in Kaup and several other nearby villages, as well as the language they speak. Ledoux’s two manuscripts and field notes from this work remain unpublished in the Museum’s archives.

Throughout the Sepik region, it was common practice to hold elaborate ceremonies for young boys to be initiated into adult male societies called tamberan. Young boys are initiated into the tamberan through increasingly intense stages of hazing and the introduction to the powerful ngwaalndu, or ancestral clan spirits, embodied in the carvings and paintings lining the tamberan house. Only adult men can enter tamberan houses that store the secret paraphernalia of the society (Mead 1938). The practices of tamberan societies differ among groups throughout the Sepik.

According to Ledoux’s field notes, a flying fox wing bone such as this would have been used in Murik tamberan initiation ceremonies. These intense and elaborate trials impart social distinction, strengthen bonds amongst peers, and encode collective memory, similar to “hazing” techniques among Western adolescents (Young 1985). As Ledoux wrote: “The big men ornament themselves and each takes a pair of bones, taken from flying fox wings. The children lie down and their ribs are prodded with the bones” (Ledoux 51e). He explained in his manuscript that this “...represents the Tamberan eating them" (Ledoux 511). The Tamberan is the supernatural guardian of the men’s "cult," which is impersonated through sounds, costumes, and hazing practices throughout the initiatory stages (Mead). The most prevalent external framework for understanding these rituals is that they formalize and publicize changes in social identity, hence the metaphor of being eaten and reborn (Roscoe 1990).

The flying fox holds emblematic significance for Murik social relationships. In the late 20th century, political power was dispersed over small kinship groups, where leaders would inherit offices that had to be be constantly renewed and reinforced by elder sons and heirs, by means of feasts and initiation rituals. These involving the exchange of boar tusk and shell ornaments. Older sons were known as suman gwan “Boars’-tusk sons,” while younger sons called nabag gwan “flying fox sons.” Illegitimate children were simply known as “flying foxes” (Lipset 1990).
