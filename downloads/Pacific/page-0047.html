Curatorial Notes: Piupiu, which translates as 'to swing', is a type of Māori grass skirt (Tamarapa 2013). Rāpaki or pākē karure are more traditional types of skirts, but the piupiu developed and became prominent in the colonial era (Te Kanawa 2014). The piupiu is a combination of those traditional forms of clothing and more contemporary styles (Smith 2014).

The piupiu itself is made out of the fibers of flax leaves and a woven taniko, which is a type of waistband (Taonui 2007). The use of flax for textile production is very identifiably a Māori style and tradition. Flax was initially used for weaving baskets, containers and mats similar to how the pandanus plant is used in other Polynesian cultures. However, the Māori learned to obtain the muka, or strong fibers, of the plant by scraping off the green flesh. Muka became a cornerstone of Māori textiles; made by working the string fibers until they were flexible enough to be used in rope, fishing nets, and clothing (Swarbrick 2007).

The piupiu is strongly associated with kapa haka; it is a dance performed in standing lines that holds a great deal of social significance with respect to the iwi’s (tribe’s) reputation and social standing. The piupiu was also free-hanging rather than woven (Te Kanawa). This causes the skirt to sway during the dance, hence the name. The flax material of the garment also creates a distinctive noise as the strands rub against one another. The chief Tinirau organized the first kapa haka; directing a group of women to dance for one of his adversaries (Smith).

Curatorial Notes: This Savai’i water jug is one of five round water vessels in the Pacific Ethnographic Collection. These vessels were collected by David Flaherty and donated by two founders of Paramount Pictures, Adolph Zukor and Jesse L. Lasky, Jr. in 1926. In Moana, Flaherty’s film about daily life on the Samoan island of Savai’i, coconut water vestles like this one can be seen used for a variety of purposes. A vessel like this one can be seen used to hold oil that is spread on Moana before he does the Siva dance in preparation for his coming of age ceremony. A coconut water vessel is also used to pour and mix water with root dust to make Kava, a drink used in Moana’s coming of age ceremony. Throughout the film, coconuts can also be seen used as bowls, cups, fire starters, and food in the form of coconut custard (Flaherty 1926).

In Samoa, coconuts are one of six main crops (Watters 1958). One reason for their wide use is because of their abundance with it being rare to be further than 30 minutes away from a coconut tree. Some variety of coconut trees live up to 70 years baring 10-60 nuts a year, year round; thus, they are an abundant, replenishing resource. With this abundance, coconuts are essential not only because they are a staple in Samoan food, but also because of their many practical uses. The strong wood of a coconut tree is used to build houses, rollers for canoes, headrests, and firewood. The leaves are made into baskets, blinds, trays, fans, hats, mats, shoes, toys and roofs. The fibers on the outer layer of the coconut are used to make Sennit, a cord used in houses and boats. The coconut shell can be used to make utensils, bottles, dishes, game pieces, and fish hooks. Oil for the coconut kernel can be used for many cosmetic purposes including washing hair, oiling skin, and making perfume. The oil is also used on cuts and wounds as well as on the chest to help with coughs (Holmes 1974). In order to keep up with the demand for coconuts, Samoans planted coconut groves that tended to be around an acer in size to maintain a steady supply of coconuts (Watters 1958).

Flaherty, the director of Moana, was a well known ethnographic filmmaker who directed key early examples of ethnographic films intended for scientific documentation and mass entertainment. His first completed film, Nanook of the North (1922), portrays an idealized version daily life of a Quebec Inuit family and is often considered to be the first documentary, although the degree and ethics of its liberal fictionalization has been a subject of ongoing debate within indigenous communities and in anthropology/media studies.

After the success of this film, Flaherty directed his second documentary style film Moana (1923-1926). Moana was filmed during the British colonial-era in the village of Safune on the island of Savai’i in Independent Samoa. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. While the film has good depictions of gardening, hunting, fishing, and other daily activities, some of the rituals were staged in order to create a more dramatic film. One of these scenes depicts a young man getting tattooed as a right of manhood, despite the fact that these painful tattoos had been suppressed during the colonial era (Weinberger 55). Like Nanook of the North, Moana is seen as a key moment in the development of visual anthropology but there have also been ongoing critiques of the ethics of fictionalizing elements of Samoan life depicted in the film.

Curatorial Notes: These headrests, called ‘ali or kali, are not exclusively found in Samoa, but are also associated with the Tongan peoples. The headrests were used to keep the head and hair off of the ground as well as to keep hair from flattening by resting the base of the skull on the object when lying down or sleeping (Dyne 1999). Across many Polynesian cultures, the head and hair are considered some of the most important parts of the body. This is where the mana, a kind of spiritual energy, is thought to reside (RAMM). This also meant the headrests should be elegant and well designed to respect and support the mana (St. Cartmail, 53). This spiritual function of the headrests lead people to believe that the mana of an individual became associated with their headrest. Because of this, it was considered highly inappropriate to touch another’s headrest without permission.

There are several types of kali (head rests). The kali hahapo has wide legs; the kali toloni has bowed legs; and kali loa which are lengths of bamboo long enough to accommodate multiple people (Dyne 1999). Very similar in design to the kali toloni, with the same bowed legs but made with multiple pieces of wood, is the kali lalonia, a type used primarily by people in positions of social influence (Dyne 1999). Kali toloni are more strongly associated with women, whereas kali hahapo are associated with men. In some traditions, when a woman is married, her family makes her an ornate kali, usually a kali toloni (Dyne 1999).

This headrest is one of two collected by David Flaherty and donated by two founders of Paramount Pictures, Adolph Zukor and Jesse L. Lasky, Jr. in 1926. Flaherty was a well known ethnographic filmmaker who directed key early examples of ethnographic films intended for scientific documentation and mass entertainment. His first completed film, Nanook of the North (1922), portrays an idealized version daily life of a Quebec Inuit family and is often considered to be the first documentary, although the degree and ethics of its liberal fictionalization has been a subject of ongoing debate within indigenous communities and in anthropology/media studies.

After the success of this film, Flaherty directed his second documentary style film Moana (1923-1926). Moana was filmed during the British colonial-era in the village of Safune on the island of Savai’i in Independent Samoa. Formerly known as Western Samoa, the islands have since been renamed after gaining their independence from New Zealand in 1963. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. While the film has good depictions of daily activities, some of the rituals were staged in order to create a more dramatic film. One of these scenes depicts Moana getting tattooed as a right of manhood, despite the fact that these tattoos had been suppressed during the colonial era (Weinberger 55). Like Nanook of the North, Moana is seen as a key moment in the development of visual anthropology but there have also been ongoing critiques of the ethics of fictionalizing elements of Samoan life depicted in the film. During the course of filming Moana, Flaherty ended up keeping many of the objects used in his film which were later donated to AMNH.

This item, known as a salu or ‘broom,’ has stiff fibers at the end that come from the dried midribs of green coconut leaflets. These fibers are then bound together at the butt end, with the option of being tied around a longer length of wood to form a handle (Hīroa 81). In Samoa there are two main designs for the broom. First, the hand broom, or salu lima, is used inside the house. The other kind salu tu, broom with the wooden handle, serves as a yard broom (Neich 17). More than just an object used around the house, the broom is also a tool for managing social status. The broom serves as an important indicator of domestic prosperity, as cleanliness of the home is historically important for the Samoan household. The responsibility of sweeping of both one’s home and yard, usually take up by the women of a household, was a task in which one took pride (Neich 17).

In Moana, Flaherty’s film about daily life on the Samoan island of Savai’i, coconut water vestles like this one can be seen used for a variety of purposes. A vessel like this one can be seen used to hold oil that is spread on Moana before he does the Siva dance in preparation for his coming of age ceremony. A coconut water vessel is also used to pour and mix water with root dust to make Kava, a drink used in Moana’s coming of age ceremony. Throughout the film, coconuts can also be seen used as bowls, cups, fire starters, and food in the form of coconut custard (Flaherty 1926).

In Samoa, coconuts are one of six main crops (Watters 1958). One reason for their wide use is because of their abundance with it being rare to be further than 30 minutes away from a coconut tree. Some variety of coconut trees live up to 70 years baring 10-60 nuts a year, year round; thus, they are an abundant, replenishing resource. With this abundance, coconuts are essential not only because they are a staple in Samoan food, but also because of their many practical uses. The strong wood of a coconut tree is used to build houses, rollers for canoes, headrests, and firewood. The leaves are made into baskets, blinds, trays, fans, hats, mats, shoes, toys and roofs. The fibers on the outer layer of the coconut are used to make Sennit, a cord used in houses and boats. The coconut shell can be used to make utensils, bottles, dishes, game pieces, and fish hooks. Oil for the coconut kernel can be used for many cosmetic purposes including washing hair, oiling skin, and making perfume. The oil is also used on cuts and wounds as well as on the chest to help with coughs (Holmes 1974). In order to keep up with the demand for coconuts, Samoans planted coconut groves that tended to be around an acer in size to maintain a steady supply of coconuts (Watters 1958).

Some variety of coconut trees live up to 70 years baring 10-60 nuts a year, year round; thus, they are an abundant, replenishing resource. With this abundance, coconuts are essential not only because they are a staple in Samoan food, but also because of their many practical uses. The strong wood of a coconut tree is used to build houses, rollers for canoes, headrests, and firewood. The leaves are made into baskets, blinds, trays, fans, hats, mats, shoes, toys and roofs. The fibers on the outer layer of the coconut are used to make Sennit, a cord used in houses and boats. The coconut shell can be used to make utensils, bottles, dishes, game pieces, and fish hooks. Oil for the coconut kernel can be used for many cosmetic purposes including washing hair, oiling skin, and making perfume. The oil is also used on cuts and wounds as well as on the chest to help with coughs (Holmes 1974). In order to keep up with the demand for coconuts, Samoans planted coconut groves that tended to be around an acer in size to maintain a steady supply of coconuts (Watters 1958).

Curatorial Notes: This shark line and float was donated by Adolph Zukor and J.J Lasky, co-founders of Paramount Pictures, in 1926. It was originally collected by David Flaherty, brother to the explorer turned filmmaker Robert Flaherty who first made his name with the release of Nanook of the North in 1922 (Zimmerman 2013). Impressed with his first film, Paramount commissioned Flaherty to travel to Samoa in 1923 and make the film Moana (1926) (Rotha 1983). David Flaherty accompanied his brother to Savai’i, an island in Samoa, and collected items like this one to take back with him.

Flaherty is said to have created the first docufiction with this film, not quite a documentary since scenes of the fictional Samoan chief's son, Moana, were manipulated for filming (Zimmerman 2013). Nevertheless, Flaherty’s works continue to be influential in both anthropology and media studies (Rotha 1983).

Shark fishing, or lepaga in Samoan (Levine & Sauafea-Le’au 2013) was an old pastime and honored tradition. Only performed during two seasons each year, April to May and then again from November to December, shark fishing is excitedly anticipated for both the enjoyment of the activity and the meat it produced (Tuioti 1955). Sharks species most commonly encountered in Independent and American Samoa are blacktip and whitetip reef sharks (Craig). Recently, shark fishing has been banned in American Samoa to limit shark fin harvesting that has been depleting local populations (Levine & Sauafea-Le’au 2013).

Shark fishing is done in shallow sea areas, such as aua or reefs. One of the many tools used is the tui-ipo, or the shark rattle shown above. The rattles are made with 6-10 coconut half-shells strung through a stick. If there are only a small number of sharks at the fishing location, the fishermen will shake the rattle in the water. The rattle attracts sharks to its location by creating a sound akin to “the splashing and the darting about of a school of big fish preying on a shoal of tiny fish” (Tuioti 1955). This harvesting process is called sele (Levine & Sauafea-Le’au 2013).

Curatorial Notes: This shark line and float was donated by Adolph Zukor and J.J Lasky, co-founders of Paramount Pictures, in 1926. It was originally collected by David Flaherty, brother to the explorer turned filmmaker Robert Flaherty who first made his name with the release of Nanook of the North in 1922 (Zimmerman 2013). Impressed with his first film, Paramount commissioned Flaherty to travel to Samoa in 1923 and make the film Moana (1926) (Rotha 1983). David Flaherty accompanied his brother to Savai’i, an island in Samoa, and collected items like this one to take back with him.

Flaherty is said to have created the first docufiction with this film, not quite a documentary since scenes of the fictional Samoan chief's son, Moana, were manipulated for filming (Zimmerman 2013). Nevertheless, Flaherty’s works continue to be influential in both anthropology and media studies (Rotha 1983).

Shark fishing, or lepaga in Samoan (Levine & Sauafea-Le’au 2013), is an old and celebrated tradition. Only performed during two seasons each year, April to May and then again from November to December, shark-fishing is excitedly anticipated for both the enjoyment of the activity and the meat it produces (Tuioti 1955). Sharks species most commonly encountered in Independent and American Samoa are blacktip and whitetip reef sharks (Craig). Recently, shark fishing has been banned in American Samoa to limit shark fin harvesting that has been depleting local populations (Levine & Sauafea-Le’au 2013).

Shark fishing is done in shallow sea areas, such as aua or reefs. One of the tools used is the Fau-ma-le-uto (Tuioti 1955), or the string and float shown above, which is needed during night fishing when visibility is low (Tuioti 1955). In order to bring the sharks closer to the surface, the float is covered in bait and thrown into the water. The string attached to the float is held by a fisherman onboard and while the sharks try to eat the bait, the holder of the string slowly pulls the float towards the boat. The lead fisherman waits holding a submerged rope-lasso, and when the shark approaches the lasso is wrapped around its neck (Tuioti 1955). This harvesting process is called sele (Levine & Sauafea-Le’au 2013).

Curatorial Notes: This trumpet is one of three collected by David Flaherty and donated by two founders of Paramount Pictures, Adolph Zukor and Jesse L. Lasky, Jr. in 1926. Flaherty was a well known ethnographic filmmaker who directed key early examples of ethnographic films intended for scientific documentation and mass entertainment. His first completed film, Nanook of the North (1922), portrays an idealized version daily life of a Quebec Inuit family and is often considered to be the first documentary, although the degree and ethics of its liberal fictionalization has been a subject of ongoing debate within indigenous communities and in anthropology/media studies.

After the success of this film, Flaherty directed his second documentary style film Moana (1923-1926). Moana was filmed during the British colonial-era in the village of Safune on the island of Savai’i in Independent Samoa. Formerly known as Western Samoa, the islands have since been renamed after gaining their independence from New Zealand in 1963. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. While the film has good depictions of gardening, hunting, fishing, and other daily activities, some of the rituals were staged in order to create a more dramatic film. One of these scenes depicts a young man getting tattooed as a right of manhood, despite the fact that these painful tattoos had been suppressed during the colonial era (Weinberger 55). Like Nanook of the North, Moana is seen as a key moment in the development of visual anthropology but there have also been ongoing critiques of the ethics of fictionalizing elements of Samoan life depicted in the film. During the course of filming Moana, Flaherty ended up keeping many of the objects used in his film which were later donated to AMNH.

These trumpets are made from conch shells, usually Charonia tritonis or Cassis cornuta. In Samoa, these trumpets are used for signaling rather than as a musical instrument (Moyle). Some examples of their use are “evening curfews, village and church meetings, evening prayers, and the several stages of fishing expeditions” (McLean). They are also blown for shows, war signals (Williamson), calling children to school, and to announce the appearance of the Pleiades, a star formation that is one of many astrological and environmental signs the Samoans would look to (McLean; Lefale). In Flaherty’s Moana, a villager is seen blowing a conch trumpet to “gather the village to their ceremonial dance,” to celebrate Moana’s coming of age (Flaherty).

Important chiefs would have a conch trumpet blown to signify their arrival, while priests would blow it before a war (Williamson). In the case of war, depending on weather the trumpets sound was clear or rough was thought to predict the outcome (McLean). The Samoan chief system is called the Fa’amatai, with the head of each village being the matai. The matai can be either male or female and is the head of the aiga, extended family. Each village follows a similar structure and has a matai who forms a larger community amongst villages (Va’a).

Samoa is an island state comprising the westernmost group of the Samoan Islands, in Polynesia. The islands include Upolu, home to most of Samoa's population, and Savai'i, one of the largest islands in the South Pacific. Archeological evidence shows that Samoa has been inhabited for over 2,000 years by voyagers from Southeast Asia. Many islands throughout the Pacific use leaves from Pandanus plants (screwpine) in order to make many different types of mats with various purposes and cultural significances. In Samoa, the Pandanus plants are used to make ceremonial fine mats as well as everyday floor and bed mats (Lal and Fortune).

Mats are made with three different types of pandanus leaves: laupaongo, laufala, and lau’ie. The leaves are cut into strips, dried, rolled out, and plaited in mats. The mats are traditionally made by women. Coarse floor mats with very wide wefts were constructed with the paongo leaf, the longest and widest of the pandus leaves. Floor mats with a narrower weft, sleeping mats, and small mats made for babies are made from the smaller fala leaf. Finally, 'ie tonga fine mats used as garments as well as, in occasion, some finer sleeping mats were created from the ‘ie leaf which has about the same size as the fala leaf (Hiroa).

Samoans also constructed mats from coconut leaves. The roughest type of floor mat was made from coconut leaves, and were given the name polavai. The polavai are still in common use as the undermost layer in the carpeting of a house with floor mats. They are easily and quickly made and, by taking the direct friction against the gravel of the floor, they save the upper mats made of pandanus leaves.

In Samoa, the fine mats are disbursed on major ceremonial occasions as a representation of traditional rank and wealth. One such ceremonial occasion was the public ceremonial defloration of a Samoan taupou (ceremonial virgin). The taupou system in Samoa dictated that the taupous occupied positions of great social importance and virginity at marriage was highly prized. This chastity was honored in the form of ceremonial deflorations in which the chief of the taupou would bestow the bridegroom with nearly a hundred fine mats. Furthermore, during the actual ceremony, the taupou was taken to her bridegroom, with a fine mat edged with the red feathers of a parakeet encircling her waist; the red feathers symbolizing hymenal blood. Through this practice, the fine mats were seen as a cultural symbol of the traditional taupou (Mead).

Many pandanus mats were shown in Moana, a fictionalized documentary directed by David Flaherty. Moana was filmed during the British colonial-era in the village of Safune on the island of Savai’i. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. The pandanus mats were notably shown when villagers laid them down before Moana’s ceremonial tattooing (Flaherty).

Samoa is an island state comprising the westernmost group of the Samoan Islands, in Polynesia. The islands include Upolu, home to most of Samoa's population, and Savai'i, one of the largest islands in the South Pacific. Archeological evidence shows that Samoa has been inhabited for over 2,000 years by voyagers from Southeast Asia. Many islands throughout the Pacific use leaves from Pandanus plants (screwpine) in order to make many different types of mats with various purposes and cultural significances. In Samoa, the Pandanus plants are used to make ceremonial fine mats as well as everyday floor and bed mats (Lal and Fortune).

Mats are made with three different types of pandanus leaves: laupaongo, laufala, and lau’ie. The leaves are cut into strips, dried, rolled out, and plaited in mats. The mats are traditionally made by women. Coarse floor mats with very wide wefts were constructed with the paongo leaf, the longest and widest of the pandus leaves. Floor mats with a narrower weft, sleeping mats, and small mats made for babies are made from the smaller fala leaf. Finally, 'ie tonga fine mats used as garments as well as, in occasion, some finer sleeping mats were created from the ‘ie leaf which has about the same size as the fala leaf (Hiroa).

Samoans also constructed mats from coconut leaves. The roughest type of floor mat was made from coconut leaves, and were given the name polavai. The polavai are still in common use as the undermost layer in the carpeting of a house with floor mats. They are easily and quickly made and, by taking the direct friction against the gravel of the floor, they save the upper mats made of pandanus leaves.

In Samoa, the fine mats are disbursed on major ceremonial occasions as a representation of traditional rank and wealth. One such ceremonial occasion was the public ceremonial defloration of a Samoan taupou (ceremonial virgin). The taupou system in Samoa dictated that the taupous occupied positions of great social importance and virginity at marriage was highly prized. This chastity was honored in the form of ceremonial deflorations in which the chief of the taupou would bestow the bridegroom with nearly a hundred fine mats. Furthermore, during the actual ceremony, the taupou was taken to her bridegroom, with a fine mat edged with the red feathers of a parakeet encircling her waist; the red feathers symbolizing hymenal blood. Through this practice, the fine mats were seen as a cultural symbol of the traditional taupou (Mead).

Many pandanus mats were shown in Moana, a fictionalized documentary directed by David Flaherty. Moana was filmed during the British colonial-era in the village of Safune on the island of Savai’i. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. The pandanus mats were notably shown when villagers laid them down before Moana’s ceremonial tattooing (Flaherty).

Curatorial Notes: This is one of seven stone adzes collected by David Flaherty and donated by two founders of Paramount Pictures, Adolph Zukor and Jesse L. Lasky, Jr. in 1926. Flaherty was a well known ethnographic filmmaker who directed key early examples of ethnographic films intended for scientific documentation and mass entertainment. His first completed film, Nanook of the North (1922), portrays an idealized version daily life of a Quebec Inuit family and is often considered to be the first documentary, although the degree and ethics of its liberal fictionalization has been a subject of ongoing debate within indigenous communities and in anthropology/media studies.

After the success of this film, Flaherty directed his second documentary style film Moana (1923-1926). Moana was filmed during the British colonial-era in the village of Safune on the island of Savai’i in Independent Samoa. Formerly known as Western Samoa, the islands have since been renamed after gaining their independence from New Zealand in 1963. The film follows Moana, the chief's son, and his family through daily life in Samoa. While the film has good depictions of gardening, hunting, fishing, and other daily activities, some of the rituals were staged in order to create a more dramatic film. One of these scenes depicts a young man getting tattooed as a right of manhood, despite the fact that these painful tattoos had been suppressed during the colonial era (Weinberger 55). Like Nanook of the North, Moana is seen as a key moment in the development of visual anthropology but there have also been ongoing critiques of the ethics of fictionalizing elements of Samoan life depicted in the film. During the course of filming Moana, Flaherty ended up keeping many of the objects used in his film which were later donated to AMNH.


For Samoans, the stones used for adzes such as this one were typically basalt. Known quarries that contain this stone existed in the mountains of Tutuila and have been connected to trade with other archipelagos of the area. As for the base, these fibrous cords (sennit in the Samoan language) were taken from coconuts and attached to pieces of timber (Mallon 32). Variations of the shape have been known to exist depending on the type of wood and angle at which the wood needed to be worked (Krämer 242). In addition to basalt, shells were known to have been used as the clever (Turner 158).

Regardless of angle or material, these tools were invaluable for the Samoans as sharpening objects that allowed for the creation of bowls, canoes, weapons, or, in the case of this adze, cutting down trees (Mallon 32).
