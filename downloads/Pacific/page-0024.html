Curatorial Notes: This Hawaiian necklace is known as le niho palaoa and has a carved sperm whales tooth amid strands of plaited human hair that can number from 200 to 1,000 strands. These necklaces were worn by Hawaiian chiefs who were members of the royalty (alii). Hawaii was settled between 800-1,000 AD and is located at the northernmost point of the Polynesian triangle that stretches to New Zealand and Easter Island. Historically, Hawaii was populated by ancestors of Polynesian descent who established the foundation for a highly stratified and complex society in which distinctions were made between the lower classes (nakanaka) and those of royal and chiefly rank (na li'i). Thus, the common people were expected to provide regular offerings from their crops to the na lii or alii for the privilege to work land that was owned by the alii. The highest-ranking chiefs became known as sacred chiefs (alii kapu) and their power was on par with the gods. Indeed, it was the alii kapu who commissioned many of the art objects such as feathered capes and headdresses to serve as physical manifestations of their power. Thus, the ivory within these necklaces is an example of the scarce and valuable material, which include boar tusks and red feathers, that was reserved for use by the chiefs. The human hair stores the power (mana) of an individual and thereby the necklace displays the power of its wearer. "Hair comes from the head, the most sacred part of the body, hair is the embodiment of personal mana so by wearing anothers, an individual affirms and increases their own mana (Neich & Pereira:160).

Curatorial Notes: Kete (woven baskets), are a part of a longstanding tradition within Maori culture, a group indigenous to Aotearoa, New Zealand. This kete is an example of the resilience and adaptability of Maori material culture in its use of materials from the natural world. Its design reveals how Maori contemporary practice has been inspired by traditional craft, as it was made in the late 20th century but showcases a customary checker-work weaving technique known as taki tahiover over, one under (Evans and Nagarimu 2005). Moreover, its handles allow the kete to be used as a fashionable purse rather than a functional basket, while traditionally, kete are made with larger straps so that they may be carried on the shoulders or back.
Like much of Maori material culture, kete are usable, everyday items that are also saturated with sacred cultural knowledge. This is due to the ketes pivotal place within Maori cosmology. For example, one story depicts a god traveling to the heavens to retrieve three of these woven baskets filled with secret knowledge to give to mankind (Evans and Ngarimu 2005). Thus kete are associated with being vessels of both knowledge and wisdom, and the art of weaving is considered a sacred practice. The weavers themselves act as a medium for the gods and a means through which cultural knowledge can be passed on (Mac Aulay and Kura 1996). As weaving is a craft practiced predominantly by women, this transmission of knowledge is matriarchal. In this way, kete have become symbols for the role of women in Maori culture and a focal point in the renaissance of Maori arts in the late 20th century. For this reason, woven items, such as kete, are highly prized and have traditionally been given as gifts. Today, they have made their way into the global marketplace, prompted by influx of tourists in the Pacific and their growing interest in purchasing Maori material culture.
This kete was gifted with another similar woven bag to the American Museum of Natural Historys Anthropology Department staff by Dalvanius Prime, a producer/songwriter of Maori music, in 1998, as part of a campaign to repatriate the preserved, tattooed Maori heads (toi moko) which the museum purchased from Horatio Gorden Robley in 1907. After a formal request was received in 2013, the AMNH returned the Maori heads in 2014 (The Echo 2015). As this kete was gifted to the AMNH during the negotiation for reparation of Maori cultural heritage, it serves as a symbol of both the precarious history of collecting as well as the resilience and changing nature of contemporary Maori culture today.
