Curatorial Notes: This ink pot was made by a member of the Hausa, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. The model allowed Muslim clergy to pursue other trades as well. Groups like the Hausa and Mande, who rapidly adopted Islam, possessed a monopoly over certain industries like iron and textiles in the region. This put skilled members of their religion, who were knowledgeable enough to act as missionaries, in high demand among other communities. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa ’ulama, called malams, service a variety of non-Muslim groups in Nigeria.

A Hausa manuscript is typically kept between wood, cardboard, or paper boards, and tied with string. It is then bound in tadarishi, a four-folded, quality red orange or brown leather, commissioned individually with an incised pattern. The finished manuscript is kept in a leather bag called a gafaka. Leatherworkers are often literate, sometimes malams or ex-students. Limited literacy is considered a mark of quality and professional integration, and most of the technical expertise come from written classical sources. Variations on this model include specially made binding, or cloth sacks for transportation.

Writing begins with the reed-cut Pen, derived from the gamba (Andropogon guyanus) or tuara (Cymbopogan genus) plants. Pen size varies with use, and they are kept in leather, metal, or wood cases. Tsume or Yambari are high-quality inks used for paper, as opposed to wooden writing boards. They involve intensive production, essentially the boiling of a mixture of wood chips, Gabarawa pods, rust and slag, and gum arabic. The result is waterproof and highly durable. Paper is traditionally hard to obtain, revered, and in the day-to-day replaced by wooden boards, which takes on an emblematic function for more common types of writing. Paper is still treated as a rare commodity even after increased availability in the modern day, or as a status symbol and a mark of experience, especially when used for Qur’ans. Watermarks on the page indicate paper quality. Writing Qur’an on paper counts as an act of legitimation, or a rite of passage for Malams. The development of printed manuscripts has lowered demand slightly, but handwritten books still carry status significant status and value on a basis of individual ownership. There is no fixed priced for commissioning a manuscript; it is instead decided informally between the malam artisan and their patron (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This is a mask worn by women in the Sande women's regional association across Liberia, often associated with the Vai and Mende cultures. This mask, known as a bundu, is worn during celebrations and ceremonies marking the transition of a girl into womanhood and gives the wearer the full rights and responsibility to participate in the Sande society. A spiritual force embodying the feminine ideal is conferred upon the young woman when she dons the mask; the sowo spirit is channeled through her in the mask and costume and in moments of civic importance, she herself becomes an embodiment of the sowo-- a difficult to attain image of female beauty and spiritual power.

The social responsibility of the woman who wears this bundu is to demonstrate the political and social strength of the women's associate through her careful conduct in the performance of the duties of the sowo. The mask "embodies and mediates contradictions between traditional feminine role models in the society and the actual political and economic power which women do have access to, if not consistently, then at least regularly and generally enough for that power to call forth a significant symbolic focus in the ideological realm." (Dubinskas 1976) This mask is worn to spiritually consecrate the vital role of women's collectives in the political and cultural life of their communities.
