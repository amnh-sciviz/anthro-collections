Curatorial Notes: Weaving and embroidery, culturally classified as a women’s craft, are passed down from mother to daughter. The weaving process begins by creating wool thread: fleece is washed, divided, spun, and sometimes dyed. While many textiles produced by Berber weavers are intricate, the looms used are often simple, consisting of sticks and stones that can be set up in a few minutes. Completing an item may take months, and skilled weavers complete around three articles in a winter. Although the process is laborious, weaving is a source of social activity: “Most Berber women say the hours at the loom are their most pleasant [...] with the company of other women who dropped by to lend a hand or hare the latest news” (Brooks 1996, 40).

Berber textiles include fertility symbolism in both production and composition and thus contain metaphors of motherhood. Wool, the loom, and the act of weaving are considered to contain “baraka,” or ‘blessing,’ and reflect female reproduction. Specifically, the weaving process is symbolic of life stages: the textile is ‘born’ when the warp threads are attached to the vertical loom, containing a ‘soul’ or “ruh,” and the textile ‘dies’ when the process is complete and the weaver cuts it from the loom. Fertility symbolism in textile motifs, colors, and composition also contribute to motherhood symbolism. According to Cynthia Becker, in the 1970s, women began embroidering elaborate nature motifs on their clothes. The flower motif in particular is a clear symbol of female fertility.

References
Becker, Cynthia J. "Amazigh Textiles and Dress in Morocco Metaphors of Motherhood." African Arts 39, no. 3 (2006): 47-52.

Brooks, Geraldine. Imazighen: The Vanishing Traditions of Berber Women. 1st ed. New York, NY: Clarkson Potter (1996): 40.

Curatorial Notes: Berber textile colors correspond to the “aesthetic of symmetry”: light and dark colors are placed next to one another and separated by a band of white wool (Becker 2006, 47). The Berber classification of light and dark colors designate red and yellow--resembling sunlight--as light colors and green, black, blue, and purple as dark colors. Although red is a dominant color in Berber textiles, red, green, yellow, and black are regularly used, associated generally with the natural life cycle and specifically with female fertility. For example, this color palette is associated with the different hues of henna as well as the colors of a rainbow--”an important metaphor of female fertility” (Becker 2006, 47). These colors were originally sourced from a variety of indiginous plants and minerals such as saffron, pomegranate, and indigo.
