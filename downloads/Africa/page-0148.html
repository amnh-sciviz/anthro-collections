Curatorial Notes: This is a mask worn by women in the Sande women's regional association across Liberia, often associated with the Vai, Kpelle and Mende cultures. This mask, known as a bundu, is worn during celebrations and ceremonies marking the transition of a girl into womanhood and gives the wearer the full rights and responsibility to participate in the Sande society. A spiritual force embodying the feminine ideal is conferred upon the young woman when she dons the mask; the sowo spirit is channeled through her in the mask and costume and in moments of civic importance, she herself becomes an embodiment of the sowo-- a difficult to attain image of female beauty and spiritual power.

The social responsibility of the woman who wears this bundu is to demonstrate the political and social strength of the women's associate through her careful conduct in the performance of the duties of the sowo. The mask "embodies and mediates contradictions between traditional feminine role models in the society and the actual political and economic power which women do have access to, if not consistently, then at least regularly and generally enough for that power to call forth a significant symbolic focus in the ideological realm." (Dubinskas 1976) This mask is worn to spiritually consecrate the vital role of women's collectives in the political and cultural life of their communities.

Curatorial Notes: This metal strip incised with a geometric pattern and sculpted into a bow with an arrow piercing its center is found throughout Yoruba shrines to Ososi (also known as Oshosi), the warrior deity (orisha) of hunters and a mighty archer of the gods. His power and strength are reflected in the speed and accuracy of his arrow as well as in his quick as lightening movements that one glimpses only the deity’s shadow and what are left behind: his hunter’s fly whisk (irukere), arrow (ofa), and bow (orun). Thus in his honor, Ososi’s bow and arrow are immortalized in iron as seen displayed here or in brass (such as the one rendered on its left, 90.2/8) as an emblem of his esteemed status.

The reverence for Ososi stemmed from an admiration of Yorubaland archery which claimed Oyo Yoruba as being the best bowmen in Africa; their arrows carrying iron heads. Indeed, Ososi is one of the gods most worshipped by persons of Ketu Yoruba descent in which these venerated metal symbols of a lone arrow boldly crossing a single bow are essential elements of traditional Ososi sacrificial altars (ojubo ososi), and his shrines—dry, leafless branches placed in a careful pile on the earth—transport the forest, that is, the hunting ground into the villages.

Curatorial Notes: This wooden figural offering bowl is called an olumeye, which translates as “one who knows honor.” Among the Yoruba it is customary to serve kola nuts (obi) to guests as an expression of hospitality and gesture of welcome and friendship. As a sign of prestige, the wealthy would offer obi in an elaborately-designed container made especially for this purpose. Many of these high-status, ornate sculptures represent a kneeling woman holding the bowl with a carved cockerel as its lid much like the olumeye displayed here. It is believed that the kneeling position embodies respect, devotion and submission; the fowl symbolizing honor and prosperity as a ritual sacrifice. Ultimately, this sculpture was identified by Lamidi Fakeye, a well-known master carver, as a traditionally conceived piece made by him in 1948 and created specifically for sale to tourists and collectors.
