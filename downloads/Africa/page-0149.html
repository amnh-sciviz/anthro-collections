Curatorial Notes: This crocodile figure was collected by missionary George Schwab during his travels to Liberia in 1928 (Schwab, 1947). There he met Gola people who today live mainly in Liberia but also in nearby Sierra Leone. They speak Gola, a branch of the Niger-Congo language family, and are an agricultural society mainly cultivating rice. Many religious ceremonies are devoted to farming because of its central role in the community. One example is the thanksgiving ceremony where the first grain of the season is offered to the ancestral spirits (Mazama & Mazama, 2008).

Figurative sculptures in Liberia, like this crocodile, are associated with the spiritual world and have a wide range of uses, including medicine, protection, and divination (Siegmann & Schmidt, 1977). Other times sculptures can simply be a personal possession that is used for decoration and reflects the individual’s prestige (Siegmann & Schmidt, 1977).

Regarding the “rings of power”, these rings are most likely bracelets or anklets which are commonly worn by women in West Africa. These ornaments serve many purposes, either for leg protection while walking in the bush or for “distinction of sex” (Sieber, 1972). Sometimes the rings illustrate a person’s honor or status; more rings are indicative of greater respect accorded to a person (Sieber, 1972). Therefore, this crocodile must have had important social or cultural significance.

Curatorial Notes: This cover was made by a member of the Hausa, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. The model allowed Muslim clergy to pursue other trades as well. Groups like the Hausa and Mande, who rapidly adopted Islam, possessed a monopoly over certain industries like iron and textiles in the region. This put skilled members of their religion, who were knowledgeable enough to act as missionaries, in high demand among other communities. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa ’ulama, called malams, service a variety of non-Muslim groups in Nigeria.

Hausa manuscripts are typically bound as loose paper in folio. To provide a sense of organization, malam authors utilize Matashi, as system wherein the first word of next page located at lower corner of the preceding page. This allows for continued recital as well as keeping unbound pages in order. A folio is kept between wood, cardboard, or paper boards, and tied with string. It is then bound in tadarishi, a four-folded, quality red orange or brown leather, commissioned individually with an incised pattern. The finished manuscript is kept in a leather bag called a gafaka. Leatherworkers are often literate, sometimes malams or ex-students. Limited literacy is considered a mark of quality and professional integration, and most of the technical expertise come from written classical sources. Variations on this model include specially made binding, or cloth sacks for transportation (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This leather-bound text of Qur’anic legal commentary was donated by Dr. Joseph Greenberg, prominent linguist and anthropologist who studied around the Hausa center of Kano from 1938-39. The handwritten copying of books, even those not considered scripture, is still said to be a pious act for highly trained scholars, and the resulting manuscripts are prized by the Hausa (Hassan 1990).

The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the fourteenth century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

The earliest spread of Islam in Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco (Greenberg). The Muslim cultures of the Maghreb (Northwestern Africa above the Sahara) have exerted a great deal of influence ever since. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. The religion was mostly restricted to urban areas, among a literate elite which preserved older styles of written Arabic (Greenberg, Hassan).

Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. The model allowed Muslim clergy to pursue other trades as well. Groups like the Hausa and Mande, who rapidly adopted Islam, possessed a monopoly over certain industries like iron and textiles in the region. This put skilled members of their religion, who were knowledgeable enough to act as missionaries, in high demand among other communities. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa ’ulama, called malams, service a variety of non-Muslim groups in Nigeria.

Hausa manuscripts are typically bound as loose paper in folio. To provide a sense of organization, malam authors utilize Matashi, as system wherein the first word of next page located at lower corner of the preceding page. This allows for continued recital as well as keeping unbound pages in order. A folio is kept between wood, cardboard, or paper boards, and tied with string. It is then bound in tadarishi, a four-folded, quality red orange or brown leather, commissioned individually with an incised pattern. The finished manuscript is kept in a leather bag called a gafaka. Leatherworkers are often literate, sometimes malams or ex-students. Limited literacy is considered a mark of quality and professional integration, and most of the technical expertise come from written classical sources. Variations on this model include specially made binding, or cloth sacks for transportation (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist culture who live in the grasslands of modern-day Kenya and Tanzania. The moran is a specific age-set in between uncircumcised boys and junior elders in Maasai society. Maasai men are initiated into the moran between the ages of twelve and eighteen, depending on their age at the point of the four or five year cycle (Hodgson 1999: 126). Maasai warriors herd cattle together, drink milk together, and sleep in homesteads together. The moran do not cut their hair. Instead, they wear it in a single braid that is treated with fat and red ochre (Galaty 1983: 369). This lasts until the warriors participate in the eunoto rite of passage and become senior warriors. Once all ornaments worn previously have been removed, they transition into junior elders. It is at this point that they must marry and give up their elaborate dress and arguably privileged lifestyle. The required constant community with other men as a warrior diminishes as the new elder establishes his own homestead, individuality, and political power.

A Maasai warrior wears this headdress during special ceremonies, such as the eunoto. This is considered the most significant compilation of events for Maasai warriors. The entire ceremony can last several months and includes a series of promotions. At the end of the ceremony, the warriors mothers cut their sons hair and shave their heads. This marks their entry into senior warriorhood (Galaty 1983: 369)

This type of headdress is called olowuaru, and is made from a lions mane and also includes a beaded strap made of red, green, yellow, and white beads. (Klumpp 1987: 211). The lion has deep cultural meaning for the Maasai. Historically, Maasai warriors had a reputation for ritually killing a lion along with one or two witnesses in order to prove his manhood. The moran participated in other types of competing events, such as wrestling and spear throwing, which enhanced prestige on an individual and group level (Hodgson 1999: 126).

Curatorial Notes: The Bambara of Mali are Mande-speaking people who live on the Bani and Upper Niger Rivers who are predominantly agrarian. The Bambara people credit Ci-Wara, a spiritual being, with teaching them agriculture; it is under Ci-Waras guidance, they say, that they became successful and able farmers.

This antelope headdress is worn by Bambara male dancers, Ci-Wara (translated to farm animals). It is often worn in pairs of one male and female antelope. The antelope symbolizes the farmer who tills his fields tirelessly (Siegmann 2009: 62). Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good
job (Phillips 1995: 502).

The headdress exemplifies the Bambara peoples devotion to agricultural cultivation and fertility. The organization of the Ci-wara performance is based on the Bamana respect for the power and efficiency of the union of male and female. As human reproduction is the result of the sexual union between man and woman, so agricultural fertility is attributed to the union between fire (the sun), an expression of the male principle, and earth and water, an expression of the female principle. (Colleyn 2001: 204).

The horns of the antelope represent ears of millet and the zigzag design of the mane represents the sun. The costume worn by the performers consists of raffia fibers that extend from the base of the headdress to the ground and represent the rain needed for cultivation.The female antelope has a child on its back depicting the way mothers carry their children, but also calling attention to animal, plant, and human fertility (Siegmann 2009: 62). The headdresses hold a parallel between the penetration of the earth through farming and copulation (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

The headdress is associated with different purposes, however, as one observer noted, like all other dances of the Bamana that of [Ci-Wara] was never static .the development and change took place somewhat independently in each region and indeed in each village (Lagamma 2002: 71). Likewise, each performance is unique and no one description will be able to convey the many tribal traditions.

It is used during agricultural competitions. During these competitions young farmers are galvanized by songs, music, and the masked dances. They seek to imitate the mythical animals that originally taught humans agriculture. During the competition the men work through a number of rows in a field and the first to completion is the winner and declared the Ci-Wara. The title brings much praise to the winner. Men are constantly invited to take up the contest, just as their ancestors had done before, and see how they measure up to the heroes of the past.

It is also used during entertainment and ritual events. They appeared during festivals at the Sgou court. After colonial conquest they were also a part of the July 14th Bastille celebrations, French National Day.

Third, it is used during an annual two day celebration that takes place between November and April, according to the moons appearance. This celebration includes sacrifices and the remaking of cult objects. It also includes a parade through the village with stops
a spots where the union of the male and female masks are symbolized (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

This woolen headcord (ifran) would have been used to secure an Atta womens fabric head covering, tassebnit. The direct effects of this transition on Atta culture are particularly notable in textile production. Additionally, Hart played in integral role in the development and construction of the museums desert diorama in the permanent Hall of African Peoples exhibit.

Curatorial Notes: Berber men wear a long white cotton shirt underneath their tajellabiyt in addition to white or blue cotton trousers. The tajellabiyt, a hooded, long-sleeved tunic made of undyed sheep’s wool, is a common outer garment worn by men across Morocco and throughout North Africa--although today, they are also worn by women.

The color white represents the “moral qualities and good fortune” of the wearer (Becker 2006, 50). The white cotton shirt, in combination with the tajellabiyt, arezziey (turban), and silham (outer cape), embraces imagery that “associates men with Islam and the qualities of modesty, honor, and dignity” (Becker 2006, 52).
