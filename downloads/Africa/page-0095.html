Category: COSTUME
Share Object

Field Notes: EGAKU. The string with which women and some men tie up their head, also used for children. It is made by women from bast of the raffia palm. Photographs have been taken of the manner of braiding and two of the sticks = GADOKIRRA upon which the string is braided are in the collection No. lll3, lll4. The string is blackened by crushing the leaves and bark of a tree = EPADA, (see sample No. lll5) putting it into water and washing it in the maceration received. Without drying it the string is put into the mud for a day and in this way becomes very black. (This is the first time I hear of the addition of the leaves and bark of this tree to blacken objects.) The interpreter I took with me from MEDJE says now that the MEDJE also use the bark and leaves of a tree (but not the same). The same bark and leaves or rather the maceration of it is used for blackening pots. It is in this process I saw it first time employed. It is put on when the jars are still hot during the process of backing them. After blackening the string is cleaned of all the small outstanding pieces. No. 1112 is fastened to a form of wood so the perfectness and size may be seen at once and especially in order to smoothen out all possible kinks, as the form has about the circumference of a human head, the arrangement is perfect. See photographs. Nearly all women know how to braid the string.

The MANGBETU keep dogs as domestic for eating, but at the present time there are many European dogs introduced which cross readily with the native dogs. The MANGBETU say that these dogs are no good eating and bring illness, etc. They have, therefore, practically stopped eating their dogs which are nearly all crossbred with European dogs. It is impossible to get any object of former times such as caps decorated with human teeth as at the death of a chief or the king everything he owned is invariably destroyed. They have great ceremonies and according to traditions certain objects are thrown in the forest, certain are burned in the fire, others thrown in the river. Nothing is preserved except objects of sorcery which are inherited from generation to generation. In former times even the women with very few exceptions were slaughtered and eaten. This practice has been stopped entirely.
