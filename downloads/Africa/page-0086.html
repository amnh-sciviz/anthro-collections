Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, they have a rich musical heritage that plays an important role in various aspects of Mangbetu society. Music, and musical instruments themselves, held symbolic significance when in employed in particular social settings.

According to the expedition field notes, this is a “Rattle with medicine whistle.” Nangbara, Mangbetu basket rattles, were played by either men or by women during dances, but specifically by men during hunts (African Reflections 206). Attached to the nangbara is a whistle, an addition that distinguishes the rattle from an ordinary court instrument. Almost all Mangbetu rattles were associated with the supernatural, and as such whistle orchestras did not exist in Mangbetu society because whistles were not played for secular entertainment (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 204). Individual whistles held different magical and medicinal properties. The odikire whistle was used to produce rainfall upon an enemy during battle in order to hinder their ability to fight. The nekire whistle held numerous functions. It was used to call gods to witness a human oath, and it was also employed to bring good luck and protect the owner. “The Mangbetu concept of newo, medicine, included the possession by certain objects and substances of the power to cure or heal,” (Lyons 1992: 180). The nekire whistle held such power, and it was blown in order to heal a sick person. Kings and chiefs usually owned several whistles.

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, they have a rich musical heritage that plays an important role in various aspects of Mangbetu society.

Mangbetu basket rattles are called nangbara (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 206). They are made from wood and plant fiber, and they are filled with seeds. Women, using the same skills used in woven basket production, typically weave the head of the rattle. During dances they are played by either men or by women, but particularly played by men during hunts. Women played them during court dances.

Schildkrout, Enid, and Curtis A. Keim. African Reflections: Art from Northeastern Zaire. Seattle: U of Washington, 1990.
