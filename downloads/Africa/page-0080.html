PRAYER BOARD
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 90.1/ 1379
Culture: TSWANA? (TWANA?)
Locale: NORTH OF LAKE LAGAHI, NGAMI?
Country: BOTSWANA?
Material: WOOD, PIGMENT, METAL
Dimensions: L:44.1 W:18.7 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1915 [PURCHASE]
Donor: DOUGLAS
Keywords: PRAYER BOARD

Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout Islamic Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general. Though it was acquired in what is now Botswana in southern Africa, the exact origins of this board are unclear. Allo are known to be utilized extensively by the Hausa people of West Africa. The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups on the continent (Hassan 1990), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Among the Hausa, the clerics who use this technology are called Malams, from Arabic mu’alim “scholar.” Malams oversee the Muslim education of a Hausa community. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of Islamic education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. This still continues, despite the lasting cultural impact of the Sokoto Caliphate, with it’s campaign for universal Islamic education, including women. The Malam does not expect regular compensation for their teaching, and regards what they do as a service. However, gifts of unfixed value are traditionally given in the middle of the week by children on behalf of grateful parents. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).

Researcher: Gabriel Chance Sevigny (2018)

Sources
Greenberg, Joseph H. The Influence of Islam on a Sudanese Religion. New York: J.J.
Augustin, 1947. Print.

Hassan, Salah E. M. Lore of the Traditional Malam: Material Culture of Literacy and
Ethnography of Writing Among the Hausa of Northern Nigeria. 1990. Print.

Catalog No: 90.1/ 1429
Culture: MANGBETU
Locale: MEDJE
Country: CONGO (BELGIAN CONGO)
Material: PLANT FIBER, FEATHER
Dimensions: L:50 W:30 H:20 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1915 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: LANG, HERBERT
Keywords: HAT
Category: COSTUME
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Field Notes: EMANGBALANGA. Worn only by men south of MEDJE as a sign of mourning at the death of an OKAPI to propitiate the bad spirits. If they do not wear these hats they become sick or even die. They are woven in the forest by the hunters themselves. Still worn long after their return into their villages.
Exhibition History
• AFRICAN REFLECTIONS: ART FROM NORTHEASTERN ZAIRE. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. June 1990 - February 1994
Exhibition Label: Basketry hat with feathers and leaves. A Meje hunter would wear this special hat to ward off misfortune after killing an okapi. The hat protected against illness or even death caused by the avenging power of the animal. The hat was woven in the forest by the hunters and worn long after their return to the village. As with hats worn for mourning, the untied fringe suggests 'loose ends' left unresolved by death.

Field Notes: EMUPETI A sort of medicine worn around the waist and the arms (See photograph). After the MANGBETU kill an OKAPI, they have great fears to die or to get seriously ill. In order to avoid such misfortunes they take a heavy creeper and beat it with a piece of wood (holding it over another smooth piece of wood) until all the fibres are separated. It is then washed out of light gray color. They lay in into the mud to blacken it. They often have dances accompanied by very monotonous songs to propitiate the bad spirits of the OKAPI. The latter expression however is not correct as they do not believe actually in the spirit of a killed OKAPI, but they believe if they will ever step into (or over the place they have lain after years) the excrement of the killed OKAPI, without having performed these ceremonies, misfortunes will never leave them. They also believe that many bad spirits are housing in the forest, but there is evidently no peculiar form or body imagined as they invariably answer on demand to describe spirits, that they are in the trees, leaves, grass on the road in fact n every possible object that might be able to reach them.

Field Notes: NEKAKA. Sewn by women worn as a decoration in front of the NOGITWE , it varies in form from a simple square of the breadth of a hand, to 1 foot in length and the width of hand or wider. The NEKAKA is composed of dried banana leaves, flattened stems of grass, the ever-varing decoration is purely individual and considered fancy. Many NEKAKAS show no decoration. As a rule the women carry no NEKAKA, but a well dressed and decorated woman, is seldom without, it may therefore be considered almost MEDJE as some piece of the dress belonging to ceremonies and extraordinary occasions, except with women of distinction who are always well painted up and decorated if at all they carry native dress. Very often the imported [?] cloth fastened above the breast and reaching nearly to the ground replaces native dress.
