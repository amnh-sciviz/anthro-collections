Field Notes: GAFU. A sort of spade, the only tool to work the fields. The LOGO (men only) do all the rough or heavier agricultural work. The women only take out the weeds, etc. For use see photograph. They stand erect and keeping it in a slanting position thrust it into the soil. Turning over the vegetation and eradicating it. They hardly scrape the soil and seldom work it deeper than 3 inches. The principal work is done at the beginning of the heavy rains at the end of June and July. First all the weeds, the sorghum that has grown up from the year before and bushes, etc., are all cut down and left drying and rotting for about a fortnight. The soil thus greatly profits as the ground is considerably roughened (by pulling out and turning over the grass with the root stocks) absorbs considerable more water and washes out the soil adhering to roots. What is left (the heavier sticks) is thrown by the women in heaps and burnt after a few sunny days. When they are clearing a new field, the men first with this spade burn all the root stocks of the grass over (eradicate it) and afterwards cut down all the trees and bushes at a height of about 2 to 3 feet. A month thereafter all is burnt. Heavier trees are burnt at their base by making just these fires around them and are thus deprived of their foliage that would throw too much shadows. These GITAS are forged by the LOGOS in a very crude way of magnetite After reducing the ore in using two or three bellows, the tubes of which come together in one h...ole made into the ground, they beat it with small blocks. Afterwards it is forged by hammering with different stones. The anvil and the hammer both are stones. For the smaller work they have made a hammer fastened to a stick. The stones with which they pound the iron are all smoothened stones as they find them in rivers (like big pebbles). They use their hands in lifting and dropping them upon the iron in works. To temper iron they use water. As they stick the iron in the pot filled with water as soon as they have beaten it. The iron is put immediately back into the fire and as soon as red hot they work upon it for about 1/2 minute and put it back in the water and then in the fire. A man can finish seldom more than 3 GITAS a day. They usually have a roughly made hut a hundred yards away from the other huts in which they carry on the work of forging the iron. Usually three men and a few boys work together. The men forge or hold the iron or hold the iron between with the boys to operate the bellows, made of clay mixed with vegetable fibres and simply hardened.
