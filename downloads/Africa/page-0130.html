Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist cultural group who live in both Kenya and Tanzania along the Rift Valley. Historically, the style and form has changed throughout time, however the symbolism and social context is relatively constant. The moran warriors are young Maasai men responsible for herding, protecting, and capturing cattle. They use their spears in throwing competitions to show off their prowess. The spear is used for decorative purposes as well; it is part of the warriors daily dress. It is iconic of the Maasai because of their violent reputation for raids and ceremonial lion kills. Spears made for sale in the tourism industry are never used or functional, and are conveniently adjustable to fit inside a travelers suitcase.

The spear can have multiple meanings depending on the age-grade. Brand new spears are considered appropriate for Maasai warriors. As they begin to become worn and aged however, the spears are given to elders and young boys. When they become even more worn out, the spears are given to younger boys and older elders. A Maasai carrying an aged spear symbolizes his non-warrior status. Each age-grade has its own stylistic preferences; therefore the style of spear is reminiscent of a specific group of warriors. They also adopt innovations from other ethnic groups.

The spears are made out of wood or metal often with an attached ball of fur, in this case, lion skin is attached at the base. The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasais animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemys cultural boundary unharmed.

Curatorial Notes: The Asante (also spelled Ashanti) are a large political confederation of people who speak the Akan language in southern Ghana, known in the colonial period as the “Gold Coast.” The ruler, or Asantehene, derives his legitimacy from a divinely created Golden Stool that has become a powerful symbol of Asante royalty, culture, and ideology. The Asante enjoyed a significant rise in prestige, wealth, and influence from their founding in the 16th century, until British invasion in the 19th (Phillips 1999).

Amulets of this type are associated with itinerant Muslim clerics throughout West Africa (Handloff 1982). Islamic mysticism holds written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, It follows that a signifier is literally connected to the idea or physical thing it represents. This allows objects containing writing, such as these amulets, to possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden, secret meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret’ (Hassan 1990).

There may be several types of text employed in these charms, from written prayer, to names of God (Hassan), designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Katemi (Handloff) or Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim. “seal”). incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, creates potency (Hassan).
