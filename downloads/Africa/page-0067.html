Field Notes: "Musical bow (6), Batua pattern, Bomashuba, 0.35F" (248) ; 15th february 1906 : " A fellow who has been a veritable nuisance, coming with unwanted insects and other things, a great hulking lazy young fellow, who always irritates me and whose tooth impresssion I took yesterday in the hope that it would finish him, appeared today with a musical bow of the Baluba type. As I saw him in the distance, my first impulse was to hurl and XXX at him, my second to flee. When, however, he was near, I saw that his instrument was vastly better than we had seen before, less amateurish and boyish, more of an instrument. And he knew how to play it too : so we took him in hand and photographed him, made two good [underlined twice] records of his music and bought his instrument at the same time ordering five more. While we were making preparations for all this Manuel told me he knew a new instrument, which I had not seen and on my demanding its production he brought out a Bomashuba with a third type of musical bow [description follows p 257 as well]" (256)

The pipe bowls were variously decorated with incised lines. They were used mostly by women: I understand they are woman's work-- Manuel saw a pipe made from a horn and pipes for hemp made from calabashes." (79); sketch of pipe (79); see also Field Nos. 14, 16

Since this object is noted as having been acquired for free, it is probably one of the necklaces Starr received from Millman.

Field Notes: Note: Although Starr's object No. 3270 is listed as ""Native money: lobela nakingo = 100 m. 19.60 [for group]" (639), this object does not appear to correspond with the description for that number. Instead, it seems to more closely resemble Starr's descriptions for either object No. 3271 ["Native money: mombonge = 60 m."] or object No. 3272 ["Native money: ekeki = 20 m.], both of which are armband-style currency. On 8 September 1906, he describes these two objects as follows: "ekeki is a rather heavy armring--20 met. Mombonge is like it but more open and heavier being worth 60 metaku" (628). Since we have only this one object, it is difficult to make a comparison and determine which of these descriptions it is supposed to match. Either way, Starr has this to say about the native monies he has purchased: "There are five forms each with a distinct name and value. They are still used but only in buying a woman or a canoe" (628).

Field Notes: "Heavy brass neckring, Bobangi, 20" (415). 31 May 1906. Starr clearly refers to purchasing this neckring on 31 May: "Several sellers called during the morning tho we bought comparatively little; one good knife and one heavy neckring were the most important" (460). Earlier in the narrative on 28 May he describes how such neckrings are made and worn (although he does not purchase the neckring until a few days later): "Brass neckrings are in constant use among the women; many of them contain 500 to 600 brass rods. Most of them have some incised decorations near the ends; this occupies a relatively short space and the pattern seems to be much the same in all. These are cast as straight bars; they are then engraved and polished; they are then bent upon a round post firmly planted in the ground, only enough opening being left for the woman's neck; after it is in place it is hammered shut by the blacksmith" (419).
