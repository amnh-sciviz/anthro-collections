Field Notes: "Salt leaching cups. Lokele. Yakusu. .40" (643). Upon purchasing a cake of salt from the Lokele on 24 September 1906, Starr comments, "... apparently they make salt here as well for they make a little perforated bottomed bowl cup, which is apparently a filter or leacher: the brine falling into the lower cups" (664). On 26 September 1906 he purchases some of these cups and tests them out: "... I took a ramble in Yakusu. Made a run on the little salt vessels. The burned plant material is put into a little conical vessel with perforated bottom, which rests in a little bowl cup of proper size. Water is poured on to leach. The brine is caught in the cup and no doubt evaporated in it. These are usually made in couple, matching in size, material and decoration and are rather nice" (667-668).

Unfortunately, my mention of this appointment at the dinner table scared Mr. Morgan, who finally begged me not to have it done in the Station. So when the man appeared we went outside, where he selected a quiet spot out on the grass and went through his performances. Curatorial Notes: This is a metal Kuba knife and an accompanying fur sheath from Central Africa. The Kuba Kingdom, from what is now the southeastern part of the Congo, was strongest in the mid-nineteenth century. It was comprised of many culture groups, including the Bushoong, Shoowa, Kel, and Ngoombe. It was taken over by the Nsapo people in the late nineteenth century and declined afterwards. The Kuba are also referred to as the "Children of Woot", in reference to the deity Woot, of the Kuba religion.

Curatorial Notes: This object is a necklace that comes from Nigeria but was acquired by Frederick Starr, through his expedition to the Congo during the early twentieth century. Nigeria, located in the heart of West Africa, is incredibly diverse and encompasses numerous ethnic groups including the Yoruba, Hausa, Fulani, Kanuri and Ibo. Although the ethnic background associated with the item is uncertain, the necklace serves both an aesthetic purpose as well as more social and spiritual ones.

In Nigeria, the primary means of ornamentation included necklaces alongside with bracelets, armlets, anklets, and earrings. These items not only possessed an aesthetic quality, but also were a means of asserting and producing status. Generally, pieces of jewelry, such as this necklace, could signify the wearers position in his or her respective culture through the quality of the materials used and the craftsmanship exhibited[1].

Furthermore, the necklace sheds light onto the manner in which Nigerian culture managed to assimilate and simultaneously manage multiple ideologies. In Nigeria, a country that is predominantly Muslim and Christian, pendants, not only serve an ornamental purpose, but often are worn as a charm against one or other of the various evil spirits which infest the land[2]. This practice harkens back to indigenous animist traditions that emphasize the veneration of spirits[3] and their influence on daily human life[4]
