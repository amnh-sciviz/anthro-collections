Curatorial Notes: This drum, apart of the African Ethnographic Collection, was purchased in 1959 from M. L. J. Lemaire, one of the first tribal art dealers in Amsterdam.
For the Ashanti, this particular shape of drum is known as a Kwadum, and would have been made of a hollowed out log of a cedar tree, locally known as tweneboa kodua, and the shape and general size of the drum produces a distinct sound (Anku 43). The drumhead of this type of drum is typically made from the skins goat, cow, duiker, or antelope (Anku 44). Drum heads on royal drums of the Akan are sometimes even made out of elephant ear hyde (Anku 44). The pegs of the drum, much like the drum itself, are also made from specific trees, particularly from the branches of the ofemma tree in Akan tradition (Anku 44). It is important to use these types of branches to ensure the sturdiness of the pegs through the strains on them caused by lacing and turning the heads of the drums (Anku 44).
Unlike other West African drumming nations who use straight drum sticks, the Akan use both straight and curved sticks depending on which drum they are beating (Anku 45). Curved sticks, for example, are most often used on Donno drums, the rounded stick almost resembling a small walking cane. However, hand techniques are also used when doing traditional drumming by the Akan (Anku 46). In fact, the Akan use several different types of stick and hand stroking on their drums, which include muted strokes (very delicate, light strokes that are almost silent), and “stopped strokes (rather loud, usually one stick or hand pressing the drum head while the other strikes at the centre of the head)” (Anku 46).
The ensemble in which the Akan arrange their instruments creates a distinct flow of different pitches. The Akan recognize the lower pitches that larger drums make, and thus organize their ensembles in a ranked order of sounds from highest to lowest (Anku 40). This drum in particular would likely be placed somewhere in the middle of the ensemble for its medium size and mid-tone. Drums, however, are not the only instrument present within these ensembles. Other instruments that may accompany them include bells, rattles, stick clappers (idiophones), and a combination of drums other than this one (the Kwadum) (Anku 40). Women often accompany the ensemble through singing in chorus.

Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret.' The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques.

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan 1990).

These particular charms were attached to a violin that was used in the Bori society, which performs a ritual of spirit possession and dance through which Hausa women seek healing (Picton & Harper). The presence of an Islamic element combined with animist utility shows the degree to which these traditions have been syncretized, or incorporated together, by the Hausa people.

Curatorial Notes: This ceramic pot shard with red paint pigment was used to create one of several varieties of costumes representing the Dodo spirit mai-ayaban daji. The costumes worn by Dodo dancers vary in material and style according to an individual’s village, family, or other of origin; mai-ayaban daji all consist of the same body costume and headdress of dried banana fronds, but cult groups distinguish themselves by the unique colors and design of a shield of coiled grass and a carved wooden crest placed at the top of the headdress. Both the shield and crest are painted with a feather brush in only blue, red, and white.
During a 1957-1959 expedition to Dass District, northern Nigeria, researcher Francis P. Conant collected hundreds of objects from the Barawa people of the Dass Plateau in northeast Nigeria. With the exception of a minority of Christian and Muslim converts, the Barawa follow the Dodo religion, which, along with the mountainous topography of the area they inhabit, both joins the Barawa under a common thread and yet also separates them.
Dodo religion centers around an ancestral spirit called Dodo, and the individual’s lifetime journey towards attaining the state of being Dodo. One’s progress towards Dodo really begins at birth, but is officially marked during the kumasu, the ceremony that marks initiation of young males into Dodo membership. The progress ends after one’s funeral rites are completed, upon which one is “fully” Dodo. While this is the general principle behind Dodo religion, the fluid nature of Dodo facilitates individualized ways of practicing the religion, leading to the rise of competing rival “cult” groups that serve to undermine Barawa political and social unity.
Conant’s research emphasized the plasticity and practicality of Dodo philosophy, a characteristic that he believed accounted for the religion’s longevity in such an ethnically and religiously diverse region: “At every level, conceptual, organizational, and technological, mountaineer religion may be characterized best as being eminently pragmatic, changed and changing according to the demands of its practitioners. That these demands are few – the mountaineer being a resolutely independent kind of person and committed to an ethic of "self-help” – perhaps largely accounts for the persistence through time of such concepts as do exist.”
