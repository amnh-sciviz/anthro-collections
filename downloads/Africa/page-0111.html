Curatorial Notes: This leather amulet was created among The Mossi people, an ethno-political group that first emerged in the 15th century, when invaders from northern Ghana conquered several disparate groups in the Volta region of Burkina Faso. The resulting society was consolidated into two demographics: the conquering/ruling class, and the indigenous underclass, called nyonyose, meaning “ancient ones.” The nyonyose are believed to wield a particularly great amount of supernatural knowledge, often manifest in physical objects and craft (Phillips 1999).

Amulets of this type are are associated with itinerant Muslim clerics throughout West Africa (Hassan 1990). Islamic mysticism holds written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. The Dogon of Mali, for example, express the Word of God through geometric designs and gridwork that are believed to have power in the physical world. In local “magical” practice, objects containing writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden, secret meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret’ (Hassan).

There may be several types of text employed in in these charms, from written prayer, to names of God (Hassan), designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Katemi (Handloff 1982) or Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, creates potency (Hassan).
