Field Notes: NOADO - The word in Mangbetu means actually only calabash, the bowl for smoking hashish is called Neboli. The Mangbetu are passionately fond of hashish-smoking and they do their utmost to make their plantations far-off and out of sight of white men. The continued use deprives the smokers of their intellectual balance and often they arrive at a stage of excitement where they become dangerous to order. The Government destroys the plantation of hemp and serverely punishes those who barter or smoke hemp. The above pipes are used in the same manner as No. 152 but represent the models in common use among the Medje.

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

Mangbetu slit drums belong to an amalgam of West African drums from various peoples referred to as “talking drums” because of their use in long distance communication. Drums are vital to Mangbetu communication and court dance. The two main types of slit drum, the conical and the bell shaped, are manufactured in various sizes and with distinct adornments. The cordia millenii and the pterocarpus soyauxii trees are the most common trees used in slit drum manufacture. (Bariaux and Demolin 1995 : 8). Prior to selecting a tree for a drum, the maker tests the tree by striking the trunk and listening to the sound it produces.

There are two main types of slit drums: a flat, bell-shaped, and a “hollowed out log” that rests upon four legs, or two pads. Both drum types are manufactured in various sizes and adorned with in distinct styles. Conical slit drums, called nekpokpo, are commonly zoomorphic, taking on the appearance of a buffalo or antelope through the addition of carvings, such as a head and tail. The carved head, horns, and tail are functional adornments that served as carrying handles as well as a seat for the musician.

On this large nekpokpo, the drummer would sit atop the animal’s head or tail and spread his leg across the narrow opening in order to dampen the sound, a method that was integral to inter-village communication. Unequally hollowing out the two sides of the drum resulted in the creation of different tones utilized in the transmission of complicated verbal messages that mimicked Mangbetu spoken language(s). (Ross Miller : 53) The larger and louder a drum, the more power it held due to the amount of labor needed in its production and the far lengths to which messages could be transmitted. Slit drums could not be manufactured without the commission of the chief, which therefore lent more royal power to the largest of the slit drums.

This nekpokpo was acquired in Niangara, Democratic Republic of the Congo, a region traditionally associated with and inhabited by the Mangbetu. Due to a rich history of sharing music with the neighboring Zande, this nekpokpo could also have been manufactured by the Zande, as is listed in the case. If you have information about this instrument, we would be happy to hear from you. Please contact curatorial associate Jacklyn Lacey at jlacey@amnh.org

Ivory horns were vital instruments for Mangbetu royalty; ivory has long been held as a highly prized material. The use of this precious ivory and the special labor involved in its production firmly linked ivory horns with the power of the king. Ivory horn production was delegated to exceptional smiths who used metals tools to carve the instrument from an animal tusk; these smiths also carved decorative images and motifs on the exterior of the horn, as seen on object 90.1/ 1794. Herbert Lang, head of the Museum’s 1915 Congo expedition during which these horns were collected, noted that many of these horns were adorned with an anthropomorphic carving on the mouthpiece, a decorative motif also found on Mangbetu harps. The length of the horns could be extended by attaching a wooden tube to the larger end of the instrument, a device that also deepened the sound of the horn. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 202)

Ivory horn orchestras were primarily employed during court dances, such as the mabolo dance, in which the king danced alone in front of his subjects order to display his skill. The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the king’s prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

Ivory horns were vital instruments for Mangbetu royalty; ivory has long been held as a highly prized material. The use of this precious ivory and the special labor involved in its production firmly linked ivory horns with the power of the king. Ivory horn production was delegated to exceptional smiths who used metals tools to carve the instrument from an animal tusk; these smiths also carved decorative images and motifs on the exterior of the horn, as seen on object 90.1/ 1794. Herbert Lang, head of the Museum’s 1915 Congo expedition during which these horns were collected, noted that many of these horns were adorned with an anthropomorphic carving on the mouthpiece, a decorative motif also found on Mangbetu harps. The length of the horns could be extended by attaching a wooden tube to the larger end of the instrument, a device that also deepened the sound of the horn. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 202)

Ivory horn orchestras were primarily employed during court dances, such as the mabolo dance, in which the king danced alone in front of his subjects order to display his skill. The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the king’s prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).

Felix, Marc Leo. White Gold, Black Hands: Ivory Sculpture in Congo. Qiquhar, Heilungkiang, China: Gemini Sun, 2010.

Schildkrout, Enid, and Curtis A. Keim. African Reflections: Art from Northeastern Zaire. Seattle: U of Washington, 1990.

Research: Ava Muhr 2014
Field Notes: EMBONGO. Three ivory horns made by the king's artist. On small tusks they cut off the top, No. 1200 is one of the tips of the tusks. They take an ADZE and cut it roughly out as, for instance, the figure No. 1238. They cut it carefully with a knife like No.s 1155 & 1156 and smoothen it down with leaves that have a sandpaper-like surface. Formerly they had large ivory horns carved in about the same style but ivory has become somewhat precious and the chiefs are not inclined to make new horns as these horns are much appreciated. As soon as I can purchase a large tusk, his artist will prepare one for the museum's collection. They have been formerly used to communicate news from one agglomeration of MANGBETU to another, also during ceremonies and dances, and especially when visiting neighboring chiefs.
