Field Notes: Ebabaza - used as a broom for the interior of the huts. Every morning the hut is cleaned by a women especially designed for this work. As a rule, one of the women who is not going to the plantation on account of a child newly born or soon to be expected perform this work. Field Notes: NEKAKA. Sewn by women worn as a decoration in front of the NOGITWE, it varies in form from a simple square of the breadth of a hand, to 1 foot in length and the width of hand or wider. The NEKAKA is composed of dried banana leaves, flattened stems of grass, the ever-varying decoration is purely individual and considered fancy. Many NEKAKAS show no decoration. As a rule the women carry no NEKAKA, but a well dressed and decorated woman, is seldom without, it may therefore be considered almost MEDJE as some piece of the dress belonging to ceremonies and extraordinary occasions, except with women of distinction who are always well painted up and decorated if at all they carry native dress. Very often the imported [?] cloth fastened above the breast and reaching nearly to the ground replaces native dress.

Field Notes: NONGU. Red wood. The pieces of red wood are usually split off from fallen trees in the forest. Such stems are usually well known to a village and used by everybody nearby. They are rather common. To get the redwood powder which is so much employed they take the small piece and rub it up and down, while sand and water is continually added, the pieces are kept in a slanting position. A mud like dark brown mass is from time scraped off, which after drying it gives the powder. It is commonly used for toilet, mixed with oil will produce a very nice coppery tint all over the skin. As powder it is often put over the breast, forehead, back of the neck and shoulders especially at dances and ceremonies or similar joyful events. They smear their doors, the supporting sticks in the houses, the pots, knives, bows, barkclothes. MALIMBA in fact nearly all their effects with it .

Field Notes: EGAKU. The string with which women and some men tie up their head, also used for children. It is made by women from bast of the raffia palm. Photographs have been taken of the manner of braiding and two of the sticks = GADOKIRRA upon which the string is braided are in the collection No. lll3, lll4. The string is blackened by crushing the leaves and bark of a tree = EPADA, (see sample No. lll5) putting it into water and washing it in the maceration received. Without drying it the string is put into the mud for a day and in this way becomes very black. (This is the first time I hear of the addition of the leaves and bark of this tree to blacken objects.) The interpreter I took with me from MEDJE says now that the MEDJE also use the bark and leaves of a tree (but not the same). The same bark and leaves or rather the maceration of it is used for blackening pots. It is in this process I saw it first time employed. It is put on when the jars are still hot during the process of backing them. After blackening the string is cleaned of all the small outstanding pieces. No. 1112 is fastened to a form of wood so the perfectness and size may be seen at once and especially in order to smoothen out all possible knicks, as the form has about the circumference of a human head, the arrangement is perfect. See photographs. Nearly all women know how to braid the string.

Field Notes: EBORO. Poles ornamented as they have before their houses. They never have two poles alike in any house. Very often there are only two, sometimes four or more. They are nearly always split lengthwise. The poles are cut only with the ADZE, no knife is used for carving any portion of them. Field Notes: Emboussa - a large horn as owned only by important Mangbetu chief. The upper part carved ivory with the typical decorations. In the middle is a piece of the hind limb of the Okapi - highly estimated by the Mangbetu, below a piece from an elephant's ear. The lower part is of hollowed wood. It takes several weeks to slowly chip out splinter by splinter. They start on both sides as the holes are made deepen they mount their chisels on gradually lengthened handles which are hit with roughly made wooden mallets. The piece of wood to be hollowed being fastened by cords to the base of a tree, usually in such a manner (opening inclined slightly downward) that the particles of loose wood may easily be removed. These are practically the only 'heirlooms' that one sees in these regions, as all other objects are destroyed after the death of a chief. The surrender of such a horn means with them absolute submission to their victorious enemies. These horns are extremely rare. I have only seen one of chief Zebandra (near Medje) and it is very seldom that they make now use of it, in fact that hide it. But the natives tell a great many stories about it, for instance they state that there is a piece of skin of a white man sewn below the other hides, that all sorts of amulets of other powerful charms are fastened in its interior, that if Zebandra would go to war they [sic] very sound would confound the enemies etc. They use it of course in great dances, sometimes still to announce the arrival of ...chiefs when visiting the Post. These horns make the reputation of a chief as the natives greatly admire them. Field Notes: A mortar and two pestles. These mortars are chiefly used to (grind) crush the coca leaves and pepper over which they pour hot water. Said to be an acrid sort of beverage that has the wonderful property of inducing some friend of yours to visit you and present some gifts. A whole band is sitting about these mortars each one drawing his liquid into his mouth by a long hollow stick, most of it is given off in a spray in saying his particular wish.
