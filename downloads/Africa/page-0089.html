Field Notes: MUESI. Small disks from snails and water mollusks put on a string and used by women and girls as a necklace and about their waist, sometimes also by men. I have never seen how they make them. But LOGOS tell me that they break up the shells in small pieces, and make a hole into them with arrow (points) which are rolled in the hand. They are strung up and afterwards rubbed upon stones in every direction until they are perfectly round. It seems that they no longer make them as they all prefer the beads, but in former times this was one of the coveted ornaments that every woman was proud of. Some of them carried up to twenty and twenty strings around the waist. There are strings of several women, also one of beads.

Field Notes: Piece of ivory (disk) carried by BAKA women in the perforated upper lip. See photographs. From the woman whose cast and measurements have been taken, see No. 150[?]. The BAKA are a related tribe to the LOGO; their language is different and they live either dependent on chiefs of AZANDE or on the chiefs of LOGO. They carry bows and spears just as the LOGO, and the work in the fields is essentially done by men as in the case with LOGO. And different from the AZANDE where the women are doing most of the agricultural work. The basics of food are the same as in the LOGO. The huts are different as may be seen by the photographs. The thatching of the huts of the BAKA is always (except where they have adopted AZANDE or LOGO huts or structures) reaching to the ground. The songs of the dances are different from either LOGO or AZANDE and also their superstitions. They certainly must grow very old or have a cult of old age as in their villages one sees more old men and women as either with LOGO or AZANDE. The old women carry usually a dish of ivory in the perforated upper lip. They are much ashamed to let the opening of this upper lip be seen without disk, as they at once push up their hand and grab any object to fill it out, though otherwise they are perfectly nude. They also carry a stick, a point of bone, a piece of iron, (often a nail) or brass in the perforated lower lip. Younger women sometimes carry a small disk in the upper lip, as seen in the photograph of the woman whose cast h...as been taken, see Cat. No. 1505. Others also a small point of bone in the lower lip. The BAKA like the LOGO are indigenous and not immigrated like the AZANDE who subdued them.Show More >>

Field Notes: BULAKA = A piece of skin arranged for carrying their children. The skin of the neck of a bull waterbuck or of a buffalo is cut squarely and put into the water to undergo a process of rotting. When it becomes completely soft they clean it with knives, turn over the edges and pin it with four sticks on the ground to dry. Before it gets perfectly dry they take an iron point and following the design marked first with coal on a knife they hit it gradually at intervals without piercing the leather. The whole is well greased with a reddish oily [illeg: paste?]. In order to fix the children upon their back the mothers put the little on their back inclining themselves so that the back nearly is one horizontal plan. With one arm they grasp the two arms of the child pulling them upward. With the other they throw this fixture upon their back reassembling at once the four straps for fastening them. They let the child's free arm make one turn with the straps and standing upright again they turn the straps to a big roundish projection somewhat above the navel. The longer straps go over the shoulder. See photographs. Made by men only, often presented by others to their friends that can make use.
