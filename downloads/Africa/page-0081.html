Field Notes: EMUPETI A sort of medicine worn around the waist and the arms (See photograph). After the MANGBETU kill an OKAPI, they have great fears to die or to get seriously ill. In order to avoid such misfortunes they take a heavy creeper and beat it with a piece of wood (holding it over another smooth piece of wood) until all the fibres are separated. It is then washed out of light gray color. They lay in into the mud to blacken it. They often have dances accompanied by very monotonous songs to propitiate the bad spirits of the OKAPI. The latter expression however is not correct as they do not believe actually in the spirit of a killed OKAPI, but they believe if they will ever step into (or over the place they have lain after years) the excrement of the killed OKAPI, without having performed these ceremonies, misfortunes will never leave them. They also believe that many bad spirits are housing in the forest, but there is evidently no peculiar form or body imagined as they invariably answer on demand to describe spirits, that they are in the trees, leaves, grass on the road in fact n every possible object that might be able to reach them.

The mothers I asked told me if the child strampels [sic] much and becomes restless the bandage is taken off. The children very often sleep with it, the bandage is seldom left longer than 48 hours. The child's head is always well oiled before putting the bandage on also after taking it off, usually the bandage is left off a day. It is not scare [sic] that sores are produced if they become of a more serious nature the bandage is left off entirely also in all cases of illness. So far as may be judged from the adult persons who enjoy the privilege of such a deformed head, it does not influence the mental abilities in any way, the children however who undergo this treatment have a very peculiar staring look which disappears very rapidly if the bandage is taken off, though as a matter of fact they cannot feel molested by it as they never touch or scratch it, never try to remove the bandage. They always stated that it was very pleasing to have such a long head, there is evidently no tradition about its actual introduction, it is certainly admired by both sexes. Field Notes: NADO. Fish nets. In the dry season the women very often erect dams across the numerous brooks by pushing sticks into the mud and piling old pieces of wood, leaves and mud against it. The brooks are very low then often only a few inches high and seldom exceed 10 or 15 yards in width but the women usually select the smallest brooks in the forest seldom more than 6 yards wide at the utmost. When the dam is finished, and the water has runoff or lowered on the other side a whole troupe of women and girls proceed along the overhanging low shores and work them over also the paddles are to catch the small fish crabs, shrimps, frogs, toads, all delightful morsels to the MEDJE. Mussels and watersnails are also gathered at this occasion.

Field Notes: NAKALU. A trap to catch genets and large squirrels also monkeys (any animal that lives upon trees and feeds on certain fruits). The natives find out by observation where the animals habitually pass and fasten this trap simply on their passageway without bait. Also used on fruit bearing trees visited by monkeys. The trap is fastened horizontally upon one of the branches so that the passing animal has to pass through the opening left. The long braided fiber string (raphia palm fibers) is firmly attached to a branch that forms the spring (if not there the natives fasten one). The small pointed stick is put between the fork of the black stick, the pointed end of the small stick forms releases that has to go in the small forked pieces which is pushed across the opening. The loop is then carefully arranged in the opening.

Field Notes: Egiwamasi - fish traps - No. 367 is the ordinary model, several other models are employed. The fishing by means of these baskets is done by women only; small fish, crabs and shrimps are caught. Three or more women generally set out together each one with a bundle of 10 or 20 of these little traps on their back. As bait they termites - they break the nests in small pieces that are dropped into the baskets as the traps are put into water. The termites gradually move out of their tunnels. Caterpillars that are previously crushed are also used as bait. The women are very experienced in this work and some enjoy a great reputation. The traps are laid under the overhanging shores between the roots in such manner that they are held fast. The fish taken are very small, seldom exceeding 5 inches, but most of them are only 2 or 3 inches long. The fist are always cooked as staple food over a slow fire. Only small quantities are eaten at principal meals. They are boiled in water, seasoned with oil, pepper and salt. The traps are made by women or girls.

Field Notes: Agworro - rat traps. The Mangbetu and neighboring tribes are very fond of meat and arrange for large hunting parties to take the field rats (house rats are considered to cause illness and are not eaten, though they are very common and annoy them by their digging in the huts). They first select a certain place such as a plantation or other open site near a village, some experienced young men or boys arrange or rather lay out the traps in the runways or near the holes. The boys particularly mark each one alternating to his traps. When all the traps are set, they give a sugar and a yelling crowd passes over the whole place. About 3:00 in the afternoon is judged to be the best time. The rats being afraid run towards the holes and land in very many cases in one of these woven funnels. The waiting boy pounces upon it and kills it and binds it firmly to a string, the trap is set out again and surprisingly often they catch several. The Mangbetu simply make an incision in the abdomen, press out the bowels, singe off the hair and spike it upon a pounded stick, which is set up close to the fire. A few turns and a few minutes and the rat has disappeared, they only take out the sharp incisors as they consider them dangerous. Both sexes partake of these meals.

Field Notes: Nesanguru - fibers woven into a sort of trough used as a sieve or filter in the process of making arrow poison. Several kinds of roots are used, such as No. 366 [Some of the roots used to make the arrow poison]. They generally crush these roots (after cutting them into small pieces) in square holes used as mortar. I have seen these holes in fallen tress near the villages and in the forest close to the roads. They crush the roots with pepper and lay the mass into these Nesanguru. They close it firmly by winding a creeper around. The two ends of the Nesanguru are bound together, a long and a short stick are passed between the folded portions and two persons sitting opposite each other hold the long stick firmly between their knees. The short lower stick is then turned and the twisted mass pressed out. The poison is liquid and caught in a calabash or a folded leaf. To poison the arrows they dip it into this liquid and hold it over the fire, as soon as it is evaporated, the process is repeated over and over again until there is a pitch-like mass that covers well the arrow and [illeg: still?] portion of its shaft.

Field Notes: NAQUORRO. A cylindrical tube made of sticks. Trap for the CRICETOMYS. It is dug with one end in the ground so as to coincide with the exit of the home of CRICETOMYS and held fast by a few flexible sticks that are bent over it and pushed on either side into the ground. One stick forming the spring is pushed firmly into the ground about 1 1/2 feet in front of each so that all three nooses may be fastened to it. Only the one at the exit is actually set, the other two nooses are simply fully opened so the rat may pass unhampered through, then as the exit is barred by a few other sticks and the fiber that acts as release so the rat is caught on the shoulders as well as on the belly by the two nooses that enter in action as soon as the fiber is [illeg]. The narrowness of the cylindrical tube makes it impossible for the rat to turn back and cut off the fibers which strangle it gradually. The CRICETOMYS is a favorite food of all natives in the region. Field Notes: Bird or animal trap. 2 sticks, the thickness of a thumb or thinner, about 50 cm long, are split at one end which is driven into the ground in such manner as to form a fork. They should be placed opposite each other at a distance of 10 to 15 cm. Another stick that may be easily bent (about 1 cm. in diameter), about 70 cm. long, is pointed at both sides. The two points are pressed into the ground at a distance of about 20 cm. From each other, right behind one of the forked sticks, at the right or left. It must be done so the highest point of the halfcircle will be still below the slit of the split sticks - which should be about in the middle, right behind the halfcircle. It is put with one end against the small stick placed at one side of the forks at 20 cm.; about 1/2 cm. back where this stick meets the halfcircle, a groove is cut across into the upper surface, to receive later the small piece of wood that holds the string down. Field Notes: Agworro - rat traps. The Mangbetu and neighboring tribes are very fond of meat and arrange for large hunting parties to take the field rats (house rats are considered to cause illness and are not eaten, though they are very common and annoy them by their digging in the huts). They first select a certain place such as a plantation or other open site near a village, some experienced young men or boys arrange or rather lay out the traps in the runways or near the holes. The boys particularly mark each one alternating to his traps. When all the traps are set, they give a sugar and a yelling crowd passes over the whole place. About 3:00 in the afternoon is judged to be the best time. The rats being afraid run towards the holes and land in very many cases in one of these woven funnels. The waiting boy pounces upon it and kills it and binds it firmly to a string, the trap is set out again and surprisingly often they catch several. The Mangbetu simply make an incision in the abdomen, press out the bowels, singe off the hair and spike it upon a pounded stick, which is set up close to the fire. A few turns and a few minutes and the rat has disappeared, they only take out the sharp incisors as they consider them dangerous. Both sexes partake of these meals.

Field Notes: Nagwa - the most common instrument of fortune telling whenever they go on a voyage or set out for a visit or in any halfway important case the medicine man or in most cases the owner (most men own one) consults his Nagwa. They take it then in their left hand and turn and turn the round piece of wood preserving it in its sheath, continually asking or commanding to tell the truth. Field Notes: NECKALACKBA. The stool or bench for the men. If the men go on visits, or social gatherings they are usually accompanied by several of their women. One is usually carrying the NECKALACKBA. They are made of split leaves and stalk of the raffia palm, the decoration in black is produced by winding around the single pieces a long strip of a creeper and leaving open the places that are afterwards held over the fire and slightly charred. The grooves and other decorations are made by hot irons and polished carefully off.

Field Notes: Nobarra - the famous stools of the Mangbetu women carved of one single piece of wood. They are made by special artists who enjoy a wide reputation, they sign their work on the lower end by a mark deeply cut into the wood and rubbed usually with redwood powder which is an expression of good wishes for future [illeg: omens?]. These stools accompany their owners (women only) on their visits and voyages and wherever they go. The strap fastened through the hole (in the rear) is laid over the forehead the stool hanging down the back the seat against the skin. Sometimes also the strap is laid upon the right or left shoulder, the stool hanging down the side. Important chiefs women have a special women to carry the stool behind them. They are surprisingly light.

Field Notes: EKANGALA formed pieces of wood commonly used by the MANGBETU to take a leisurely position when sitting on their NECKALACKBA(they are used by men only, though I have seen a woman using one). The men lean backward and very often place their arms back of one or hook arms back of the lateral upper branchings. Used as a back of a chair.
