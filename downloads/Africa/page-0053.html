Field Notes: "Salt leaching cups. Lokele. Yakusu. .40" (643). Upon purchasing a cake of salt from the Lokele on 24 September 1906, Starr comments, "... apparently they make salt here as well for they make a little perforated bottomed bowl cup, which is apparently a filter or leacher: the brine falling into the lower cups" (664). On 26 September 1906 he purchases some of these cups and tests them out: "... I took a ramble in Yakusu. Made a run on the little salt vessels. The burned plant material is put into a little conical vessel with perforated bottom, which rests in a little bowl cup of proper size. Water is poured on to leach. The brine is caught in the cup and no doubt evaporated in it. These are usually made in couple, matching in size, material and decoration and are rather nice" (667-668).

Field Notes: "Pottery cone support - for pot making. Lokele. Yakusu" (642). 24 September 1906. "Pottery is in full swing here and the outfit and types present much new to me. The clay is fine grained gray in color: it is pounded up in th end of an old canoe with a long handled pounder far more slender at the lower end than Ikoko's. Sand is used to temper the paste. The mass to be shaped is thrown upon a board or block in which a shallow, neat, rounded hollow exists. When neatly balled it is struck from above by a hearty pestle-shaped pounder and then hollowed into a rather thin walled form. This is then worked into shape by beating from outside with a little broad potter's paddle upon an inside support of pottery [drawing of support] neatly made [object No. 3354]. In final shaping and sun drying the vessel is supported in a broken pot base. The neck and rim of cooking pots are decorated with cordline and check surfaces, which are produced by impressing with (a) notched edged seed or (b) a slender bit of stick, pitted and cut which is rolled to produce a surface of decoration. To prevent adhesion these are used on spaced rubbed with palmoil" (663).

Field Notes: "Fetich: leaf bunch (d) Ikoko" [part of group of fetishes that belonged to Chief Ebobolo] (509). 8 July 1906. While purchasing objects from Mr. Clark: "Among the stuff today was a lot of five fetiches that belonged to the old chief--Ebobolo--'the wolf', who died some months ago and for whom a red flag still hangs out along the Lake road above the Station. He was one of the most cruel and brutal men in Mr. Clark's experience. After his death, the heirs quarreled over his two drums &, curiously, over his fetiches; to end the palaver Mr. Clark bought both and all are now mine. They--the fetiches--are indeed mere rubbish, having been so treated, but are interesting as a chief's full fetich outfit" (513).

Field Notes: "Pottery shaping block. Lokele. Yakusu. .81" (644). On 24 September 1906, Starr notes: "Pottery is in full swing here and the outfit and types present much new to me. The clay is fine grained gray in color: it is pounded up in th end of an old canoe with a long handled pounder far more slender at the lower end than Ikoko's. Sand is used to temper the paste. The mass to be shaped is thrown upon a board or block in which a shallow, neat, rounded hollow exists. When neatly balled it is struck from above by a hearty pestle-shaped pounder and then hollowed into a rather thin walled form. This is then worked into shape by beating from outside with a little broad potter's paddle upon an inside support of pottery [drawing of support] neatly made. In final shaping and sun drying the vessel is supported in a broken pot base [possibly object No. 3424?]. The neck and rim of cooking pots are decorated with cordline and check surfaces, which are produced by impressing with (a) notched edged seed or (b) a slender bit of stick, pitted and cut which is rolled to produce a surface of decoration. To prevent adhesion these are used on spaced rubbed with palmoil" (663). On 27 September 1906, Starr briefly mentions buying "potblocks" (676).
