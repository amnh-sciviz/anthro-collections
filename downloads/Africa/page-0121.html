Curatorial Notes: This shield was collected from the Maasai in Kenya. It is oval shaped, made of buffalo hide and painted with ochres. The hide has been lashed to a wooden frame. There is also a wooden reinforcement on the verso side of the shield and a wooden handgrip. Maasai warriors are part of the moran. In this case, it is important to know that each group of moran’s ornaments differs slightly from the next. This is because each group of Maasai warriors has gone through the required circumcision ritual together and lives in a community separate from their families. The patterns on a Maasai warrior’s shield identify with his specific age-group in the moran, and can also symbolize a certain district as well.

Maasai warriors are known as part of the moran. The Maasai travel through subsequent stages in life based on both age and accomplishments. In pastoralist Maasai society, the moran is a specific age-set in between uncircumcised boys and junior elders. Maasai men are initiated into the moran between the ages of twelve and eighteen, depending on their age at the point of the four or five year cycle (Hodgson 1999: 126). Maasai warriors herd cattle together, drink milk together, and sleep in homesteads together. This lasts until the warriors participate in the eunoto rite of passage and become senior warriors and eventually junior elders. It is at this point that they must marry and give up their elaborate dress and liberal lifestyle. The constant community with other men as a warrior diminishes as the new elder establishes his own homestead, individuality, and political power.

Maasai warriors would use this shield for hunting and warfare purposes. Tourists would often associate the Maasai shield with Maasai lion hunting and violent raids. Historically, Maasai warriors would hunt and kill a lion in order to prove their worthiness to society.

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist cultural group who live in both Kenya and Tanzania along the Rift Valley. Historically, the style and form has changed throughout time, however the symbolism and social context is relatively constant. The moran warriors are young Maasai men responsible for herding, protecting, and capturing cattle. They use their spears in throwing competitions to show off their prowess. The spear is used for decorative purposes as well; it is part of the warrior’s daily dress. It is iconic of the Maasai because of their violent reputation for raids and ceremonial lion kills. Spears made for sale in the tourism industry are never used or functional, and are conveniently adjustable to fit inside a traveler’s suitcase.

The spear can have multiple meanings depending on the age-grade. Brand new spears are considered appropriate for Maasai warriors. As they begin to become worn and aged however, the spears are given to elders and young boys. When they become even more worn out, the spears are given to younger boys and older elders. A Maasai carrying an aged spear symbolizes his non-warrior status. Each age-grade has its own stylistic preferences; therefore the style of spear is reminiscent of a specific group of warriors. They also adopt innovations from other ethnic groups.

The spears are made out of wood or metal often with an attached ball of fur, in this case, lion skin is attached at the base. The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasai’s animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemy’s cultural boundary unharmed.

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist culture who live in the grasslands of modern-day Kenya and Tanzania. The moran is a specific age-set in between uncircumcised boys and junior elders in Maasai society. Maasai men are initiated into the moran between the ages of twelve and eighteen, depending on their age at the point of the four or five year cycle (Hodgson 1999: 126). Maasai warriors herd cattle together, drink milk together, and sleep in homesteads together. The moran do not cut their hair. Instead, they wear it in a single braid that is treated with fat and red ochre (Galaty 1983: 369). This lasts until the warriors participate in the eunoto rite of passage and become senior warriors. Once all ornaments worn previously have been removed, they transition into junior elders. It is at this point that they must marry and give up their elaborate dress and arguably privileged lifestyle. The required constant community with other men as a warrior diminishes as the new elder establishes his own homestead, individuality, and political power.

A Maasai warrior wears this headdress during special ceremonies, such as the eunoto. This is considered the most significant compilation of events for Maasai warriors. The entire ceremony can last several months and includes a series of promotions. At the end of the ceremony, the warriors’ mothers cut their sons’ hair and shave their heads. This marks their entry into senior warriorhood (Galaty 1983: 369)

This type of headdress is called olowuaru, and is made from a lion’s mane and also includes a beaded strap made of red, green, yellow, and white beads. (Klumpp 1987: 211). The lion has deep cultural meaning for the Maasai. Historically, Maasai warriors had a reputation for ritually killing a lion along with one or two witnesses in order to prove his manhood. The moran participated in other types of competing events, such as wrestling and spear throwing, which enhanced prestige on an individual and group level (Hodgson 1999: 126).

Curatorial Notes: This is container made from a gourd that holds the medicine used by the iloibonok, or witch-doctors and diviners, of the Maasai. The first oloiboni in oral history was in the 17th century, in which a man who could foresee the future and work miracles was adopted into tribal life. Because of their clairvoyance and mastery of ritual medicine, the iloibonok are consulted for both spiritual and physical ailments. Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist group who occupy the grassland region of the Rift Valley in Kenya and Tanzania. This necklace, from the Maasai in Kenya, is used by medicine men. It once contained medicines in the attached horns, according to museum archives. Miscellaneous bones and shells are placed at the outer circumference of the necklace. White beads also border the necklace, which is common in most beaded neckpieces.

Maasai medicine men, called iloibonok, most likely wore this type of necklace. They were known to have a divine relationship with Enkai, the Sky God and the Maasai’s main god. Enkai was thought to bestow magical powers through certain objects that the iloibonok possessed in order to provide spiritual aid. Maasai warriors would trade cattle and other goods for charms. The iloibonok could provide assistance to the sick well as predict the warriors’ upcoming raid. However, many Maasai feared the iloibonok was a type of malevolent spirit. British colonialism and Catholic missionaries challenged the authenticity of the iloibonok and gradually, their role in society has diminished (Hodgson 2005: 242).

Curatorial Notes: Both the Maasai and Kikuyu have experienced cultural diffusion from intermarriage, frequent raiding, and instable trade relations throughout time. The Kikuyu live relatively close to Maasai territory, however their agriculturalist livelihood is completely opposite of Maasai nomadic pastoralism. They reside in the highlands and live off a typical agriculturalist diet of maize, millet, sweet potatoes, etc. There is a Maasai variation of this leglet called entuala enkelu, which is a leg ornament worn by warriors while dancing.

The bell or leglet is worn on the warrior’s thigh, specifically the right leg, while dancing during specific occasions, such as circumcision rites. It is usually worn with other ornaments such as armlets and other leglets. The bell is made from iron, which is attached to a leather strap adorned with cowrie shells and outlined by glass beads. Scholars suggest that some leglets were made from recycled leather ear ornaments previously worn by woman. Women could offer warriors these ornaments or they could be transformed into a leglet to sell at a higher price for the tourist market (Klumpp 1994: 17).

Curatorial Notes: Married Maasai women wear these elaborate ear ornaments to celebrate their sons’ circumcision. The spiral pendant indicates a woman’s status of having a circumcised son. The beaded leather flap is called, inkonita oonkiyiaa and the brass pendant is called, isurutia in the Maa language (Klumpp 1987: 96). Circumcision initiation is an extremely important stage in a man’s life. Therefore, it provides prestige to his mother by association.

Women are responsible for creating all ornaments for themselves and for the men of their group. They may create ornaments for special occasions such as circumcision rites, marriages, or other rites of passage. Women often create art communally within their homestead borders. They may also make ornaments to sell to tourists.

Maasai women use alternating complementary and contrasting colors. Color blocking rules creates a vibrating composition. They are broken down into strong and weak. Blue, black, and red are considered strong colors and yellow and green are considered weak colors. Two strong colors can be next to each other, but two weak cannot. Green can be considered strong if next to yellow. Black and more commonly white are used as outlines. Maasai women take great care to follow these rules in order to create art that is considered aesthetically pleasing. Each ornament must contrast with other ornaments that he or she is wearing as well. Maasai ideologies including, duality, balance, and equality are illustrated in beaded art. Enkai, the sky god, has two forms (day and night). Male and female gender roles, although different, together allow society to function as a whole. They complement each other, just as the colors do in a beaded necklace. The color blocking creates dividers, which reflects the Maasai ideas on continuity. This is said to be left for Enkai and so, there must be breaks in society such as where grass ends and water begins, pouring milk in separate segments, etc.

Curatorial Notes: The display above is a cache of ostrich eggshell flasks. These eggshells were most likely utilized as water containers by the San, a traditional hunting and gathering people, who live throughout the Kalahari plain in Southern Africa. Archaeological evidence indicates that ostrich eggshells have been used as containers by the ancestral San for some 15,000 years. Flasks were also beneficial in storing items such as ant larvae (sometimes called "Bushman Rice"), shell fragments for making beads, and ground red ochre and powdered specularite for ritual and cosmetic purposes. In addition, eggshell flasks have functioned as drinking vessels as signified by spout-like features created over their apertures.

Containers were protected from the sun and other outside elements by a seal of grass/scrub cover like those exhibited, mastic (a gum-like plant resin) or beeswax. Oftentimes these fragile holders were buried in the sand for storage and safekeeping, and caches of multiple flasks have been discovered such as those shown here. In San social tradition, a member would not expect to have exclusive ownership or use of an eggshell flask and its contents. Instead, these objects were widely shared within a customary hxaro exchange, a network of gift-giving that established and maintained societal relations and partnerships among distant groups.

Researcher: Siu Ying Ng, 2013

Henderson, Zoë. 2002. “A Dated Cache of Ostrich-Eggshell Flasks From Thomas’ Farm, Northern Cape Province, South Africa,” South African Archaeological Bulletin, 57 (175):38-40.

Curatorial Notes: The display above is a cache of ostrich eggshell flasks. These eggshells were most likely utilized as water containers by the San, a traditional hunting and gathering people, who live throughout the Kalahari plain in Southern Africa. Archaeological evidence indicates that ostrich eggshells have been used as containers by the ancestral San for some 15,000 years. Flasks were also beneficial in storing items such as ant larvae (sometimes called "Bushman Rice"), shell fragments for making beads, and ground red ochre and powdered specularite for ritual and cosmetic purposes. In addition, eggshell flasks have functioned as drinking vessels as signified by spout-like features created over their apertures.

Containers were protected from the sun and other outside elements by a seal of grass/scrub cover like those exhibited, mastic (a gum-like plant resin) or beeswax. Oftentimes these fragile holders were buried in the sand for storage and safekeeping, and caches of multiple flasks have been discovered such as those shown here. In San social tradition, a member would not expect to have exclusive ownership or use of an eggshell flask and its contents. Instead, these objects were widely shared within a customary hxaro exchange, a network of gift-giving that established and maintained societal relations and partnerships among distant groups.

Researcher: Siu Ying Ng, 2013
