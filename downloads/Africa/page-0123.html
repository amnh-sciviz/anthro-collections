Curatorial Notes: This object comes from an unspecified region of Liberia, but is possibly attributable to the Malinke people. Amulets of this type are associated with itinerant Muslim clerics throughout West Africa. There may be several types of text concealed in these charms, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk (Hassan 1990).

Not only does this charm shed light onto the religious beliefs of its creators, but it also provides insight into the manner in which Islam diffused into a specific region of Western Africa. Liberia, an area that once belonged to the Mali Empire, is a unique place for a number of reasons (Olukoju 2006). Unlike the rest of West Africa, the territory that is now Liberia was never colonized. It became a nation state not from rejection of European colonial ambition, but through the efforts of an American philanthropic group that aimed to establish a homeland for freed slaves (Moran 2006). Also unique for its location, the people in and around Liberia only relatively recently started adopting Islam and Muslim practices (Olukoju).

During the late nineteenth century, Malinke traders migrated into the area and, not only conducted their business, but also simultaneously “propagated Islam” (Olukoju). The merchants’ commercial success attracted local populations to Islam as did its ability to incorporate indigenous beliefs. For example, the religion’s acceptance of “polygyny, slavery, divination, ancestral veneration, and sacrifice” (Olukoju), often encouraged communities to adopt Islam. Furthermore, the religion also provided structure for groups whose customary forms of authority had been compromised by historical and political developments. Specifically, the establishment of a modern nation and new values, such as the abolishment of slavery, damaged the Vai’s traditional social structure; this then forced them to seek new alternatives which led them towards Islam. In contrast, the Gbandi resisted the religion due to their “relatively highly centralized social and political systems, underpinned by a resilient culture” (Olukoju). Merchants, however, only played a partial role in the dispersal of Islam. Muslim clerics further entrenched the religion by providing “spiritual guidance for local leaders” and encouraging the use of amulets and charms such as this one (Olukoju). Ultimately, this charm illustrates how the entrenchment of Islam in Liberia occurred due the interplay of a number of factors involving indigenous belief system, social structures, and political and historical trajectories.

PRAYER BOARD
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 90.1/ 9272
Culture: ARAB (ARABIC)
Country: LIBERIA
Material: WOOD, PIGMENT, CORD
Dimensions: L:68.2 W:29.3 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1950 [GIFT]
Donor: SAUNDERS
Keywords: PRAYER BOARD
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout Islamic Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general. Though it was acquired from Liberia, the exact origins of this board are unclear. Allo are known to be utilized extensively by the Hausa people of West Africa. The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups on the continent (Hassan 1990), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Among the Hausa, the clerics who use this technology are called Malams, from Arabic mu’alim “scholar.” Malams oversee the Muslim education of a Hausa community. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of Islamic education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. This still continues, despite the lasting cultural impact of the Sokoto Caliphate, with it’s campaign for universal Islamic education, including women. The Malam does not expect regular compensation for their teaching, and regards what they do as a service. However, gifts of unfixed value are traditionally given in the middle of the week by children on behalf of grateful parents. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).
