Curatorial Notes: Exhibited above is an ostrich eggshell flask in a carrying sling. This flask was most likely utilized as a water container by the San, a hunting and gathering group who live throughout the Kalahari plain in Southern Africa. Archaeological evidence indicates that ostrich eggshells have been used as containers by the ancestral San for some 15,000 years. Flasks were also beneficial in storing items such as ant larvae (sometimes called "Bushman Rice"), shell fragments for making beads, and ground red ochre and powdered specularite for ritual and cosmetic purposes. In addition, eggshell holders have functioned as drinking vessels as signified by spout-like features created over their apertures.

Containers were protected from the sun and other outside elements by a seal of grass/scrub cover, mastic (a gum-like plant resin) or beeswax. Oftentimes these fragile holders were buried in the sand for storage and safekeeping, and caches of multiple flasks have been discovered (such as the one displayed in #90.1/8942 A-H). In San social tradition, a member would not expect to have exclusive ownership or use of an eggshell flask and its contents. Instead, these objects were widely shared within a customary hxaro exchange, a network of gift-giving that established and maintained societal relations and partnerships among distant groups.

Curatorial Notes: Both the Maasai and Kikuyu have experienced cultural diffusion from intermarriage, frequent raiding, and instable trade relations throughout time. The Kikuyu live relatively close to Maasai territory, however their agriculturalist livelihood is completely opposite of Maasai nomadic pastoralism. They reside in the highlands and live off a typical agriculturalist diet of maize, millet, sweet potatoes, etc. There is a Maasai variation of this leglet called entuala enkelu, which is a leg ornament worn by warriors while dancing.

The bell or leglet is worn on the warrior’s thigh, specifically the right leg, while dancing during specific occasions, such as circumcision rites. It is usually worn with other ornaments such as armlets and other leglets. The bell is made from iron, which is attached to a leather strap adorned with cowrie shells and outlined by glass beads. Scholars suggest that some leglets were made from recycled leather ear ornaments previously worn by woman. Women could offer warriors these ornaments or they could be transformed into a leglet to sell at a higher price for the tourist market (Klumpp 1994: 17).

Curatorial Notes: This hide-bound Qur’an (older spelling "Koran") and gafaka bag (Hassan 1990) were part of the earliest systematic collection purchased by the Museum directly from a collector on the continent. It was obtained from a trader named Richard Douglas on whom little has been written. Although most of the objects obtained from Douglas relate to the Barotse people of south Africa (“African Ethnographic Collection”), this text comes from the Hausa center of Kano in northern Nigeria. The handwritten copying of books, even those not considered scripture, is still said to be a pious act for highly trained scholars, and the resulting manuscripts are prized by the Hausa (Hassan).

The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

The earliest spread of Islam in Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco (Greenberg). The Muslim cultures of the Maghreb (Northwestern Africa above the Sahara) have exerted a great deal of influence ever since. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. The religion was mostly restricted to urban areas, among a literate elite which preserved older styles of written Arabic. (Greenberg, Hassan).
