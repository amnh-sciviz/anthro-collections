Curatorial Notes: The Yoruba peoples are located in southwestern Nigeria and southern Benin and are one of the largest ethnic groups in West Africa They were not a unified political state, but rather bands of interconnected independent city-states sharing a common language and culture.

Yoruba epistemology holds that the Supreme Being created heaven, earth and its inhabitants, and divinities and spirits known as Orisa, Imole, or Ebora. These divinities and spirits help the Supreme Being in overseeing and governing the world (Awolalu 1979: 20). Yoruba iron bells are usually employed in religious ceremonies. Iron bells were the primary instrument of Obatalata, the divinity associated with creation. Obatala shapes the child within its mother’s womb. He is commonly worshipped as a male although there are oral traditions that identify him as a woman or androgynously (Awolalu 1979: 22). Obatala is popular amongst women because of his ability to give children to infertile women (King 1961: 5).

A young virgin or a woman past childbearing age is tasked each morning with replenishing the water within Obatala’ sanctuary. This woman carries a bell that she rings on her way to the sanctuary in order to notify surrounding people that she is undertaking a holy task. The sacred water is then ceremoniously distributed to worshippers, and it is this water that aides in fertility.

Curatorial Notes: The Yoruba peoples are located in southwestern Nigeria and southern Benin and are one of the largest ethnic groups in West Africa They were not a unified political state, but rather bands of interconnected independent city-states sharing a common language and culture.

Yoruba epistemology holds that the Supreme Being created heaven, earth and its inhabitants, and divinities and spirits known as Orisa, Imole, or Ebora. These divinities and spirits help the Supreme Being in overseeing and governing the world (Awolalu 1979: 20). Yoruba iron bells are usually employed in religious ceremonies. Iron bells were the primary instrument of Obatalata, the divinity associated with creation. Obatala shapes the child within its mother’s womb. He is commonly worshipped as a male although there are oral traditions that identify him as a woman or androgynously (Awolalu 1979: 22). Obatala is popular amongst women because of his ability to give children to infertile women (King 1961: 5).

A young virgin or a woman past childbearing age is tasked each morning with replenishing the water within Obatala’ sanctuary. This woman carries a bell that she rings on her way to the sanctuary in order to notify surrounding people that she is undertaking a holy task. The sacred water is then ceremoniously distributed to worshippers, and it is this water that aides in fertility.
