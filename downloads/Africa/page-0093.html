BUNDLE
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 90.1/ 3765
Locale: RUNGU
Country: CONGO (BELGIAN CONGO)
Material: WOOD, PLANT FIBER
Dimensions: L:32.5 W:7 H:6 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1915 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: LANG, HERBERT
Keywords: BUNDLE
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Field Notes: A bundle representing the various gifts presented by a man for his wife to her parents. Though a woman is considered the property of a man the custom has it that they are not bought outright as generally believed by white men, but the husband is obliged not only to make continued presents throughout the life of his wife, but certain events, such as birth, illness of her as well as on many other occasions he has to make small donations, such as knives, spears, and chickens. Often the woman escapes if the husband does not follow this often onerous custom and he has to ransom her by new gifts. It is rather scarce that women rest very long with her husband. The running away is a rather common feature what gives rise to continued palavers.
Exhibition History
• AFRICAN REFLECTIONS: ART FROM NORTHEASTERN ZAIRE. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. June 1990 - February 1994
Exhibition Label: Wooden sticks bound with grass, used as a method of counting the various gifts given by a man to his wife's parents. Gifts are given at the time of marriage, after the birth of a child, and at times of sickness. If a wife leaves, the husband would be expected to give new gifts to win her back.

Donor: LANG, HERBERT
Keywords: CARRIER (CHILD)
Category: EQUIPMENT
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Field Notes: BULAKA = A piece of skin arranged for carrying their children. The skin of the neck of a bull waterbuck or of a buffalo is cut squarely and put into the water to undergo a process of rotting. When it becomes completely soft they clean it with knives, turn over the edges and pin it with four sticks on the ground to dry. Before it gets perfectly dry they take an iron point and following the design marked first with coal on a knife they hit it gradually at intervals without piercing the leather. The whole is well greased with a reddish oily [illeg: paste?]. In order to fix the children upon their back the mothers put the little on their back inclining themselves so that the back nearly is one horizontal plan. With one arm they grasp the two arms of the child pulling them upward. With the other they throw this fixture upon their back reassembling at once the four straps for fastening them. They let the child's free arm make one turn with the straps and standing upright again they turn the straps to a big roundish projection somewhat above the navel. The longer straps go over the shoulder. See photographs. Made by men only, often presented by others to their friends that can make use.
