Curatorial Notes: The Bambara of Mali are Mande-speaking people who live on the Bani and Upper Niger Rivers who are predominantly agrarian. The Bambara people credit Ci-Wara, a spiritual being, with teaching them agriculture; it is under Ci-Waras guidance, they say, that they became successful and able farmers.

This antelope headdress is worn by Bambara male dancers, Ci-Wara (translated to farm animals). It is often worn in pairs of one male and female antelope. The antelope symbolizes the farmer who tills his fields tirelessly (Siegmann 2009: 62). Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good
job (Phillips 1995: 502).

The headdress exemplifies the Bambara peoples devotion to agricultural cultivation and fertility. The organization of the Ci-wara performance is based on the Bamana respect for the power and efficiency of the union of male and female. As human reproduction is the result of the sexual union between man and woman, so agricultural fertility is attributed to the union between fire (the sun), an expression of the male principle, and earth and water, an expression of the female principle. (Colleyn 2001: 204).

The horns of the antelope represent ears of millet and the zigzag design of the mane represents the sun. The costume worn by the performers consists of raffia fibers that extend from the base of the headdress to the ground and represent the rain needed for cultivation.The female antelope has a child on its back depicting the way mothers carry their children, but also calling attention to animal, plant, and human fertility (Siegmann 2009: 62). The headdresses hold a parallel between the penetration of the earth through farming and copulation (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

The headdress is associated with different purposes, however, as one observer noted, like all other dances of the Bamana that of [Ci-Wara] was never static .the development and change took place somewhat independently in each region and indeed in each village (Lagamma 2002: 71). Likewise, each performance is unique and no one description will be able to convey the many tribal traditions.

It is used during agricultural competitions. During these competitions young farmers are galvanized by songs, music, and the masked dances. They seek to imitate the mythical animals that originally taught humans agriculture. During the competition the men work through a number of rows in a field and the first to completion is the winner and declared the Ci-Wara. The title brings much praise to the winner. Men are constantly invited to take up the contest, just as their ancestors had done before, and see how they measure up to the heroes of the past.

It is also used during entertainment and ritual events. They appeared during festivals at the Sgou court. After colonial conquest they were also a part of the July 14th Bastille celebrations, French National Day.

Third, it is used during an annual two day celebration that takes place between November and April, according to the moons appearance. This celebration includes sacrifices and the remaking of cult objects. It also includes a parade through the village with stops
a spots where the union of the male and female masks are symbolized (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

Curatorial Notes: The bracelet is from Mauritania, a nation carved out from the western reaches of the Sahara by the French during the 20th century. As a modern state, Mauritania encompasses numerous ethnic groups. Moors constitute the majority of the population and either descend strictly from Berbers and Arabs or include Sudanic African influence. Ethnic minorities, numbering at about a 1/3 of the population, include other groups such as the Fulani, Tukulor, Soninke, and Wolof. Despite the diversity of Mauritanias ethnic makeup, many of the groups, specifically the Moors, Tukulor and Soninke, follow similar cultural practices and uphold similar societal structures

The relative homogeneity of social practice in Mauritania can be linked back to a history of trade and social upheaval which allowed for cultural exchange and dissemination. The first group to occupy the region were the Berbers who moved southwards in search of new pasture lands and trade routes to control; through their endeavors they displaced the original inhabitants of the area, the Bafour peoples. Not too much is known about these indigenous peoples but evidence suggests that they were a pastoral proto-Berber group that migrated to the area during the Neolithic, when Mauritania had not yet succumbed to processes of desertification. The Berbers control over the region, however, did not last indefinitely. Africans from the Mali, Ghana and Songhai empires respectively wrested power from the Berbers and ruled the region until they in turn were overthrown and displaced to the South and East by Arab invaders during the 16th century. Many of those peoples from West Africa that remained become part of a subordinated known as the haratine that remains to this day[1]

The entrenchment of Islam in Mauritania occurred through the migration of Muslim traders during the 10th century[2]. Islam in West Africa generally pursued the paths of least resistance, dissemination of the faith being marked by slow and steady advances. Even in cases where the religion was forcibly imposed upon indigenous inhabitants it is important to note that of all the avenues of expansions, only this one was sporadic and short-lived[3].

As a result of Islams dispersal across the region and its central role in mediating cultural life in Mauritania, the religions influence can be seen in the design of the object. Specifically, the bracelets silver surface, composed of six bands, is covered in geometric patterns. This aesthetic choice, emphasizing abstraction, reflect Islams prohibition against the depiction of figurative forms, such as people or animals, due to the fact that the creation of living forms is unique to God [4] Furthermore, the use of nonrepresentational forms also emphasizes the concept of cultural syncretism in that indigenous West African art also traditionally utilized abstraction in its pieces. Therefore, this bracelet illustrates how Islam not only brought new cultural mores to West Africa but simultaneously reinforced older values as well[5].

Curatorial Notes: This dagger comes from Mauritania, a nation carved out from the western reaches of the Sahara by the French during the 20th century. As a modern state, Mauritania encompasses numerous ethnic groups. Moors constitute the majority of the population and either descend strictly from Berbers and Arabs or are a Sudanic African mix. Ethnic minorities, numbering at about a 1/3 of the population, include other groups such as the Fulani, Tukulor, Soninke, and Wolof. Despite the diversity of Mauritanias ethnic makeup, many of the groups, specifically the Moors, Tukulor and Soninke, follow similar cultural practices and uphold similar societal structures

The relative homogeneity of social practice in Mauritania can be linked back to a history of trade and social upheaval which allowed for cultural exchange and dissemination. The first occupiers of the region were the Berbers who moved southwards in search of new pasture lands and trade routes to control; through their endeavors they displaced the original inhabitants of the area, the Bafour peoples. Not too much is known about these indigenous peoples but evidence suggests that they were a pastoral proto-Berber group that migrated to the area during the Neolithic, when Mauritania had not yet succumbed to processes of desertification. The Berbers dominance over the region, however, did not last indefinitely. Black Africans from the Mali, Ghana and Songhai empires respectively wrested power from the Berbers and ruled the region until they in turn were overthrown and displaced to the south and east by Arab invaders during the 16th century. Many of those Black Africans that remained become part of an underclass known as the haratine that remains to this day[1]

Despite the tradition of invasion, the entrenchment of Islam in Mauritania occurred through the migration of Muslim traders across the region during the 10th century[2]. Even in cases where the religion was forcibly imposed upon indigenous inhabitants it is important to note that of all the avenues of expansions, only this one was sporadic and short-lived[3].

Due to the method in which Islam was introduced to Mauritania, the artistic expression of the region involves cultural syncretism. The silver tendrils inscribed on the daggers hilt are one of the main indicators of this cultural interaction. Due to theological principles that prohibit the depiction of animal or human figures, nonrepresentational designs, such as the tendrils, were heavily used in Islamic art pieces. Moreover, indigenous West African art also heavily utilized abstraction in its works[4]. As such, the dagger represents the ways in which, sometimes, Islam not only introduced new cultural mores to a region but reinforced older values as well in West Africa.

Ethnic minorities, numbering at about a 1/3 of the population, include other groups such as the Fulani, Tukulor, Soninke, and Wolof. Despite the diversity of Mauritanias ethnic makeup, many of the groups, specifically the Moors, Tukulor and Soninke, follow similar cultural practices and uphold similar societal structures

The relative homogeneity of social practice in Mauritania can be linked back to a history of trade and social upheaval, which allowed for cultural exchange and dissemination. The first group to occupy the region were the Berbers who moved southwards in search of new pasture lands and trade routes; through their endeavors they displaced the original inhabitants of the area, the Bafour peoples. Not much is known about these indigenous peoples but evidence suggests that they were a pastoral proto-Berber group that migrated to the area during the Neolithic, when Mauritania had not yet succumbed to processes of desertification. The Berbers control over the region, however, did not last indefinitely. Africans from the Mali, Ghana and Songhai empires respectively wrested power from the Berbers and ruled the region until they in turn were overthrown and displaced to the South and East by Arab invaders during the 16th century. Many of those peoples from West Africa that remained become part of a subordinated class known as the haratine that remains to this day[1]

The entrenchment of Islam in Mauritania occurred through the migration of Muslim traders during the 10th century[2]. Islam in West Africa generally pursued the paths of least resistance, dissemination of the faith being marked by slow and steady advances. Even in cases where the religion was forcibly imposed upon indigenous inhabitants it is important to note that of all the avenues of expansions, only this one was sporadic and short-lived[3].

As a result of Islams dispersal across the region and its central role in mediating cultural life in Mauritania, the religions influence can be seen in the design of the object. Specifically, the lock features geometrical patterns inscribed into its silver surface. This aesthetic choice reflects Islams prohibition on the depiction of figurative forms, such as people or animals, due to the fact that the creation of living forms is unique to God [4] Furthermore, the use of nonrepresentational forms also emphasizes the concept of cultural syncretism in that indigenous West African art also traditionally utilized abstraction in its pieces. Therefore, this lock illustrates how Islam not only brought new cultural mores to West Africa but simultaneously reinforced older values as well[5].

Curatorial Notes: The Ait Atta are the largest Berber group in southern Morocco, one of many that can be found throughout the Middle Atlas Mountains and the southeastern Saharan region. “Ait” means “people of,” and “Atta” is the “Daddy Atta” of legend who was the shared ancestor of all Ait Atta peoples. Well into the mid-nineteenth-century, most Atta groups lived a life of semi-nomadism known as transhumance, where a small number of shepherds and goatherds in an Atta community seasonally departed their home for grazing pastures up into the mountains. Transhumance was accompanied by sedentary agriculture, which defined the lives of the majority. Throughout the 20th century, nomadic movement was disrupted by Moroccan governments, many distinctive aspects of precolonial Atta culture slowly became untenable. Today, very few Atta families transhume, and most have settled on specific land.
This style of teapot is known as a Manchester teapot and called a barrahd. Sugar is one of Morocco’s most valuable assets and part of the reason it was flooded with Europeans at the turn of the 20th century. While never a colony of a European nation, it was carved up as different protectorates throughout the early 1900s. It is known as a Manchester teapot because of a Manchester manufacturer, Richard Wright who exported silver teapots to Morocco. Silver is highly valued in Berber culture due to its connection with the color white which symbolizes purity and piety. The red bead on the top could be associated with womanhood and fertility.
Popularized by the British in the 1870s, tea is an important ritual in Moroccan culture and nearly every household has a teapot. While there are clay teapots and tea sets created by hand, metal teapots are considered to be an important of the tea pouring ritual. Poorer families may have a simple aluminum barrahd while richer families may have more ornate teapots made of silver. They also may have accessories such as a siniyya, a round table with feet that the teapot and glasses rest on. There are also rbaia, circular containers for tea, mint and sugar and a bahbur or samovar which is used to hold the water for tea making. Even on long journeys, a teapot is brought along and tea is drunk during pauses in the trek. Tea drinking is an integral part of hospitality and thus people are willing to spend more on an elaborate tea set or carry the extra weight of the teapot to be a generous host. The pressure of hosting falls on the men to serve the tea although often the women may make the tea out of view.
Alcohol is generally prohibited by Islamic societies, however teahouses are common. Teahouses provide places to gather and men will discuss business over tea. Here the tea is made and served by the men who run the tea house.
Mint tea is considered Morocco’s national drink, in the north made with sugar and fresh mint to sweeten it, while in the south it is stronger and more bitter with only dried mint. In the north, it is brought to a boil once then the bitter water is discarded, then new water is added and brought to a boil, then more water along with the sugar. Meanwhile, in the south, water is added, not discarded. This is because the north has abundant water, mint, and sugar but is less available in the arid south.
