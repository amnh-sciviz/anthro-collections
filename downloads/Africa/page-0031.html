ARMLET

Culture: MASAI?
Locale: WAMKA, NEAR MOMBASSA
Country: KENYA
Material: HORN
Acquisition Year: 1907 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: TJADER
Keywords: ARMLET
Category: COSTUME

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a pastoralist culture who live in the Rift Valley grasslands in Kenya and Tanzania. Their nomadic livelihood requires their art be portable. This armlet is an example of Maasai personal ornamentation from surrounding Mombasa. It is worn on the upper arm of Maasai warriors while herding cattle and other daily activities. It is argued that it is typically worn on the left arm, and can be made of horny, ivory, wood, or metal (Braunholtz 191: 65).

In the Maasai’s language, Maa, the armlet called errap and resembles the curved form found in the horns of cattle (Klumpp 1987: 101). During special occasions, such as battle and feasting, the armlet may have an attached three-foot strand of animal fur (Braunholtz 1921: 65). It does not have brightly colored beads or other types of attachments, which are found in later versions of this object. Increased access to European traders trickled in the beginnings of the twentieth century, which made glass beads and other important imported materials available.

Maasai warriors are known as part of the moran. The Maasai travel through subsequent stages in life based on both age and accomplishments. In pastoralist Maasai society, the moran is a specific age-set in between uncircumcised boys and junior elders. Maasai men are initiated into the moran between the ages of twelve and eighteen, depending on their age at the point of the four or five year cycle (Hodgson 1999: 126). Maasai warriors herd cattle together, drink milk together, and sleep in homesteads together. This lasts until the warriors participate in the eunoto rite of passage and become senior warriors and eventually junior elders. It is at this point that they must marry and give up their elaborate dress and liberal lifestyle. The constant community with other men as a warrior diminishes as the new elder establishes his own homestead, individuality, and political power.

Researcher: Rebecca Cruz, 2014

Curatorial Notes: The gourd, or ‘gourd water bottle’ is from the Maasai people whose territory consists of areas along the Rift Valley in Kenya and Tanzania. According to museum archives, this gourd may have been acquired specifically in the Laikipia reserve. The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist culture, relying mainly on cattle for subsistence and wealth (Somjee 2000: 98).

Although this object is listed as a water bottle, the Maasai predominately use this type of object to carry milk (Palsson 1990: 83). A Maasai warrior most likely carried this object while herding cattle and participating in activities corresponding to his age-grade ('moran'). In addition, this object was intended for Maasai men based on the lack of beaded ornamentation. A Maasai woman’s version, called Enkukuri, contains elaborate beaded straps and a slightly different shape (Klumpp 1987: 103).

Early European travelers often feared the moran for their notorious reputation for violence and frequent raids. A member of the moran would typically obtain a gourd filled with milk left especially for him from his mother, who is in charge of milking and setting aside portions of milk for the members of the household unit (Ibrahim 2001: 88). The gourds are cleaned out with a combination of charcoal, cow’s urine, and water (Tjader 1910: 257). Maasai values, such as the importance of community, are shown through the use of this object. For instance, a warrior may not finish a gourd of milk or drink from it alone (Kaj 1989: 18). This is to avoid any sense of individuality or self-indulgence.

The tubular shape of the gourd is convenient for the Maasai because it can be easily slung over the shoulder with the attached leather straps. These gourds must be portable because of their nomadic livelihood.

Curatorial Notes: These Maasai earplugs are worn to stretch the ear lobes so that elaborate earrings and adornments can be worn. Contemporary earplugs are most often made out of wood, but historically, stone and ivory were often the preferred material for crafting earplugs. See 90.0/1864 and 90.0/1869 for examples of alternative form and style (Klumpp 1987: 289) (Hollis 1905: 22).

Ear stretching is a gradual process. A child’s father will first pierce his or her ears when the child is able to demonstrate that he or she can look after small cattle around age six or seven. He may use a thorn of a tree called ol-ngoswa, also known as the soapberry or thorn tree in English, to pierce the lobe (Quattrocchi 2000: 256). As the child becomes more independent, the father cuts larger holes in stages to hold larger ear ornaments. This process will prove a child’s resistance to pain. Boys will insist their father cut their ears so that they show they are eager to become part of the moran who are specific age-set of Maasai warriors. After or around circumcision rites, wooden plugs are inserted in ears of both boys and girls in order to stretch the ear. Progressively, the earplugs are replaced with larger and larger forms until the desired size is achieved (Bland-Sutton 1910: 1608).

The shape and style of an ear ornament set is representative of status and age-grade in Maasai society. The earplugs, or inkulalin in Maasai terminology, are a sign of young age and immaturity. In an effort to show off, young boys may attempt to dress like warriors, but they are not yet ready or allowed to wear ear ornaments corresponding to the moran such as isakankari (single ear fobs) or ilmintoni (double ear fobs) (Klumpp 1987: 166). Ear ornaments consisting of intricate beaded patterns are an indication that a woman is married. See objects 90.1/8820 and 90.2/648 for examples. To take them off in front of her husband or in public is a sign of disgrace. In addition elders have a special form of ear ornamentation (surutya) consisting of a leather strap holding a coiled brass pendant and kaurie shell. This is worn when their sons become warriors (Bland-Sutton 1910: 1609).

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist group whose territory relatively consists of areas along the Rift Valley in Kenya and Tanzania (Somjee 2000: 98). Historically, only women have been known to make all forms of art (Klumpp 1987: 112-113). Art making is typically a communal process. Recently married women and Maasai warriors are the most ornamented in society.

This necklace is classified as an early nineteenth century necklace, called olomuton that was the basis for the evolution of a Maasai woman’s beaded necklace. Both men and women wore this type of object (Klumpp 1987: 97, 140, 256). Later on, a single row of beads called arukisho, could be broadened into a collar with multiple rows of beads. The shell may have been gathered from rivers such as the Talek in the Narok District in Tanzania or in other nearby rivers where clamshells were abundant. Other variations of this necklace include a conus shell with similar chains running from it. The chain may have been made by the Kikuyu blacksmiths, who live in the highlands of Mount Kenya and often trade with the Maasai.

It is important to note that the necklace relatively consists of a single blue color. Part of the necklace includes a few out of place white and brown beads, but is overall a solid blue necklace. Blue is considered black in Maasai terminology (Klumpp 1987: 81). It is a very strong color in Maasai color ideology and is often related to religious ornamentation. This is because blue represents the Sky God, Enkai.

Maasai women use alternating complementary and contrasting colors. Color blocking rules creates a vibrating composition. They are broken down into strong and weak. Blue, black, and red are considered strong colors and yellow and green are considered weak colors. Two strong colors can be next to each other, but two weak cannot. Green can be considered strong if next to yellow. Black and more commonly white are used as outlines. Maasai women take great care to follow these rules in order to create art that is considered aesthetically pleasing. Each ornament must contrast with other ornaments that he or she is wearing as well. Maasai ideologies including, duality, balance, and equality are illustrated in beaded art. Enkai, the sky god, has two forms (day and night). Male and female gender roles, although different, together allow society to function as a whole. They complement each other, just as the colors do in a beaded necklace. The color blocking creates dividers, which reflects the Maasai ideas on continuity. This is said to be left for Enkai and so, there must be breaks in society such as where grass ends and water begins, pouring milk in separate segments, etc. Edges of a triangle reflect this ideal as well.

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist cultural group who live in both Kenya and Tanzania along the Rift Valley. Historically, the style and form has changed throughout time, however the symbolism and social context is relatively constant. The moran warriors are young Maasai men responsible for herding, protecting, and capturing cattle. They use their spears in throwing competitions to show off their prowess. The spear is used for decorative purposes as well; it is part of the warrior’s daily dress. It is iconic of the Maasai because of their violent reputation for raids and ceremonial lion kills. Spears made for sale in the tourism industry are never used or functional, and are conveniently adjustable to fit inside a traveler’s suitcase.

The spear can have multiple meanings depending on the age-grade. Brand new spears are considered appropriate for Maasai warriors. As they begin to become worn and aged however, the spears are given to elders and young boys. When they become even more worn out, the spears are given to younger boys and older elders. A Maasai carrying an aged spear symbolizes his non-warrior status. Each age-grade has its own stylistic preferences; therefore the style of spear is reminiscent of a specific group of warriors. They also adopt innovations from other ethnic groups.

The spears are made out of wood or metal often with an attached ball of fur, in this case, lion skin is attached at the base. The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasai’s animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemy’s cultural boundary unharmed.

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist cultural group who live in both Kenya and Tanzania along the Rift Valley. Historically, the style and form has changed throughout time, however the symbolism and social context is relatively constant. The moran warriors are young Maasai men responsible for herding, protecting, and capturing cattle. They use their spears in throwing competitions to show off their prowess. The spear is used for decorative purposes as well; it is part of the warrior’s daily dress. It is iconic of the Maasai because of their violent reputation for raids and ceremonial lion kills. Spears made for sale in the tourism industry are never used or functional, and are conveniently adjustable to fit inside a traveler’s suitcase.

The spear can have multiple meanings depending on the age-grade. Brand new spears are considered appropriate for Maasai warriors. As they begin to become worn and aged however, the spears are given to elders and young boys. When they become even more worn out, the spears are given to younger boys and older elders. A Maasai carrying an aged spear symbolizes his non-warrior status. Each age-grade has its own stylistic preferences; therefore the style of spear is reminiscent of a specific group of warriors. They also adopt innovations from other ethnic groups.

The spears are made out of wood or metal often with an attached ball of fur, in this case, lion skin is attached at the base. The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasai’s animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemy’s cultural boundary unharmed.
