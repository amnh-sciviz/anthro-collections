Curatorial Notes: The Bambara of Mali are Mande-speaking people who live on the Bani and Upper Niger Rivers who are predominantly agrarian. Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good job (Phillips 1995: 502).

Religious life in Bambara villages is characterized by diversity and competition. There are many different power associations (jow).(Colleyn 185). Jow (plural) are, Institutions that wield power by concentrating it into material objects and launching it, under control, out into the world, are common throughout Africa (Colleyn 167). The head of a family can freely affiliate their dependents, all male members of the family younger then themselves, to the different jow. The same man can be a member of many different power associations (Colleyn 185-186).

Pictured is a Kn mask. Kn cannot be portrayed in any clear cut analysis nor is its name said it public. It cannot be reduced to materials involved nor the metaphors used to describe it in song or prayer. It also cannot be deduced from the setting of the ritual performances. It has to be approached through multiplicity of indices. Kn, Belongs to the mythical class of waraw, the wild beasts, which as pitiless, dangerous and omnipotent, but can be propitiated (Colleyn 186). Each waraw is, feared as well as worshipped. The priests (jo-tigiw) ask for their protection, while being well aware of their destructive power (Colleyn 186).

Mythical in nature, Kn is considered to be female. Characteristics are borrowed from the hyena, lion, elephant, but also from a variety of species of antelopes and birds (Colleyn 186). Kn serves to foster female fertility as well as agricultural fertility. It also functions to resolve conflicts, calm down or punish troublemakers, and intimidate a soul-eating (suba-gaw) creatures (Colleyn 188).

The Bambara believe that human birth must have a deitys favor. It said that Kn gives favor and, gives birth to people. People make wishes, promising sacrifices to Kn if they are given a baby. In addition, to bring the deity that favors birth, Kn also imposes justice (Colleyn 188).

The mask represents both an elephant with its wisdom and intelligences as well as a bird (spirit, ubiquity) with emphasis on the ears. It may seem simple and even bare compared to Km mask which is covered with horns, claws, teeth, and feathers (Colleyn 185).
