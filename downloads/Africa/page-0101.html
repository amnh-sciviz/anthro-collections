Field Notes: NOGI - Bark cloth still new rather large specimen; the only native clothing among the men in use, if one discounts the clothing or at least the imported cloth which is distributed among the natives. The chiefs often distinguish themselves especially when visiting white men by putting a blanket around their waist in exactly the same manner as their NOGI. The imported cloth is often made into wide pants, then the upper portion of the body is clothed with a shirt, often is cut out and sewn in fairly good style, but such clothing is only worn either by the chiefs or men whom they send to white men such as messengers (or in similar functions) and by natives who have finished their service as soldiers or workmen. They have two kinds of different trees planted irregularly in their villages in such fashion that for every complex of houses (Mangbetu have several wives) posseses several or many trees. The planting is a very simple affair as they cut off the upper shoots or even some of the principal branches forming the crown and simply stick it into the ground where it thrives very well indeed. They call the trees, ARBRES OF NOGI [or] BARKCLOTH TREE. If the trees arrive at a certain thickness, about 3 to 4 inches in diameter, they make 2 circular incisions, one somewhat above the ground, the other somewhat below the crown and one vertical incision running from one circular incision to the other, the bark is then peeled off. It evidently does not hurt the tree as this process is repeat...ed whenever the bark has regenerated. At the edge of these incisions are often growing fine rootlets. The 2 trees planted are FICUS ROKO and WROISTINIA KOTSEHYANA. The manner of preparing the barkcloth is rather simple. First it is beaten upon a round log with an ordinary piece of round wood, when slightly pliable, the bark is worked over upon the same block with a hammer cut out generally of elephant-bones, often of ivory, but I have also seen a wooden hammer. The hammer shows on the surface small irregular squares rounded on their upper surface. It is about 4-6 cm in diameter and fastened by means of a rope to a split stick. The work is performed by men. They hammer and hammer and keeping the bark double, triple, single; beating crossways and straight ahead. After having it beaten well, it is washed out and slightly dried. The process is repeated. If they desire to blacken it, the finished barkcloth is laid into a certain kind of blackish mud, 5 or 6 hours immersion suffice to give it rather blackish color, otherwise it either has a lighter red-brown color. After the immersion in the mud it is washed and pressed out and beaten over after having previously nearly dried out. The manner of wearing and arranging the barkcloth is among all the men rich or poor exactly the same, but it is of course clear that rich men or chiefs of importance wear much larger NOGI which is heavily folded around the waist, the sides overlap, the ends passing then upward in front or in back as well as on the side right under the arm. The NOGI is firmly held around the waist by belts of either hide of animal or by a string, or by fibers of palm, etc. To put on their NOGI they first pass one end underneath their belt in front, then they pass the other end limbs and push it upward in the back. Then follows the arranging of the folds. The knife in the belt and the little woven hat fastened to its dressed hair completes his clothing. Poor men often wear small pieces, very much born up, sometimes just enough to cover the median parts, but in principle and arrangement exactly the NOGI of the chief of distinction . The above samples of NOGIS show also the capacity of the natives with regard to sewing and repairing holes. They use their own native needles.Show More >>

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, they have a rich musical heritage that plays an important role in various aspects of Mangbetu society.

Mangbetu basket rattles are called nangbara (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 206). They are made from wood and plant fiber, and they are filled with seeds. Women, using the same skills used in woven basket production, typically weave the head of the rattle. During dances they are played by either men or by women, but particularly played by men during hunts. Women played them during court dances.

Schildkrout, Enid, and Curtis A. Keim. African Reflections: Art from Northeastern Zaire. Seattle: U of Washington, 1990.

AMULET NECKLACE
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 90.1/ 5249
Country: NORTH AFRICA
Material: HIDE, PIGMENT
Dimensions: LARGEST: L:11 W:10 CORD:L:60 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1918 [GIFT]
Donor: BRACE
Keywords: AMULET NECKLACE
Category: COSTUME
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret.' The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques.

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan 1990).

Researcher: Gabriel Chance Sevigny (2018)

Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret.' The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques.

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan 1990).
