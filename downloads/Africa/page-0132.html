Curatorial Notes: The AbaGusii, or Gusii, are an amalgam of ekeGusii speaking peoples who live primarily in the Nyanza Province in the western highlands of western Kenya (Varnum 1971: 242). While sharing a common language, culture, and historical traditions, the Gusii were never a unified political state (Hakansson: 1988 30). The British colonial administration took control of Gusii land settling Kisii Town in 1908 ( Hakansson: 1988 73), and they subsequently referred to the Gusii people as Kisii, after a Luo mistranslation of their name (Varnum 1971: 242).

An obukano is a bowl lyre that is very common in the western Lake Victoria district. It is used as an accompaniment for songs and dances, and is only rarely employed as a solo instrument to introduce a song. Amatera, which literally means “a man who sings (Varnum 1971: 244), is a performer who both plays an instrument and sings; a person who does not sing but plays an instrument is known only as a musician. Professional musicians in Kenya, all of whom are male, are often hired for social events, such as weddings and funerals (Hyslop 1972: 49). Used in praise songs to “glorify important personages or to commemorate memorable events” (Varnum), songs played on the obukano are typically improvised. The obukano is usually not played in conjunction with other instruments except for some kayamba, which are smaller instruments employed for “rhythmic emphasis.” A player may have rattles attached to his ankle, which he then plays by stamping his foot on the ground (Varnum 1971: 247).

The bowl and its two wooden arms are carved from wood. Obukano have eight strings that, while once made from zebra sinew, are now commonly made from sisal string or synthetic chords of the type used to make tennis racquets. Prior to covering the bowl with the hide of either a zebra or cow, two or three stones are placed within the bowl in order to create a rattling sound when the instrument is shaken. The animal hide is then tied in place by rawhide chords. The obukano has both a real and a false bridge. The former is located at the foot of the instrument, while the latter is placed in the center so that the strings rattle against the bridge order to provide the instrument with its distinctive “rattling” sound.

A performer plays the instrument by placing its side on his left thigh while his left hand is placed on the top four strings and the right hand is placed on the lower four strings. Typically using the thumb and first two fingers, the strings must be strongly plucked in order to elicit the ‘buzzing’ noise (Varnum 1971: 246).The obukano is no longer widely in use in the Kisii District.

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist cultural group who live in both Kenya and Tanzania along the Rift Valley. Historically, the style and form has changed throughout time, however the symbolism and social context is relatively constant. The moran warriors are young Maasai men responsible for herding, protecting, and capturing cattle. They use their spears in throwing competitions to show off their prowess. The spear is used for decorative purposes as well; it is part of the warrior’s daily dress. It is iconic of the Maasai because of their violent reputation for raids and ceremonial lion kills. Spears made for sale in the tourism industry are never used or functional, and are conveniently adjustable to fit inside a traveler’s suitcase.

The spear can have multiple meanings depending on the age-grade. Brand new spears are considered appropriate for Maasai warriors. As they begin to become worn and aged however, the spears are given to elders and young boys. When they become even more worn out, the spears are given to younger boys and older elders. A Maasai carrying an aged spear symbolizes his non-warrior status. Each age-grade has its own stylistic preferences; therefore the style of spear is reminiscent of a specific group of warriors. They also adopt innovations from other ethnic groups.

The spears are made out of wood or metal often with an attached ball of fur, in this case, lion skin is attached at the base. The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasai’s animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemy’s cultural boundary unharmed.

The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasai’s animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemy’s cultural boundary unharmed.

Curatorial Notes: This single ear ornament is made of small round and flat beads and iron wire, which are typical materials used by Maasai women. This object may have even been commissioned for Queeny in Kenya before it was acquired by the Museum in 1953. It includes eleven individual strands of a different colors ranging from blue, red, green, white and yellow. There are also three flat beads at each end and at the center.

Maasai women use alternating complementary and contrasting colors. Color blocking rules creates a vibrating composition. They are broken down into strong and weak. Blue, black, and red are considered strong colors and yellow and green are considered weak colors. Two strong colors can be next to each other, but two weak cannot. Green can be considered strong if next to yellow. Black and more commonly white are used as outlines. Maasai women take great care to follow these rules in order to create art that is considered aesthetically pleasing. Each ornament must contrast with other ornaments that he or she is wearing as well. Maasai ideologies including, duality, balance, and equality are illustrated in beaded art. Enkai, the sky god, has two forms (day and night). Male and female gender roles, although different, together allow society to function as a whole. They complement each other, just as the colors do in a beaded necklace. The color blocking creates dividers, which reflects the Maasai ideas on continuity. This is said to be left for Enkai and so, there must be breaks in society such as where grass ends and water begins, pouring milk in separate segments, etc. Edges of a triangle reflect this ideal as well.

Only women historically have been known to manufacture ornaments. In times of economic hardship, women may go into urban areas and to work in bead-working businesses. Maasai women are also active in the tourist industry and make ornaments specifically to be sold, which is often called ‘deviant art,’ because its cultural and religious value is taken out of the object (Klumpp 1987: 177).
