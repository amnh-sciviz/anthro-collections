Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout West Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general. Although this board most likely was made by a Kuranko craftsperson, this technology is primarily associated with the Hausa people, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990). Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa and Mande ’ulama, called malams in Hausa, service a variety of Muslim and non-Muslim groups in the region (Hassan).

Malams oversee the traditional education of a community. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of this education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).

The Kuranko are themselves one of the many groups descended from the 13th-17th century empire of Mali, a mande-speaking kingdom that was itself a major factor in the political spread of West African Islam. The Kuranko fled south and settled in what is now northern Sierra Leone following a wave of 15th century invasions in Mali from the Mossi people of the upper Volta (Jackson 1977).

This antelope headdress is worn by Bambara male dancers, Ci-Wara (translated to farm animals). It is often worn in pairs of one male and female antelope. The antelope symbolizes the farmer who tills his fields tirelessly (Siegmann 2009: 62). Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good
job (Phillips 1995: 502).

The headdress exemplifies the Bambara peoples devotion to agricultural cultivation and fertility. The organization of the Ci-wara performance is based on the Bamana respect for the power and efficiency of the union of male and female. As human reproduction is the result of the sexual union between man and woman, so agricultural fertility is attributed to the union between fire (the sun), an expression of the male principle, and earth and water, an expression of the female principle. (Colleyn 2001: 204).

The horns of the antelope represent ears of millet and the zigzag design of the mane represents the sun. The costume worn by the performers consists of raffia fibers that extend from the base of the headdress to the ground and represent the rain needed for cultivation.The female antelope has a child on its back depicting the way mothers carry their children, but also calling attention to animal, plant, and human fertility (Siegmann 2009: 62). The headdresses hold a parallel between the penetration of the earth through farming and copulation (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

The headdress is associated with different purposes, however, as one observer noted, like all other dances of the Bamana that of [Ci-Wara] was never static .the development and change took place somewhat independently in each region and indeed in each village (Lagamma 2002: 71). Likewise, each performance is unique and no one description will be able to convey the many tribal traditions.

It is used during agricultural competitions. During these competitions young farmers are galvanized by songs, music, and the masked dances. They seek to imitate the mythical animals that originally taught humans agriculture. During the competition the men work through a number of rows in a field and the first to completion is the winner and declared the Ci-Wara. The title brings much praise to the winner. Men are constantly invited to take up the contest, just as their ancestors had done before, and see how they measure up to the heroes of the past.

It is also used during entertainment and ritual events. They appeared during festivals at the Sgou court. After colonial conquest they were also a part of the July 14th Bastille celebrations, French National Day.

Third, it is used during an annual two day celebration that takes place between November and April, according to the moons appearance. This celebration includes sacrifices and the remaking of cult objects. It also includes a parade through the village with stops
a spots where the union of the male and female masks are symbolized (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

Curatorial Notes: Across the continent, wire toys, such as this toy animal from Nigeria, are an exuberant and efficient way of fabricating children's toys. These toys are made from a variety of recycled materials such as scrap metal, metal rods, rubber bands, tin cans, and many others (Deregowski). In the past, toys such as these tended to be made out of wood; however, with increasing availability of wire power cables from formal and informal electrical grids and other infrastructure projects, metal wire has become an increasingly popular option. The wire’s malleability allows for many different design options. Creating planes and toys out of scrap materials popular with children for their own recreational use, but it is also encouraged by schools with many different levels of competition (Donlon).

While these toys were originally exclusively for personal play, artisans have started to produce and sell wire toys to tourists. These toys tend to be more elaborate, using craft workshop techniques such as welding to create profitable, albeit less play-friendly finished products. These toys also tend to not be made from as many recycled materials with figures being “entirely created in formed wire represent[ing] a style largely, if not solely, market driven by tourism pressure in Southern Africa” (Donlon, 49). Not only is there a lack of recycled materials, but wire toys produced for the tourist market may also include not recycled materials, utilizing mass produced parts like toy car wheels (Donlon). Donlon states that these figures marketed towards tourists are a medium between the wooden toys of the past and the and the recycled toys that have come into popularity over the past years. On one end the wooden toys tend to be viewed as priceless by both locals and visitors. On the other hand, the wire toys used by kids are not as marketable to a tourist market. This creates room for artisans to create their own styles in order to appeal to tourists.
