Culture: AMHARA
Country: ETHIOPIA
Material: HIDE, FUR, INK, PIGMENT
Dimensions: ROLLED) L:5.8 W:5.2 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1967 [GIFT]

Keywords: AMULET, SCROLL

Curatorial Notes: Exhibited above is an Amharic vellum scroll used as an amulet by the poor to guard against evil. In addition to the red and black Amharic or Ge’ez script, the scroll features an illustration of spitting serpents. Based in central Ethiopia, the Amhara constitute close to one-third of the country’s population. They follow the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, a tradition with fifth-century Byzantine roots; and strengthened by this connection, Amharic art embodies Christian elements that were influenced by Coptic and Byzantine beliefs. These magically-endowed scrolls were created for an individual by the debtera, a religious specialist, to address specific curative or protective needs. To personalize the scrolls the healer would include the name of the individual and sized the parchment to his exact height.

Researcher: Emily Fite, 2012

Source
Strelcyn, Stefan. 1972. "Catalogue of Ethiopian Manuscripts of the Wellcome Institute of the History of Medicine in London.” Bulletin of the School of Oriental and African Studies (University of London/Cambridge University Press), 35 (1):27-55.
