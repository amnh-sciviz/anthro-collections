Field Notes: "Pottery paddle: Lokele. Yakusu" (642). 24 September 1906. "Pottery is in full swing here and the outfit and types present much new to me. The clay is fine grained gray in color: it is pounded up in th end of an old canoe with a long handled pounder far more slender at the lower end than Ikoko's. Sand is used to temper the paste. The mass to be shaped is thrown upon a board or block in which a shallow, neat, rounded hollow exists. When neatly balled it is struck from above by a hearty pestle-shaped pounder and then hollowed into a rather thin walled form. This is then worked into shape by beating from outside with a little broad potter's paddle [object No. 3370] upon an inside support of pottery [drawing of support] neatly made. In final shaping and sun drying the vessel is supported in a broken pot base. The neck and rim of cooking pots are decorated with cordline and check surfaces, which are produced by impressing with (a) notched edged seed or (b) a slender bit of stick, pitted and cut which is rolled to produce a surface of decoration. To prevent adhesion these are used on spaced rubbed with palmoil" (663).
