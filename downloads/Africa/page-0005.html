Curatorial Notes: This hide amulet is a Gift from the Royal Ethnographic Museum in Berlin. The Dagomba are an ethnic group known for the kingdom of Dagbon, in northern Togo and Ghana, which persisted from its capital of Yendi until German colonization in 1914. In the 14th century, the kingdom controlled almost all of the territory in the great bend of the river Niger (Cardinall 1921). Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. (Hassan 1990).

This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir0 (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret’ (Hassan 1990).

Many of these charms owe their origin to Dagomba and other people of the Volta basin. In the 19th century, most of the 900 Arabic folios owned by the kingdom of Dagbon were concerned with the production of such items (Handloff 1982). There may be several types of text concealed in these charms, from written prayer, to names of God (Hassan), designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Katemi (Handloff) or Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”) (Hassan), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan).
