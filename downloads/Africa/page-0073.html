Field Notes: "Girdle and tail. Lokele. Yakusu. 81" (644). On his initial visit to the Lokele on 24 September 1906, Starr remarks that "Belts of natural color fibre rather closely and unattractively woven are worn..." (662). However, he revises his opinion to a more positive one the next day, when he returns to purchase several examples: "We bought this time beautifully made girdles and tails of fine cord--either left in natural color or dyed black, purple-black, or rich red. The tail is worn by women hanging behind. It folds at the middle, carefully woven, over the girdle. But the cord workmanship is fine. There is another form of girdle, which apparently is esteemed even more than this. It is plaited work of some vegetable material" (667). Perhaps this last comment is directed toward the belts he noticed the first day he visited. At any rate, this object appears to be one of the plaited examples rather than the cord ones.

Cowries generally preceded the use of imported European glass beads, becoming more important in ritual and as a medium of gift exchange as glass beads became more abundant. Exhibition Label: Face mask. Cloth, cowrie shells, glass beads, metal, vegetable fiber. Kuba-Bushong. 19th-early 20th century. This mask, known as Mwash aMbooy, the chosen leader, is one of three masks used in Kuba initiations and ceremonies performed under royal patronage. Mwash aMbooy represented a spirit who had the power to punish quarrelsome women and children by inflicting them with sickness. The beadwork designs on the back of the mask refer to Woot, the Kuba ancestor, and were repeated on body scarification as well as on other Kuba objects.

Field Notes: "Girdle cord and tail: Lokele. Yakusu. 1.00" (643). On his initial visit to the Lokele on 24 September 1906, Starr remarks that "Belts of natural color fibre rather closely and unattractively woven are worn..." (662). However, he revises his opinion to a more positive one the next day, when he returns to purchase several examples: "We bought this time beautifully made girdles and tails of fine cord--either left in natural color or dyed black, purple-black, or rich red. The tail is worn by women hanging behind. It folds at the middle, carefully woven, over the girdle. But the cord workmanship is fine. There is another form of girdle, which apparently is esteemed even more than this. It is plaited work of some vegetable material" (667). Perhaps this last comment is directed toward the belts he noticed the first day he visited. At any rate, this object appears to be one of the cord examples rather than the plaited ones.
