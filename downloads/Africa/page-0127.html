This instrument is a small dundun called a gudugudu. It is a small, bowl-shaped drum that is hit with two lashes made from cowhide. Within North America, Yoruba slaves were brought primarily to Virginia, Georgia, Florida, North Carolina, and South Carolina (Falola 2013: 127). The male slave seated directly to his left is playing a stringed instrument that is likely a goje, a precursor to the banjo that is played by numerous northern West African peoples; goje is the Hausa term. The watercolor is a visual linkage to the cultures and traditions that African peoples attempted, in the face of incredible adversity, to sustain throughout this era.

Curatorial Notes: The Yoruba peoples are one of the largest ethnic groups in West Africa. Located in Southwest Nigeria and Southern Benin, Yorubaland consisted of independent city states interconnected through a shared language and culture. The diaspora of Yoruba peoples brought their religions and cultures to the Americas and the United Kingdom resulted in a contemporary practice of a mixture of traditional beliefs as well as Christian influences.

The incorporation of the drums as the musical aspect of everyday life is an enduring element of Yoruba culture, religion and practice that connects their history to their present, living culture. The gongo which is a curved stick used as a beater in the playing of the Dndn drum and is typically about a foot in length. The gongo is carved from either the celtis soyauxii tree or synsepalum dulcificum plants (King 1961), while the body of the drum is usually carved from the cordia millenii tree. The curvature of the gongo is unique and sufficient in producing the right tones and pitches of each drum played. It is also used to mute the drum by pressing the tip of the curve to the skin of the instrument. The body of the drum is typically held under the arm and supported by decorated leather straps (gbkọ) (King 1961) while the curved mallet, or gongo, is used to beat the membranous skin of the instrument.

Drums are a integral part of Yoruba religion. Drums were not only used during ritual or ceremonial occasions but also as a medium for people to communicate with the divine. When the drums accompany dancing or singing, the assembly of the drums mimic the hierarchical family structure, a family of five instruments, the largest drum being called the y or y l which means “Mother Drum” (King 1961: 2). Whether the drums are used during tt, (ritual), er (play), ẹbẹ (offerings), each instrument varies for different activities (Drewal: 1992, 201). The unimembranophonic drums bn, Ogdn, ps, or Bt (Adegbite 1988) are used mainly for religious ceremonies while bi-membranophonic drums like the Dndn, which were referred to by English colonists as “talking drums,” (Ruskin 2013: 1) are used for their ability to mimic the tonal patterns in the Yoruba language. The ensemble of drums create combinations of distinct rhythmic patterns that compare to the sound of different voices talking at once. Depending on the amount of tension placed on the drumhead by leather thongs called ọṣọn (King 1961), different tones and pitches are expressed to linguistically sound like the human voice, giving them a way to speak.

The surviving religion shows signs of cultural blending, including Christianity introduced by both colonizers and missionaries. Yoruba religion had an reciprocal impact on the colonizing culture as well; the Dndn drum has been incorporated in Christian worship in African Separatist churches (AHA). The Yoruba people resisted imperialism’s imposition of European culture in the midst of ongoing physical and structural violence. Today, about twenty percent of Yoruba people still practice the traditional form of the religion that does not incorporate any Christian influence. The Yoruba cultural landscape of today (including the diaspora) is vibrant, complex and highly diverse in relation to peoples’ histories and experiences.

Curatorial Notes: In rituals to Shango, the deity of thunder and lightning, dancers carry these dance wands, or Oshe Shango. Oshe can also be seen in a protective context, displayed in houses, next to shrines, or carried on potentially dangerous outings. Like this one, most are carved out of wood (but can also be made of brass, iron, or beads) and have three parts: a handle, a human figure, and the double-headed axe, recognized widely as a depiction of the thundercelts that Shango hurls to earth during thunderstorms (60). The figure represented is female, as females are seen as having special ritual purity as the nurturers of the gods.

The priest or devotee carries the oshe in his or her left hand; in Yoruba ritual, the left side is considered the side of the spirit world and rituals emphasize the left hand in order to establish spiritual communication. Priests, through their training, come to act as mediums for the deities. In Yoruba ritual, priests enter possession states through dance. When mounted by the deity, the attitude and characteristics of the priests motions change; the deities all have distinct movements that represent their vital force. Shangos motions are defined by sharp, forceful movements of the shoulders.

Curatorial Notes: Shango, the orisa of thunder and lightning, was an early king of Oyo who had immense powers. According to myth, when Shango was defeated in a magical contest and forced to leave Oyo, he hanged himself. Each city-state in Yorubaland was affiliated with a certain patron deity and when territories were conquered, they were forced to worship the deity of the conquerors. Because the rise and fall of a certain city meant an increased or decreased population worshipped its deities, history is embedded in the prominence of certain deities throughout time. When old Oyo gained power, the territory that revered Shango, their patron deity, expanded.

In rituals to Shango, the deity of thunder and lightning, this double axe is held in a devotees hand while dancing to him. The double-axe is the weapon of Shango and is used as a symbol of his possession on altars, objects, and in ceremonies dedicated to him. Priests, through their training, come to act as mediums for the deities. In Yoruba ritual, priests enter possession states through dance. When mounted by the deity, the attitude and characteristics of the priests motions change; the deities all have distinct movements that represent their vital force. Shangos motions are defined by sharp, forceful movements of the shoulders.

Curatorial Notes: The Yoruba peoples are located in southwestern Nigeria and southern Benin and are one of the largest ethnic groups in West Africa They were not a unified political state, but rather bands of interconnected independent city-states sharing a common language and culture.

Yoruba epistemology holds that the Supreme Being created heaven, earth and its inhabitants, and divinities and spirits known as Orisa, Imole, or Ebora. These divinities and spirits help the Supreme Being in overseeing and governing the world (Awolalu 1979: 20). Yoruba iron bells are usually employed in religious ceremonies. Iron bells were the primary instrument of Obatalata, the divinity associated with creation. Obatala shapes the child within its mothers womb. He is commonly worshipped as a male although there are oral traditions that identify him as a woman or androgynously (Awolalu 1979: 22). Obatala is popular amongst women because of his ability to give children to infertile women (King 1961: 5).

A young virgin or a woman past childbearing age is tasked each morning with replenishing the water within Obatala sanctuary. This woman carries a bell that she rings on her way to the sanctuary in order to notify surrounding people that she is undertaking a holy task. The sacred water is then ceremoniously distributed to worshippers, and it is this water that aides in fertility.
