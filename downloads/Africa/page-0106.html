Curatorial Notes: This sword comes from East Africa though the exact point of origin is uncertain; it may have been used and created in the Sudanic region or in Abyssinia an area now known as Ethiopia. Despite its ambiguous origins, the item exhibits a great amount of Islamic influence, indicated through its morphology, and illustrates, not only the role Arab migrations had in shaping the culture of
Northeastern Africa, but also how Islamic conceptions of art differ from those of the Western tradition.

The sword follows the mold of a kaskara sword; a weapon that is characterized by a straight two-edged blade and a cross shaped guard. However, the kaskara, and other regional swords that follow a similar pattern, is not an indigenous phenomenon; it derives from the remnants of Arab influence in the area[1]. Although Sudan became heavily Arabized, but Abyssinia did not due to the strength of the Christian state, both areas experienced influxes of Arabs who brought Islam with them. Where Islam did spread it did so peacefully; itinerant teachers and traders disseminated their ideologies as they voyaged across the land[2].

Along with their beliefs, Arab migrants also brought their own cultural artifacts. Traditional Arab swords exhibit a similar morphology; kaskaras, and related arms, differ very little from their Arab forebearers in the style of their blades and straight quillons. Another similarity between Arab swords and those from the region is the use ornamentation. This swords blade features a generous amount of engraving that exists for decoration; this creates a strong parallel between this item and other Islamic swords, which are typologically categorized through their heavy use of lavish decoration[3]. Finally, the item features a Quranic inscription on its blade that serves as a testament to the religious beliefs of its user.

Though the object exhibits many of the hallmarks of Islamic art, it is important to note that it is also a decidedly practical object. Therefore, the fusion of both practical and spiritual ideals provides insight into the manner in which this swords maker and user viewed these seemingly separate realms. Seyyed Hossein, in a book on the spirituality of Islamic art, explains this worldview best when he states: The decoration of the most commonly used objects of every day.reveals the care with which Islamic art dealt with objects of practical utility, identifying art with the making of objects of everyday life, in fact life itself, rather than a special kind of activity relegated to a distinct and separate domain of life[4]. Essentially, Hosseins argument considered in tandem with this object, should illustrate how objects in the Islamic world oftentimes escape the Western notion of art for arts sake.
