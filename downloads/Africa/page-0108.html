Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, they have a rich musical heritage that plays an important role in various aspects of Mangbetu society.

Mangbetu basket rattles are called nangbara (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 206). They are made from wood and plant fiber, and they are filled with seeds. Women, using the same skills used in woven basket production, typically weave the head of the rattle. During dances they are played by either men or by women, but particularly played by men during hunts. Women played them during court dances.

Schildkrout, Enid, and Curtis A. Keim. African Reflections: Art from Northeastern Zaire. Seattle: U of Washington, 1990.

Curatorial Notes: This object is a cup carved by the Kuba people of the Congo region who are a coalition of at least eighteen different ethnic groups who have united through mechanisms of an elaborate political system. The Kuba system of governance features a number of figures, including a king, called the nyim, who holds the most authority, and also lesser councils and representatives who wield a certain amount of power[1]. The Kuba are agriculturalists and cultivate a number of different resources. These include beans, manioc, raffia, and various other palms. The palms and the sap they produce are used in the making of palm wine. The sap is usually placed in gourds and fermented until a mildly alcoholic and acidic liquid develops.[2]

This type of cup features prominently in the social lives of the Kuba people. Wealthy individuals often engaged in conspicuous consumption and would serve large quantities of palm wine to their guests. The consumption of palm wine is a social activity, not meant to be done alone[3]. Cups of this type provided a means for the Kuba to forge significant relationships and, by extension, community solidarity.

Drinking palm wine is an activity that is only undertaken in public by men. Although women do imbibe palm wine as well, they do so clandestinely. Female intoxication is considered deviant and women who are discovered to be drinking are socially castigated. This cup almost certainly was made for and used by a Kuba man[4].

These cups also speak to Kuba epistemologies beyond the role of palm wine as a social lubricant. Kuba cups, along with other aspects of their material culture and art “need to be understood in the context and development of the kingship.[5]” Bilotta, in her analysis, indicated that the “artistry and craftsmanship” of Kuba art, including cups, “was a tribute to prosperity and the institution of kingship[6]” and, as such, reproduced “royal statuses[7]”

Curatorial Notes: This object is a charm, or talisman, that comes from the Kpelle (also called Kpessi) peoples of Liberia. Amulets of this type are associated with itinerant Muslim clerics throughout West Africa (Handloff 186). There may be several types of text concealed within, from written prayer, to names of God (Hassan 1990), designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Katemi (Handloff 1982) or Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk (Hassan). This particular charm was tied to the leg to cure or prevent sores.

Not only does this charm shed light onto the religious beliefs of its creators, but it also provides insight into the manner in which Islam diffused into a specific region of Western Africa. Liberia, an area that once belonged to the Mali Empire, is a unique place for a number of reasons (Olukoju 2006). Unlike the rest of West Africa, the territory that is now Liberia became a was never colonized. It became a nation state not from resisting the imperial ambitions of European powers, but through the efforts of an American philanthropic group that aimed to establish a homeland for freed slaves (Moran 2006). Also unique for its location, the people in and around Liberia only relatively recently started adopting Islam and Muslim practices (Olukoju).

During the late 19th century, Malinke traders played a partial role in the dispersal of Islam in Liberia. Muslim clerics further entrenched the religion by providing “spiritual guidance for local leaders” and encouraging the use of amulets and charms such as this one (Olukoju). Ultimately, this charm illustrates how the entrenchment of Islam in Liberia occurred due the interplay of a number of factors involving indigenous belief system, social structures, and political and historical trajectories. The Kpelle are a Mande-speaking population that immigrated to what is now Liberia in the late 16th century, residing inland but taking tribute from peoples on the coast. They are traditionally rice farmers, practicing a form of slash-and-burn agriculture. Their secret gender-based Poro and Sande initiation societies have historically been of interest to anthropologists. The Kpelle also have a long history of trade with Islamic-influenced groups such as the Vai and Mandingo (Bledsoe 1980).
