RATTLE, SNAKE MOTIF
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 26 / 724
Culture: SARAMACCANER
Locale: UPPER SURINAM RIVER
Material: WOOD
Dimensions: L:41 W:12 H:12 (in CM)
Technique: CARVED
Acquisition Year: 1936 [GIFT]
Donor: KAHN, MORTON C.
Keywords: RATTLE
Category: SOUND DEVICES
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: The Saramacca are one of six distinct groups of the Suriname Maroons: runaway slaves from the Dutch plantations who established independent communities inland from the coastal Dutch settlements. The carved wooden object on display here is known as a saka-sake that has been incised with a snake motif. As researcher Morton Kahn relates, At some villages we saw the saka-sake in use. This is a rattle-like instrument composed of a hollow gourd containing seeds or pebbles, with a handle through the center. At most places the saka-sake is reserved for the Voodoo or obeiah ceremonies, but usually several of the dancers had anklets and bracelets of seed rattles (485). Their religion is centered around a supreme being (Gran Gadu) and the belief that jungles, rivers, snakes and most entities embody a spirit thus each village, house and person hosts a deity to which offerings are made.

Researcher: Emily Fite, 2012


Source
Kahn, M. C. 1929. "Notes on the Saramaccaner Bush Negroes of Dutch Guiana." American Anthropologist, 31:468-490.
