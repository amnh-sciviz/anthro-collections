Curatorial Notes: This goldweight of a male figure, apart of the African Ethnographic Collection, was donated in 1943. The driving force behind the growth of the Ashanti Kingdom was the gold trade. Gold became a fundamental part of Ashanti’s monetary system as the kingdom grew into one of the largest and prosperous kingdoms in West Africa. Goldweights were an essential part of a longstanding measuring system. The weights, which were made in a wide variety of forms were used to weigh out gold dust on a balanced scale. Brass goldweights were placed on one balance and gold dust was placed on the other balance and measured out to be of equal weight.

Gold dust was a central currency used to pay for goods, taxes, fines, and duties. The Ashanti Kingdom was a major cultural and political power in Western Africa between 1670 and 1902 (Gifford, 1998). The Ashanti’s use of gold dust currency required daily utilization of balances and these goldweights were a vital part of daily economic activity until 1902 when the Ashanti Kingdom was dissolved and colonized.

This resulted from the end of the Napoleonic Wars which had the British government’s debt reached a peak in 1815 (Napoleonic Wars and the United States, 1803–1815, 2019). The Napoleonic Wars were series of major conflicts which pit the French Empire and its allies against a fluctuating array of European powers who were financed and led by the United Kingdom.

Between the 1870s and 1900, many African countries faced “European imperialist aggression, diplomatic pressures, military invasions, and eventual conquest” (Iweriebor, 2011). The British exploitation of the West African gold industry was one way the British Empire sought to resolve their own accumulating debts. In 1957, the area historically in the possession of the Ashanti kingdom was formally decolonized and the independent nation of Ghana was established (Edgerton, 1995).

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.Music, and musical instruments themselves, held symbolic significance when in employed in particular social settings.

This is an ivory carving of a conical slit drum called nekpokpo. Mangbetu slit drums belong to an amalgam of West African drums referred to as talking drums because of their use in long distance communication. Due to the incredibly small size of this object, as well the use of ivory instead of tradition wood, it is likely that this was not intended as a functioning drum but rather as an art piece meant to depict a larger nekpokpo drum used in inter village communication. Western collectors, such as scientists, colonial administrators, educators, and art collectors actively collected ivory pieces made by the Mangbetu during the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
