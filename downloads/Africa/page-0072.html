Field Notes: "Girdle and tail. Lokele. Yakusu. 81" (644). On his initial visit to the Lokele on 24 September 1906, Starr remarks that "Belts of natural color fibre rather closely and unattractively woven are worn..." (662). However, he revises his opinion to a more positive one the next day, when he returns to purchase several examples: "We bought this time beautifully made girdles and tails of fine cord--either left in natural color or dyed black, purple-black, or rich red. The tail is worn by women hanging behind. It folds at the middle, carefully woven, over the girdle. But the cord workmanship is fine. There is another form of girdle, which apparently is esteemed even more than this. It is plaited work of some vegetable material" (667). Perhaps this last comment is directed toward the belts he noticed the first day he visited. At any rate, this object appears to be one of the cord examples rather than the plaited ones.

Field Notes: "Clapping sticks (pair). Bafoma. .61" (645). 1 October 1906. Starr purchases "Three pair of the hollowed clapping sticks, which are only from Bafoma so far as I have seen them" (681). Previously, on 26 September 1906, he got to see these sticks being used during a dance at Yakusu: "At one of the clubhouses a crowd was gathered and in the road before it was a group of Foma dancers. They each had a pair of hollowed sticks painted red, which they clapped against each other, producing a really excellent dance time. There were some fifteen or twenty men and women. Field Notes: Bought on March 13th 1906. "On our way back we stopped for a moment at the Bakete-Balba villages, where we bought a little girl's dress and others of the same kind. These have interested me greatly, of late; while little boys rarely wear anything attractive or noticable, while they may have only a waiststring, a waiststring with a gourd tip, or nothing at all--little girls commonly have a more or less elaborate decoration pendent from thecord, which hang down over the sex organs clearly toattract attention thereto; the band itself is frequently of bright beads; seabeans, a cord of beads, a lot of brass copper or iron rings and spirals are the attach part. One little girl baby scarecly able to walk had a girdle-cord of blue beads, and the pendant, twinkling over her parts was a bright, broad, iron bell, at least 3 or 4 inches across; one had a little disk or ???? Of calabash; women wear in dances over their cloth, but just as the spot to which attention is to be directed brilliant metal armrings that clink and rattle and shine."

Field Notes: "Cloth: nkombe: pattern baña. Nkundu, Bolengi" (511). 30 July 1906. Some at least of the patterns have special names; two of my three are baña, which means easy, not difficult; the other is named baina bansusu, chicken tracks; the cloth itself is called biloko: the price, considering the size is a little high, 40 rods apiece, but the work is excellent. Field Notes: Bought on March 13th 1906. "On our way back we stopped for a moment at the Bakete-Balba villages, where we bought a little girl's dress and others of the same kind. These have interested me greatly, of late; while little boys rarely wear anything attractive or noticable, while they may have only a waiststring, a waiststring with a gourd tip, or nothing at all--little girls commonly have a more or less elaborate decoration pendent from thecord, which hang down over the sex organs clearly toattract attention thereto; the band itself is frequently of bright beads; seabeans, a cord of beads, a lot of brass copper or iron rings and spirals are the attach part. One little girl baby scarecly able to walk had a girdle-cord of blue beads, and the pendant, twinkling over her parts was a bright, broad, iron bell, at least 3 or 4 inches across; one had a little disk or ???? Of calabash; women wear in dances over their cloth, but just as the spot to which attention is to be directed brilliant metal armrings that clink and rattle and shine."
