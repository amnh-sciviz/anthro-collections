Curatorial Notes: The Kuba Kingdom was located in the Zaire region, south-east of the modern day Democratic Republic of the Congo. The Kuba Kingdom was the most renowned producer of raffia cloth in Central Africa (Cooksey 2011: 109). This region is bordered by three rivers; the Sankuru, Lulua and Kasai Rivers. The Kingdom comprised an association of many linguistically-linked groups, which prospered in Zaire between the seventeenth and nineteenth centuries. During this time raffia cloth symbolically represented a family's wealth, was used in ceremonies and funerals and as well as in home decoration (Cooksey 2011: 109). Raffia cloth is a skillfully woven material made from the fibers of the Raphia vinifera palm. Raffia cloths offer insight into the ideology of collective work, as work from all members of the clan is necessary to fabricate the cloth. The palms were gathered and split by the children, woven by the men, and embroidered by the women. The most prominent group of the Kuba Kingdom, the Bushoong, were also known as the Bambai, the“people of the cloth.” Raffia cloth was a vital part of Kuba identity and life. Every group within the Kuba Kingdom created distinctly different cloths (Glazer 1996).

Children gathered and split the palms either by hand or with a raffia rib comb. These strands were then soaked in warm water for an hour or more until they are silk-soft. Men in the Kuba Kingdom wove the cloths on a single heddle loom, which is a simple device, composed of a heddle and two horizontal bars, between which the palms are woven. A plain weave cloth has horizontal and vertical threads crossing over and under one another. An experienced weaver could’ve completed a cloth in a few hours (Glazer: 27). The size of the cloth is determined by the length of the raffia fibers. After a cloth was woven, it was often softened by wetting and pounding with a mortar.

Cut pile raffia were often worn as wrappers. Cloth could be used to mark a house or family during event of significance, or given as gifts to establish relations of reciprocity. When raffia cloths were worn as skirts, they showed the wearer’s status, wealth and social class. The most luxuriously decorated cloths were the most expensive and valuable. Wealthy people and the king can afford to have these skirts, but not exclusively. If a non-elite person could afford an elaborate skirt, he/she would be entitled to wear it. Fancier cloth does not announce a person’s title, but instead implies their social or economic class (Glazer 1996: 128/129).

Curatorial Notes: The Kuba Kingdom was located in the Zaire region, south-east of the modern day Democratic Republic of the Congo. The Kuba Kingdom was the most renowned producer of raffia cloth in Central Africa (Cooksey 2011: 109). This region is bordered by three rivers; the Sankuru, Lulua and Kasai Rivers. The Kingdom comprised an association of many linguistically-linked groups, which prospered in Zaire between the seventeenth and nineteenth centuries. During this time raffia cloth symbolically represented a family's wealth, was used in ceremonies and funerals and as well as in home decoration (Cooksey 2011: 109). Raffia cloth is a skillfully woven material made from the fibers of the Raphia vinifera palm. Raffia cloths offer insight into the ideology of collective work, as work from all members of the clan is necessary to fabricate the cloth. The palms were gathered and split by the children, woven by the men, and embroidered by the women. The most prominent group of the Kuba Kingdom, the Bushoong, were also known as the Bambai, the“people of the cloth.” Raffia cloth was a vital part of Kuba identity and life. Every group within the Kuba Kingdom created distinctly different cloths (Glazer 1996).

Children gathered and split the palms either by hand or with a raffia rib comb. These strands were then soaked in warm water for an hour or more until they are silk-soft. Men in the Kuba Kingdom wove the cloths on a single heddle loom, which is a simple device, composed of a heddle and two horizontal bars, between which the palms are woven. A plain weave cloth has horizontal and vertical threads crossing over and under one another. An experienced weaver could’ve completed a cloth in a few hours (Glazer: 27). The size of the cloth is determined by the length of the raffia fibers. After a cloth was woven, it was often softened by wetting and pounding with a mortar.

Cut pile raffia were often worn as wrappers. Cloth could be used to mark a house or family during event of significance, or given as gifts to establish relations of reciprocity. When raffia cloths were worn as skirts, they showed the wearer’s status, wealth and social class. The most luxuriously decorated cloths were the most expensive and valuable. Wealthy people and the king can afford to have these skirts, but not exclusively. If a non-elite person could afford an elaborate skirt, he/she would be entitled to wear it. Fancier cloth does not announce a person’s title, but instead implies their social or economic class (Glazer 1996: 128/129).
