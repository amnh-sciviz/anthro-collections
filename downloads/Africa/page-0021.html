Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret’ (Hassan 1990).

The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques (Hassan). Colonial scholars at the time the Museum acquired this charm did not make any distinction between Muslim and local animist traditions, using the term juju to mean any West African object with religious or magical power. The origin this word is somewhat ambiguous. It may be a descendant of the Mande gru-gru “charm,” or else derived from the french Joujou, “plaything.” In contemporary Nigeria, there is disagreement among politicians, in Christian churches, and among practitioners of traditional medicine as to what exactly the word complicated word juju represents. Nigerian academics, such as Igbo Anthropologist Patrick Iroegbu, are still seeking to untangle the many different meanings surrounding juju among their own peoples. The word "juju" itself is not used without this critical lens in scholarship today, as it has an indistinct meaning, and carries a political stigma that is associated with harmful Eurocentric notions of “primitivism” versus “modernity.” (Iroegbu 2010).

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. This concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout West Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general. It is probably Hausa in origin. The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Among the Hausa, the clerics who use this technology are called Malams, from Arabic mu’alim “scholar.” Malams oversee the Muslim education of a Hausa community. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of Islamic education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. This still continues, despite the lasting cultural impact of the Sokoto Caliphate, with it’s campaign for universal Islamic education, including women. The Malam does not expect regular compensation for their teaching, and regards what they do as a service. However, gifts of unfixed value are traditionally given in the middle of the week by children on behalf of grateful parents. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout West Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general. Although this board most likely was made by a Basa-Kuomo craftsperson, this technology is primarily associated with the Hausa people, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria.

Among the Hausa, the clerics who use this technology are called Malams, from Arabic mu’alim “scholar.” Malams oversee the traditional education of a Hausa community. Hausa Malams are highly regarded by West African Muslims, and often sought after to provide islamic education, both in their communities and abroad. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of this education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. This still continues, despite the lasting cultural impact of the Sokoto Caliphate, with it’s campaign for universal Islamic education, including women. The Malam does not expect regular compensation for their teaching, and regards what they do as a service. However, gifts of unfixed value are traditionally given in the middle of the week by children on behalf of grateful parents. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).

KORAN JUJUS
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 90.0/ 186
Locale: LOKOJA
Country: NIGERIA
Material: HIDE, WOOD
Dimensions: FOLDED L:14 W:9 H:5 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1905 [GIFT]
Donor: BOWDOIN, GEORGE S., HON.
Keywords: JUJU, KORAN
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret’ (Hassan 1990).

The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. (Hassan). Colonial scholars at the time the Museum acquired this charm did not make any distinction between Muslim and local animist traditions, using the term juju to mean any West African object with religious or magical power. The origin this word is somewhat ambiguous. It may be a descendant of the Mande gru-gru “charm,” or else derived from the french Joujou, “plaything.” In contemporary Nigeria, there is disagreement among politicians, in Christian churches, and among practitioners of traditional medicine as to what exactly the word complicated word juju represents. Nigerian academics, such as Igbo Anthropologist Patrick Iroegbu, are still seeking to untangle the many different meanings surrounding juju among their own peoples. The word "juju" itself is not used without this critical lens in scholarship today, as it has an indistinct meaning, and carries a political stigma that is associated with harmful Eurocentric notions of “primitivism” versus “modernity.” (Iroegbu 2010).

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. This concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan).
