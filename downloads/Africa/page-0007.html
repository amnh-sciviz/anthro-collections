Curatorial Notes: This drum, apart of the African Ethnographic Collection, was gifted in 1904 from John Dyneley Prince, a professor at Columbia University, among various other titles, who had received the object directly from Africa in 1902.
For the Ashanti, this particular shape of drum is known as Apentemma, and would have been made of a hollowed out log of a cedar tree, known locally as tweneboa kodua, and the shape and general size of the drum produces a distinct sound (Anku 43). The drumhead of this type of drum is typically made from the skins goat, cow, duiker, or antelope (Anku 44). Drum heads on royal drums of the Akan are sometimes even made out of elephant ear hyde (Anku 44). The pegs of the drum, much like the drum itself, are also made from specific trees, particularly from the branches of the ofemma tree in Akan tradition (Anku 44). It is important to use these types of branches to ensure the sturdiness of the pegs through the strains on them caused by lacing and turning the heads of the drums (Anku 44).
Apentemma drums are one handed drums, meaning only one hand is used in the beating of the drum (Anku 52). The Akan use several different types of stick and hand stroking on their drums, which include muted strokes (very delicate, light strokes that are almost silent), and “stopped strokes (rather loud, usually one stick or hand pressing the drum head while the other strikes at the centre of the head)” (Anku 46). In traditional Akan drumming, different phonetic syllables are correlated with the different sounds a drum produces. For the Apentemma drum in particular, open tone hand drum sounds are associated with the pe-dem syllables, whereas muted toned hand drumming sounds more like pa-ta (Anku 48). As well as the use of different phonetic syllables, the Akan also employ “running parts” in their drumming of the Apentemma which consists of short and fast repeated rhythms (Anku 51).
The ensemble in which the Akan arrange their instruments creates a distinct flow of different pitches. The Akan recognize the lower pitches that larger drums make, and thus organize their ensembles in a ranked order of sounds from highest to lowest (Anku 40). This drum in particular would likely be placed somewhere towards the end of the ensemble for its larger size and mid to lower pitch. Drums, however, are not the only instrument present within these ensembles. Other instruments that may accompany them include bells, rattles, stick clappers (idiophones), and a combination of drums other than this one (the Apentemma) (Anku 40). Women often accompany the ensemble through singing in chorus.

Curatorial Notes: The knobkerrie is a weapon consisting of a long narrow shaft and a spherical head. It can be carved from a single piece of wood, but it may also be formed from rhinoceros horn. The head and shaft have varying forms and lengths. See 1/7025, 90.2/1090 and 90.2/7871 D for other examples. The knobkerrie is a common and effective instrument used during wartime. Warriors can be seen wearing a leather belt, which would hold the knobkerrie and sword in place when not in use. Knobkerries are also used while herding cattle (Hollis 1905: 320). The knobkerrie, along with a shield and a spear, completes a warrior’s regalia.

Knobkerries can be used for other purposes as well. For example, a warrior might use a knobkerrie as a sort of staff to effectively deliver a speech. He may raise or lower the object depending on the tone of his voice. When he is done speaking, the knobkerrie is passed onto the next speaker (Hinde 1901: 33). It is a representation of power and respect, as well as intimidation.

Curatorial Notes: The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist cultural group who live in both Kenya and Tanzania along the Rift Valley. Historically, the style and form has changed throughout time, however the symbolism and social context is relatively constant. The moran warriors are young Maasai men responsible for herding, protecting, and capturing cattle. They use their spears in throwing competitions to show off their prowess. The spear is used for decorative purposes as well; it is part of the warrior’s daily dress. It is iconic of the Maasai because of their violent reputation for raids and ceremonial lion kills. Spears made for sale in the tourism industry are never used or functional, and are conveniently adjustable to fit inside a traveler’s suitcase.

The spear can have multiple meanings depending on the age-grade. Brand new spears are considered appropriate for Maasai warriors. As they begin to become worn and aged however, the spears are given to elders and young boys. When they become even more worn out, the spears are given to younger boys and older elders. A Maasai carrying an aged spear symbolizes his non-warrior status. Each age-grade has its own stylistic preferences; therefore the style of spear is reminiscent of a specific group of warriors. They also adopt innovations from other ethnic groups.

The spears are made out of wood or metal often with an attached ball of fur, in this case, lion skin is attached at the base. The Maasai are barred from blacksmithing because their cultural norms forbid anyone to break the earth. Instead, the Maasai trade with the nearby agriculturalist Kikuyu who desire the Maasai’s animal skins. The Kikuyu call the spears itimu and have adopted the spear. It is said that their trade network is so essential that during wartime, traders are still promised to be able to enter the enemy’s cultural boundary unharmed.

Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret.' The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques.

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan 1990).
