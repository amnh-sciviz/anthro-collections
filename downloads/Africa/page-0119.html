Curatorial Notes: The Yoruba peoples are located in southwestern Nigeria and southern Benin and are one of the largest ethnic groups in West Africa They were not a unified political state, but rather bands of interconnected independent city-states sharing a common language and culture.

The dundun is a bi-membraphonic unipercussive drum struck with a curved stick called a gongo. The gongo is carved from either the celtis soyauxii tree or synsepalum dulcificum plants, while the body of the drum is usually carved from the cordia millenii tree (King 1961: 10). The Dundun is an umbrella term for drums of different sizes (pitches/tones) that all adhere to the following physical attributes: Two heads of equal size made from animal hide that are located on each end of a wooden hourglass shaped body. These heads are connected by up to one-hundred tension (cords/straps), called ogon (King 1961: 10) that the player can tighten by squeezing the drum with his arm against his body in order to modify the drums pitch. The two membranes and the tension cords are made from either male goat or ram hide (King 1961: 10). Types of dundun drums include the iya ilu, the mother drum, the gudugudu, the kanago, the isaju, and the aguda. Object 90.1/ 9777 is an example of a gudugudu drum.

This drum is an iya ilu, meaning mother drum, because it is the loudest and most important dundun. It is differentiated from other dundun by its size and the small bell-shaped brass shells that are sewn along the leather border of the adorned cloth. The iya ilu is carried on the left shoulder by a an embellished leather strap called an agbeko. While the drum is bi-membraphonic, only the front membrane is beaten.

In order to play the iya ilu, the musician must be a master drummer, a title given when he has proven his capabilities both as an individual drummer and as a drum orchestra leader. The preponderance of master drummers are born into drumming families, or have fathers who are master drummers. These drumming families are known collectively as the House of Ayan, an allusion to/in homage of the drum deity Ayan; drumming families often add Ayan to their family name. (Bankole 1976: 25)

Curatorial Notes: This hide-bound Qur’an (original spelling: "Koran") text was obtained in the Hausa center of Kano, Nigeria. The handwritten copying of books, even those not considered scripture, is still said to be a pious act for highly trained scholars, and the resulting manuscripts are prized by the Hausa (Hassan 1990).

The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1997). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

The earliest spread of Islam in Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco (Greenberg). The Muslim cultures of the Maghreb (Northwestern Africa above the Sahara) have exerted a great deal of influence ever since. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. The religion was mostly restricted to urban areas, among a literate elite which preserved older styles of written Arabic. (Greenberg, Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This prayer mat was made by a member of the Hausa, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

Researcher: Gabriel Chance Sevigny (2018)
