AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 90.1/ 2746
Culture: MANGBETU, MEDJE
Locale: RUNGU
Country: CONGO (BELGIAN CONGO)
Material: WOOD, CORD
Dimensions: L:16.5 W:1.3 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1915 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: LANG, HERBERT
Keywords: TOP, TOY
Category: RECREATIONAL ARTIFACTS
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Field Notes: [One of] Two tops used by children of the MANGBETU and also the MEDJE. To make it spin proceed as follows: the string is put through the hole of the second stick so they are placed together. The end of the string is placed up, but also downward. The longer end of the top and the string is then from below or above rolled up or downward close together until the two sticks meet. The stick at the end of the string is held in the left or sometimes right hand, the other stick with the hole in its upper end is held by the right (or left) hand at right angle with the other so the crossed sticks hold the top in position pushed off rapidly that sets the top spinning with a loud buzzing noise. They say it is a rather recent invention of their children (probably introduced from some other tribe) and call it MEHEDE or MAYEHEDE. The fruit also used as a clapper for children is from a bush called ABASINGA. NOTE: Sometimes 10 or more children may be seen to amuse themselves on the open place of their villages. With these tops the loudest [illeg] is considered the most [illeg].

Field Notes: NEKUDWE. A bow with a sort of resonant appliance of wood called by the MANGBETU 'EMANGBE.' The EMANGBE is carried inside the curvature of the bow but may turn around as the arrows are shot off. It is always at the right hand side (if the bow is held vertically) or above. Every time an arrow is shot it makes a harsh sound. In fighting their enemies they often let the string loose without shooting an arrow as usually all tribes bend themselves down at the very hearing of this sound and rest motionless. They profit of this absolute motionlessness in placing their arrows. The wrist cushion of the Pygmies, also an arrangement of folded leaves about the wrist serve the same purpose. [The objects inscribed from No. 809 to No. 908 have been presented by Monsieur Charles Gilson, Chef de Poste de RUNGU. rue Theodore Vorhaogon 69, St. Gilles - Bruxelles.]
