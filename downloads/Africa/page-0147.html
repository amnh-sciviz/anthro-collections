Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout West Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general. It is probably Hausa in origin. The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Among the Hausa, the clerics who use this technology are called Malams, from Arabic mu’alim “scholar.” Malams oversee the Muslim education of a Hausa community. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of Islamic education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. This still continues, despite the lasting cultural impact of the Sokoto Caliphate, with it’s campaign for universal Islamic education, including women. The Malam does not expect regular compensation for their teaching, and regards what they do as a service. However, gifts of unfixed value are traditionally given in the middle of the week by children on behalf of grateful parents. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This bag was made by a member of the Hausa, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. The model allowed Muslim clergy to pursue other trades as well. Groups like the Hausa and Mande, who rapidly adopted Islam, possessed a monopoly over certain industries like iron and textiles in the region. This put skilled members of their religion, who were knowledgeable enough to act as missionaries, in high demand among other communities. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa ’ulama, called malams, service a variety of non-Muslim groups in Nigeria.

Hausa manuscripts are typically bound as loose paper in folio. To provide a sense of organization, malam authors utilize Matashi, as system wherein the first word of next page located at lower corner of the preceding page. This allows for continued recital as well as keeping unbound pages in order. A folio is kept between wood, cardboard, or paper boards, and tied with string. It is then bound in tadarishi, a four-folded, quality red orange or brown leather, commissioned individually with an incised pattern. The finished manuscript is kept in a leather bag called a gafaka. Leatherworkers are often literate, sometimes malams or ex-students. Limited literacy is considered a mark of quality and professional integration, and most of the technical expertise come from written classical sources. Variations on this model include specially made binding, or cloth sacks for transportation (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This penis sheath is part of the Dodo costume that members of the Dodo religion learn to weave during the kumasu, the ceremony that marks initiation of young males into Dodo religion membership. Initiates, or yara, are told to wear these sheaths upon emerging from the seclusion they must undergo during kumasu.
During a 1957-1959 expedition to Dass District, northern Nigeria, researcher Francis P. Conant collected hundreds of objects from the Barawa people of the Dass Plateau in northeast Nigeria. With the exception of a minority of Christian and Muslim converts, the Barawa follow the Dodo religion, which, along with the mountainous topography of the area they inhabit, both joins the Barawa under a common thread and yet also separates them.
Dodo religion centers around an ancestral spirit called Dodo, and the individual’s lifetime journey towards attaining the state of being Dodo. One’s progress towards Dodo really begins at birth, but is officially marked during the kumasu, the ceremony that marks initiation of young males into Dodo membership. The progress ends after one’s funeral rites are completed, upon which one is “fully” Dodo. While this is the general principle behind Dodo religion, the fluid nature of Dodo facilitates individualized ways of practicing the religion, leading to the rise of competing rival “cult” groups that serve to undermine Barawa political and social unity.
Conant’s research emphasized the plasticity and practicality of Dodo philosophy, a characteristic that he believed accounted for the religion’s longevity in such an ethnically and religiously diverse region: “At every level, conceptual, organizational, and technological, mountaineer religion may be characterized best as being eminently pragmatic, changed and changing according to the demands of its practitioners. That these demands are few – the mountaineer being a resolutely independent kind of person and committed to an ethic of "self-help” – perhaps largely accounts for the persistence through time of such concepts as do exist.”

Curatorial Notes: This metal bracelet is part of the regalia worn by initiates, or yara, into Dodo religion membership upon emerging from the seclusion they must undergo during the initiation ceremony, kumasu.
During a 1957-1959 expedition to Dass District, northern Nigeria, researcher Francis P. Conant collected hundreds of objects from the Barawa people of the Dass Plateau in northeast Nigeria. With the exception of a minority of Christian and Muslim converts, the Barawa follow the Dodo religion, which, along with the mountainous topography of the area they inhabit, both joins the Barawa under a common thread and yet also separates them.
Dodo religion centers around an ancestral spirit called Dodo, and the individual’s lifetime journey towards attaining the state of being Dodo. One’s progress towards Dodo really begins at birth, but is officially marked during the kumasu, the ceremony that marks initiation of young males into Dodo membership. The progress ends after one’s funeral rites are completed, upon which one is “fully” Dodo. While this is the general principle behind Dodo religion, the fluid nature of Dodo facilitates individualized ways of practicing the religion, leading to the rise of competing rival “cult” groups that serve to undermine Barawa political and social unity.
Conant’s research emphasized the plasticity and practicality of Dodo philosophy, a characteristic that he believed accounted for the religion’s longevity in such an ethnically and religiously diverse region: “At every level, conceptual, organizational, and technological, mountaineer religion may be characterized best as being eminently pragmatic, changed and changing according to the demands of its practitioners. That these demands are few – the mountaineer being a resolutely independent kind of person and committed to an ethic of "self-help” – perhaps largely accounts for the persistence through time of such concepts as do exist.”

Researcher: In Hae Yap, Science Research Mentorship Program student, 2014.


Sources
Conant, Francis P. "Dodo of Dass, a Study of a Pagan Religion of Northern Nigeria." Ph.D. diss., Columbia University, 1960.

One such form of Dodo is called Jankai, which is generally seen as a fearsome spirit, unlike any other form of Dodo. The headdress is created by covering a basket headpiece in sticky gum arabic, or acacia tree sap, and then covered in the red beans idon zakara, or literally “cock’s eye”, which lend Jankai its name (Jankai means “red head” in Barawa). Fragments of reflective objects, such as mirror shards, are also placed in the gum so that there are rapid flashes when Jankai dancers, notorious for their self-injurious and often dangerous dances, spin.
