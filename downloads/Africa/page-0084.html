Field Notes: ABILI - the leaves with which they clean their bodies. The green leaves are put into the water and then two or three of them laid on the fingers, slightly surpassing the tips, the stem is held fast with the thumb. Only the underside of the leaves touches the skin as they rub slowly one place after another. Used by all ages and sexes. The bush is cultivated by the Mangbetu in and near the villages. Sidenote: Used also to rub off [illeg: scarification?], and as toilet paper by the Mangbetu of Niangara. the women there very often a bunch in the hollow of their stools.

Field Notes: Stones of palm nuts. When dried they crush them and take out the kernels and [illeg: rather burn?] them to make black oil to grease their bodies (See description under No. 262). One of the three shells (of a fruit) on the same bundle are usually dropped into the oil jar and used as a small receptacle to take out a few drops upon their palms. They always [illeg: swim?] on the surface of the oil.
