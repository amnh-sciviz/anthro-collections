Curatorial Notes: The San Complex -- The Bow

The San peoples live throughout the Kalahari plains in Southern Africa, including parts of Botswana, Namibia, Angola, Zambia, Zimbabwe and South Africa, and are well known for the continuation of their hunting and gathering traditions.

A San hunting bow such as the one exhibited above is typically crafted from a flexible shaft of the bush known as Grewia flava or Grewia bicolor. This wooden piece is carved and smoothed with an iron knife, then repeatedly heated in hot sand as it is gently bent to induce a curvature using the hands and knee. If the shaft displays the slightest flaw or crack during this process, it is discarded and the hunter must begin again.

The bow is tightly bound with thongs at both ends, which support a string that is created from long strips of sinew from the back muscles of gemsbok or other antelope. Holding ten to fifteen centimeter strands of wet sinew, the hunter rolls them into twine with the heel of his hand until a uniformly thick bowstring about eighty centimeters long is constructed, which is then finished off with a loop.

This wet string is attached to the bow with a knot while its pre-formed loop is pulled over and seated above the thong at the other end. As the bowstring dries, it shrinks under pressure to the point where the assembly achieves a taut, satisfactory pull. The bow is completed when it has been ritually strengthened with the blood of a freshly-killed antelope and with proper maintenance, the hunting bow will have a useful life for many years (Lee 1979).

Researcher: Siu Ying Ng, 2014

Curatorial Notes: The San Complex -- The Arrow

The Khoisan (aka San) peoples live throughout the Kalahari plains in Southern Africa, including parts of Botswana, Namibia, Angola, Zambia, Zimbabwe and South Africa, and are well known for the continuation of their hunting and gathering traditions.

The San living mainly in southern Angola and northeastern Namibia have been known to use hunting arrows made of feathered wooden shafts and socketed metal triangular points such as the one displayed in the exhibit above. The arrows resemble, in fact, those attributed to neighboring Bantu peoples, which suggest that these type of San hunting arrows may have been modeled after their weapons or obtained from them by trade.

Traditional hunting arrows crafted by the San typically have main shafts produced from the stems of perennial grass called Phragmites australis. The shaft’s end is placed into a bone link carved from the humerus or femur of giraffe, kudu or gemsbok. This link shaft is attached to a metal arrowhead through a joint constructed from the hollow section of grass stem, and all three pieces are tightly bound together with sinew to create a permanent assembly. Until a century ago, the arrowhead and link were once a single unit carved of bone and today this vintage composite has been seen in use for hunting birds. In fact, the link shaft is still carved from bone; however, the arrowhead is hammered from iron fencing wire that has been heated and straightened; the tip forged into a small, flat triangular blade.

The finished arrow is checked and rechecked for balance, strength and straightness and if the component is correctly assembled, an animal will only be able to extract the main shaft from its wound while the metal arrowhead remains embedded. Thus the link shaft design renders the animal incapable of removing the head, for instance, by rubbing it against a tree.

What equips the San bow and arrow with its formidable killing power is the arrow poison: a potent toxin obtained from the pupal forms of chrysomelid beetles (specifically, Diamphidia nigro-ornata Stal, Diamphidia vittati-pennis Baly and Polyclada flexuosa Baly). After the grubs have been retrieved from underground where they live in the pupal stage, they are crushed and mixed into a thin orange paste that is then applied to the arrowhead. Living pupae are wrapped in a piece of cloth or leather and used as needed, its poisonous power enduring for months. This potency is very high as a small quantity of fresh poison in the bloodstream is enough to kill or seriously injure a person, and tipped with full-strength poison, an arrow can kill a large antelope in six to twenty-four hours.

The San quiver carries a hunter’s arrows, supply of blanks and oftentimes, a pair of fire drills (tools developed explicitly for creating fire: one stick made of hardwood with a pointed end and the other of softwood with a notch for tinder, Marshall Thomas 2006); the quiver on display, which contains six hunting arrows (Catalog #90.2/1126 B through G), is constructed from rawhide and styled with a strap and cuff as a leather strip is laced down its side.

The traditional hunting quiver crafted by the San is typically molded from a sleeve of bark taken from a tree’s heavy, thick roots (preferably Acacia hebeclada, Acacia tortilis or Acacia luederitzii) and covered by hide at either end. The hunter searches for a segment of root that is smooth, straight and at least sixty centimeters in length and seven to thirteen centimeters in diameter. The tree root is chopped free at its upper and lower ends in which the severed section is heated and pounded until the bark slides off the wood as a hollow cylinder.

The quiver’s circumference is then reinforced by leather rings cut from the leg hide of an antelope. These rings are slipped over the top of the sleeve and spaced along the quiver’s length to provide added strength against splitting. Finally the bottom is capped with leather formed from the hide of an antelope’s kneecap or scrotal sac. Usually a removable hood is secured to the top by a thong and with the attachment of a leather shoulder strap, the hunting quiver is completed.

The shaft’s end is placed into a bone link carved from the humerus or femur of giraffe, kudu or gemsbok. This link shaft is attached to a metal arrowhead through a joint constructed from the hollow section of grass stem, and all three pieces are tightly bound together with sinew to create a permanent assembly. Until a century ago, the arrowhead and link were once a single unit carved of bone and today this vintage composite has been seen in use for hunting birds. In fact, the link shaft is still carved from bone; however, the arrowhead is hammered from iron fencing wire that has been heated and straightened; the tip forged into a small, flat triangular blade.

The finished arrow is checked and rechecked for balance, strength and straightness and if the component is correctly assembled, an animal will only be able to extract the main shaft from its wound while the metal arrowhead remains embedded. Thus the link shaft design renders the animal incapable of removing the head, for instance, by rubbing it against a tree.

What equips the San bow and arrow with its formidable killing power is the arrow poison: a potent toxin obtained from the pupal forms of chrysomelid beetles (specifically, Diamphidia nigro-ornata Stal, Diamphidia vittati-pennis Baly and Polyclada flexuosa Baly). After the grubs have been retrieved from underground where they live in the pupal stage, they are crushed and mixed into a thin orange paste that is then applied to the arrowhead. Living pupae are wrapped in a piece of cloth or leather and used as needed, its poisonous power enduring for months. This potency is very high as a small quantity of fresh poison in the bloodstream is enough to kill or seriously injure a person, and tipped with full-strength poison, an arrow can kill a large antelope in six to twenty-four hours.

Curatorial Notes: The San Complex -- The Arrow

The Khoisan (aka San) live throughout the Kalahari plains in Southern Africa, including parts of Botswana, Namibia, Angola, Zambia, Zimbabwe and South Africa, and are well known for the continuation of their hunting and gathering traditions.

The San living mainly in southern Angola and northeastern Namibia have been known to use hunting arrows made of feathered wooden shafts and socketed metal triangular points such as the one displayed above (the arrowhead is missing). The arrows resemble, in fact, those attributed to neighboring Bantu peoples, which suggest that these type of San hunting arrows may have been modeled after their weapons or obtained from them by trade.

Traditional hunting arrows crafted by the San typically have main shafts produced from the stems of perennial grass called Phragmites australis. The shaft’s end is placed into a bone link carved from the humerus or femur of giraffe, kudu or gemsbok. This link shaft is attached to a metal arrowhead through a joint constructed from the hollow section of grass stem, and all three pieces are tightly bound together with sinew to create a permanent assembly. Until a century ago, the arrowhead and link were once a single unit carved of bone and today this vintage composite has been seen in use for hunting birds. In fact, the link shaft is still carved from bone; however, the arrowhead is hammered from iron fencing wire that has been heated and straightened; the tip forged into a small, flat triangular blade.

The finished arrow is checked and rechecked for balance, strength and straightness and if the component is correctly assembled, an animal will only be able to extract the main shaft from its wound while the metal arrowhead remains embedded. Thus the link shaft design renders the animal incapable of removing the head, for instance, by rubbing it against a tree.

Curatorial Notes: The San Complex -- The Quiver

The Khoisan (aka San) live throughout the Kalahari plains in Southern Africa, including parts of Botswana, Namibia, Angola, Zambia, Zimbabwe and South Africa, and are well known for the continuation of their hunting and gathering traditions.

The San quiver carries a hunter’s arrows, supply of blanks and oftentimes, a pair of fire drills (tools developed explicitly for creating fire: one stick made of hardwood with a pointed end and the other of softwood with a notch for tinder, Marshall Thomas 2006); the quiver exhibited in the photograph above is constructed from leather and styled with a two-strand twisted thong strap and cuff as a leather strip is laced down its side.

The traditional hunting quiver crafted by the San is typically molded from a sleeve of bark taken from a tree’s heavy, thick roots (preferably Acacia hebeclada, Acacia tortilis or Acacia luederitzii) and covered by hide at either end. The hunter searches for a segment of root that is smooth, straight and at least sixty centimeters in length and seven to thirteen centimeters in diameter. The tree root is chopped free at its upper and lower ends in which the severed section is heated and pounded until the bark slides off the wood as a hollow cylinder.

The quiver’s circumference is then reinforced by leather rings cut from the leg hide of an antelope. These rings are slipped over the top of the sleeve and spaced along the quiver’s length to provide added strength against splitting. Finally the bottom is capped with leather formed from the hide of an antelope’s kneecap or scrotal sac. Usually a removable hood is secured to the top by a thong and with the attachment of a leather shoulder strap, the hunting quiver is completed.

(For information regarding other San complex components, please review Catalog #s 90.2/1125A or 1/5579 for the hunting arrow and its poison, and Catalog #90.2/1122 for the hunting bow; all of these objects are on display in the African Peoples Hall).
