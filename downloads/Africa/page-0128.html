Culture: YORUBA

Donor: BASCOM, WILLIAM R., DR.
Keywords: FIGURE

Curatorial Notes: These pair of figures or staffs cast in brass are known as edan (edan Ogboni), the prime insignia of the Ogboni Society. The Ogboni is a powerful religious Yoruba secret association which is formed by the oldest and wisest elders of a town. They are equal in moral and political authority to the King (Oni) and are responsible for his selection and installation. The staffs are joined by a chain attached to loops on the tops of their heads, allowing them to be worn around the neck.

One figure is male and the other female, who is shown here holding her breasts, symbolizing the traditional gender role of providing nourishment and care; both exhibit characteristic Yoruba features of almond-shaped eyes with heavy eyelids, flared nostrils and defined lips. Indeed, the edan wield considerable force and are markers of high status and power. Recognized for its judicial function, the society judges and punishes severe crimes. Should the Ogboni fail to arrive at a decision, the edan are placed in the ground by their spikes and will fall if a person is deemed guilty.

Researchers: Emily Fite and Siu Ying Ng, 2013

Curatorial Notes: These pair of figures or staffs cast in brass are known as edan (edan Ogboni), the prime insignia of the Ogboni Society. The Ogboni is a powerful religious Yoruba secret association which is formed by the oldest and wisest elders of a town. They are equal in moral and political authority to the King (Oni) and are responsible for his selection and installation. The staffs are joined by a chain attached to loops on the tops of their heads, allowing them to be worn around the neck. The seated figure is male and the kneeling form is female; each of them holds an animal which appears to signify symbolic sacrifices.

Both sculptures exhibit characteristic Yoruba features of almond-shaped eyes with heavy eyelids, flared nostrils and defined lips. Indeed, the edan wield considerable force and are markers of high status and power. Recognized for its judicial function, the society judges and punishes severe crimes. Should the Ogboni fail to arrive at a decision, the edan are placed in the ground by their spikes and will fall if a person is deemed guilty.

Curatorial Notes: Divining trays are religious objects that showcase the artistic richness of Yoruba art. Contemporary Yoruba are inheritors of an artistic tradition that remains influential in their daily life. (Drewal, Pemberton and Abiodun 1989). Most of the trays have a circular shape and are made of wood, sometimes containing metal. The images and/or patterns observed around the circumference of the trays often represent Ifa (method of divination) verses that serve as a reminder to the diviner and those seeking knowledge. The majority of the divination trays contain an image of Esu (Exu, Elegba, Elegbara). As Elegba or Elegbara, he is the owner of the crossroads, including those that lead to the spiritual realm. Due to this, during Ifa divination, he is present as a figure of neutral power between good and evil. Esu is always present in all divining matters. In the article “Art and Oracle: Spirit Voices of Africa,” Alisa LaGamma defines “to divine” as a method of foretelling future events and/or to reveal undisclosed information by supernatural mediums. In many cases, these mediums are inanimate objects used by an experienced consultant to gain access to hidden knowledge. (LaGamma 2000: 53).

The Yoruba people are located in the southeastern part of Nigeria and are considered the most populous tribe on the African continent. The origin of this cultural group is largely understood through oral history. Most information is consistent that the Yoruba migrated from Egypt and its surrounding areas. The dating of artifacts suggests that this migration happened before the twelfth century. (Stoll, Stoll and Klever 1980).

In its earliest years, the Yoruba kingdom was comprised of a large federation of affiliated chiefdoms. These chiefdoms were ruled by the Alafin, who was an influential ruler of the city of Oyo. According to Yoruba historian Samuel Jackson, the fall of the city of Oyo, in 1930, was the event that marked the erosion of Yoruba culture and traditions. The British colonial period of Nigeria began in 1886 and lasted until 1963, when Nigeria became an independent republic. There was a short period of military rule from 1966 to 1979, when the nation returned to being ruled by the people. (Stoll, Stoll and Klever 1980).

The religious doctrine of traditional Yoruba culture, which is still influential today, emphasizes reincarnation and the eternal soul. They believe that their moral decisions and actions are judged after their death and determine their circumstances in their next life on earth. (Stoll, Stoll, and Klever 1980). These principles help motivate their practices as one of many cultural groups that continue to utilize divination in order to understand, explain, and predict issues important to their lives.

Curatorial Notes: Exhibited above is an Ifa Divination Tray (opon Ifa), a wooden oval board covered in wood dust that is used within Yoruba rituals. The board is carved in high relief with four kneeling figures and a geometric motif around its rim drawn from the Yoruba cosmic world. Ifa, also known as Orunmila, is the God of Divination and this complex system of foresight is sought by those who seek solutions to serious problems in which the diviner/priest (babalawo) consults the deity, serving as an intermediary between the spiritual and physical worlds. During this divine process, the babalawo manipulates sixteen palm nuts within his hands which slowly “reveal” a numerical pattern that is then recorded in the wood dust. He recites associated stories or verses correlating with that pattern and relevant to the client’s dilemma, along with any prescribed sacrifices, to ensure a desired outcome or to avert an impending misfortune. The face of mischievous Eshu (also known as Elegba), a close friend of Ifa as well as the divine messenger and embodiment of the crossroads, is symbolized on the tray as the babalawo positions himself directly across from his image.

Curatorial Notes: This miniature brass bow and arrow is found on shrines to Ososi, the deity of hunters. Made out of one piece of metal, it is not made to be used but instead made specifically for use on shrines. One of the largest ethnic groups south of the Sahara, the Yoruba consists of many subgroups that are diverse but have significant linguistic and cultural similarities. Highly urbanized before colonization, Yoruba kingdoms varied in size. Each Yoruba kingdom is organized around a sacred hereditary king or oba that is descendant of one of the sixteen sons of Odua, the creator of the Earth. The oba and the Ogboni, a council of elder chiefs, hold the political authority over each kingdom. Each city-state in Yorubaland was affiliated with a certain patron deity.
