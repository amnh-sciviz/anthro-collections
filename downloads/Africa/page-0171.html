Curatorial Notes: This beaded basket with a lid, along with unbeaded finely made ditto, was made by royal women among the Watutsi in Central Africa. The 2.5 million Watutsi people comprise the second largest ethnic group in Rwanda and Burundi. "The Tutsi adopted the mutually intelligible Bantu languages Rwanda and Rundi, which were originally spoken by the Hutu." They had a monarchy until 1961, when Belgium got rid of the king (the mwami). - "Tutsi (people)" 2011. Britannica Encyclopedia.

Curatorial Notes: This cover was made by a member of the Hausa, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. The model allowed Muslim clergy to pursue other trades as well. Groups like the Hausa and Mande, who rapidly adopted Islam, possessed a monopoly over certain industries like iron and textiles in the region. This put skilled members of their religion, who were knowledgeable enough to act as missionaries, in high demand among other communities. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa ’ulama, called malams, service a variety of non-Muslim groups in Nigeria.

Hausa manuscripts are typically bound as loose paper in folio. To provide a sense of organization, malam authors utilize Matashi, as system wherein the first word of next page located at lower corner of the preceding page. This allows for continued recital as well as keeping unbound pages in order. A folio is kept between wood, cardboard, or paper boards, and tied with string. It is then bound in tadarishi, a four-folded, quality red orange or brown leather, commissioned individually with an incised pattern. The finished manuscript is kept in a leather bag called a gafaka. Leatherworkers are often literate, sometimes malams or ex-students. Limited literacy is considered a mark of quality and professional integration, and most of the technical expertise come from written classical sources. Variations on this model include specially made binding, or cloth sacks for transportation (Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This portfolio was made by a member of the Hausa, one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan 1990), who occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim.

Early missionaries in West Africa were Arab and Berber traders from the north who, due to a lack of an organized, segregated priesthood in Islam, held a dual religious/economic role as ‘ulama (scholars) and merchants. The model allowed Muslim clergy to pursue other trades as well. Groups like the Hausa and Mande, who rapidly adopted Islam, possessed a monopoly over certain industries like iron and textiles in the region. This put skilled members of their religion, who were knowledgeable enough to act as missionaries, in high demand among other communities. Islam and its trappings have historically carried a high degree of both scholarly and “Magico-religious” prestige in West Africa, and Hausa ’ulama, called malams, service a variety of non-Muslim groups in Nigeria.

Hausa manuscripts are typically bound as loose paper in folio. To provide a sense of organization, malam authors utilize Matashi, as system wherein the first word of next page located at lower corner of the preceding page. This allows for continued recital as well as keeping unbound pages in order. A folio is kept between wood, cardboard, or paper boards, and tied with string. It is then bound in tadarishi, a four-folded, quality red orange or brown leather, commissioned individually with an incised pattern. The finished manuscript is kept in a leather bag called a gafaka. Leatherworkers are often literate, sometimes malams or ex-students. Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

Ivory horns were vital instruments for Mangbetu royalty; ivory has long been held as a highly prized material. The use of this precious ivory and the special labor involved in its production firmly linked ivory horns with the power of the king. Ivory horn production was delegated to exceptional smiths who used metals tools to carve the instrument from an animal tusk; these smiths also carved decorative images and motifs on the exterior of the horn, as seen on object 90.1/ 1794. Herbert Lang, head of the Museum’s 1915 Congo expedition during which these horns were collected, noted that many of these horns were adorned with an anthropomorphic carving on the mouthpiece, a decorative motif also found on Mangbetu harps. The length of the horns could be extended by attaching a wooden tube to the larger end of the instrument, a device that also deepened the sound of the horn. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 202)

Ivory horn orchestras were primarily employed during court dances, such as the mabolo dance, in which the king danced alone in front of his subjects order to display his skill. The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the king’s prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).
