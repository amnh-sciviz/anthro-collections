Curatorial Notes: This cast iron C-shaped ring with flared terminations is called a manilla. Originally worn as decorative bracelets and armlets, manillas (okpoho) were also referred to as penannular bracelet currency as they transformed into a traditional exchange medium throughout West Africa until their replacement by western currency towards the end of the nineteenth century. Constructed mainly in copper (regarded as African red gold), bronze and brass, manillas held different values based upon their specific type, composition and market location.

Manillas eventually became known as slave trade money as these objects were being used by Europeans to acquire the enslaved, becoming the main currency underpinning the slave marketplace. King and Queen manillas, however, were not intended for common trade and these pieces were considerable, being much heavier and bulkier than those used as currency. The manilla exhibited above is known as a Queen, which is smaller and lighter than its King companion (displayed in 90.2/4472). This Queen is incised with a geometric design on one side as opposed to both sides of the King, and they were thought to function as a store of wealth for nobility or possess a ceremonial purpose at funerals.

Researchers: Emily Fite and Siu Ying Ng, 2013
