Keywords: TOY TRUCK
Category: RECREATIONAL ARTIFACTS
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: Across the continent, wire toys, such as this Truck from Senegal, are an exuberant and efficient way of fabricating children's toys. These toys are made from a variety of recycled materials such as scrap metal, metal rods, rubber bands, tin cans, and many others (Deregowski). In the past, toys such as these tended to be made out of wood; however, with increasing availability of wire power cables from formal and informal electrical grids and other infrastructure projects, metal wire has become an increasingly popular option. The wire’s malleability allows for many different design options. Creating cars and toys out of scrap materials popular with children for their own recreational use, but it is also encouraged by schools with many different levels of competition (Donlon).

While these toys were originally exclusively for personal play, artisans have started to produce and sell wire toys to tourists. These cars tend to be more elaborate, using craft workshop techniques such as welding to create profitable, albeit less play-friendly finished products. These toys also tend to not be made from as many recycled materials with cars and other figures being “entirely created in formed wire represent[ing] a style largely, if not solely, market driven by tourism pressure in Southern Africa” (Donlon, 49). Not only is there a lack of recycled materials, but wire toys produced for the tourist market may also include not recycled materials, utilizing mass produced parts like toy car wheels. Toy cars produced for trade also tend to evoke specific car brands in their shape and stylistic construction that are more commonly associated with those found in foreign rather than domestic markets (Donlon). Donlon states that these figures marketed towards tourists are a medium between the wooden toys of the past and the and the recycled toys that have come into popularity over the past years. On one end the wooden toys tend to be viewed as priceless by both locals and visitors. On the other hand, the wire toys used by kids are not as marketable to a tourist market. This creates room for artisans to create their own styles in order to appeal to tourists.

Researchers: Thomas Barker & Jacklyn Grace Lacey, 2019


Sources

Deregowski, J. B. “Wire Toys in Africa South of the Sahara.” Leonardo, 3rd ed., vol. 13, The MIT Press, 1980, pp. 207–208.

Donlon, Jon. “Tradition and Change in the Wire Toys of Southern Africa.” Studies in Popular Culture, 1st ed., vol. 22, Popular Culture Association in the South, 1999, pp. 43–51.
