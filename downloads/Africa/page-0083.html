Field Notes: NAHDRU. (The 'u' is hardly audible) The band around a childs head to produce the gradual lengthening of the occipital portion. The band is put on the very day of the child's birth after the first bath with lukewarm water has been given, it is often taken off at irregular intervals which are determined by their respective mothers. The mothers I asked told me if the child strampels [sic] much and becomes restless the bandage is taken off. The children very often sleep with it, the bandage is seldom left longer than 48 hours. The child's head is always well oiled before putting the bandage on also after taking it off, usually the bandage is left off a day. It is not scare [sic] that sores are produced if they become of a more serious nature the bandage is left off entirely also in all cases of illness. So far as may be judged from the adult persons who enjoy the privilege of such a deformed head, it does not influence the mental abilities in any way, the children however who undergo this treatment have a very peculiar staring look which disappears very rapidly if the bandage is taken off, though as a matter of fact they cannot feel molested by it as they never touch or scratch it, never try to remove the bandage. They always stated that it was very pleasing to have such a long head, there is evidently no tradition about its actual introduction, it is certainly admired by both sexes. Curatorial Notes: The necklace shown above was made from iron beads, copper pendants, and plant fiber. It was made by the Mangbetu people of the Congo during the era of Belgian colonial control. The Mangbetu people live in many different parts of the northern Congo, which is located in central Africa. This piece was collected in Medje, a village in the northeastern Congo, during an American Museum of Natural History expedition in 1915.
Mangbetu jewelry is often worn for protection from witches and bad luck, as well as adornment by both men and women. The plant fiber in the necklace is likely woven banana leaf. The iron beads and copper pendants on this necklace were crafted by a local specialized blacksmith. In Mangbetu culture, certain parts of animals, such as hair, teeth, and bone, acquire the powers of the animal, which can then be transferred to humans who wear or possess the body parts. The copper on this necklace has been made to look like leopards’ teeth, perhaps to bring some of the element of the leopard’s power to the necklace’s owner, and, since the leopard is also a status symbol, to show their high status in the society. Iron is also a symbol of high status, although metal is not believed to hold spiritual power on its own. This necklace was probably crafted for someone of especially high importance in the community, such as the chief of Medje or his wife. (Schildkrout 1990).
In the early 18th century, the Mangbetu culture consisted of several small clans, who often came into contact with northward-migrating Bantu tribes. By the late 18th century, the Mabiti clan took control of the other Mangbetu clans and some neighboring Bantu tribes as well. They did this by taking control of several neighboring clans, and combining that into one large clan. The political power of this federation enabled further expansion, and soon the Mangbetu kingdom was born. The Mangbetu kingdom lasted two generations, but their influence was so prominent in the region that their culture, language, and traditions are still a strong part of contemporary Congolese society. (Schildkrout 1990).
The necklace was collected on the 1909-1915 Herbert Lang and James P. Chapin expedition to the Congo. The expedition was originally scheduled to last two years, but the two scientists extended their trip repeatedly in order to engage in more collecting trips. Their interest resulted in Lang and Chapin collecting several thousand ethnological objects in addition to their planned zoological collection. (Slack 2015).
It was during the colonial era that the Mangbetu way of life changed, and polygamy and animism were outlawed. “Although the Mangbetu were admired for their centralized government and artistic achievement, most Europeans of the period denigrated them for their reputed immorality.” (Schildkrout 1990, pg. 30). Once King Leopold took control of the Congo, the Mangbetu people were forced to labor on rubber plantations. The Congo gained independence in 1960, after pressure was put on Belgium from both the Congo and other countries around the world to end their colonial control.

A most highly valued piece and privilege of only the chiefs to carry such decorations. Usually their women or men bear it after them, but when they arrive in a village after having beaten their drums and gongs, these shields are held high and swayed to and fro in a very peculiar manner. The lower end of the shield rests then usually on the left forearm (bent at a right angle). They take hold of the strap about at halfway its length and moving the left arm in a semicircle, they jerk at the band so the wart hog bristles and feathers will sway up and down and sideways. They always call the name of their chiefs and some wishes for his health. The shields of chiefs are usually cleaned so the whitish natural color will at once denote the position any chief has taken, in battle where he usually takes its position on a slightly elevated position, on meetings where his shields are held high or hung up conspiciously. There are always about two or three bundles of spears with them (each bundle [illeg] about ten).

Field Notes: EMOAH. [quiver for arrows] Made of the skin of a large python. The band or string is passed over the head and under the left arm. During voyages the quiver is carried back of the arm, when actually shooting the quiver is put in front right below the armpit, the lower end backward; the higher side of the quiver is then outside. It is a wonderful sight to behold one or several Mangbetu warriors simulating an attack. Such a performance represents the utmost of possible efforts so far as agility and dexterity are concerned and it is clear that such an expenditure of power or rather such quickness of movements can only last a very short time (about 10 to 20 minutes). If they can not carry victory they go back either discouraged or to take new forces. Attacking they generally carry the shield in the left had with several javelins (5 up to 8). They continuously dodge, jump from the right to left, the shield is rapidly thrown in every direction following the turbulent movements of the body so as to form protection. With all this they howl continuously. The spear is thrown rather high up into the air about a distance of 15 to 30 yards, they land in nearly perpendicular position. I have never tested their ability of throwing accurately. After such a display they are very much exhausted. The Mangbetu nearly always carry one or several throwing spears, not only during short voyages, but also during visits or gatherings of any kind in their own village. As a rule men carry these knives in nearly sewn scabbards of elephant hair, pigskin, or antelope-skin. • AFRICAN REFLECTIONS: ART FROM NORTHEASTERN ZAIRE. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. June 1990 - February 1994
Exhibition Label: Knife with wooden handle and iron blade, given to Herbert Lang by the Mangbetu chief Zebuandra, in whose village such knives were then made. A well-dressed man always wore an ornamental knife tucked inside his belt and used it as an implement when it was old. The carving on this knife depicts the fashionable elongated head, the back apron worn by women, and a typical Mangbetu pose with one hand in front and one behind.
