HOUSE GOD
AFRICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 26 / 150
Culture: SARAMACCANER
Locale: UPPER SURINAM RIVER, VILLAGE OF LOMBE
Country: DUTCH GUIANA
Material: WASP NEST, PIGMENT (VEGETABLE JUICE), WOOD
Dimensions: L:28 W:22 H:13 (in CM)
Subject: HOUSE GOD
Acquisition Year: 1928 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: GRANGER, MYRON AND KAHN, MORTON
Keywords: FIGURE
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: The Saramacca are one of six distinct groups of the Suriname Maroons: runaway slaves from the Dutch plantations who established independent communities inland from the coastal Dutch settlements. Their religion is centered around a supreme being (Gran Gadu) and the belief that jungles, rivers, snakes and most entities embody a spirit thus each village, house and person hosts a deity to which offerings are made. In Saramaccan communities, every family worships a House God. When compared to the intricate designs of everyday items, house deities are conceived within more basic forms: the object exhibited here, for example, was created from a wasp's nest and stained with a pigment made from vegetable juice.

Researcher: Emily Fite, 2012

Source
Kahn, M. C. 1929. "Notes on the Saramaccaner Bush Negroes of Dutch Guiana." American Anthropologist, 31:468-490.

Curatorial Notes: The Saramacca are one of six distinct groups of the Suriname Maroons: runaway slaves from the Dutch plantations who established independent communities inland from the coastal Dutch settlements. Exhibited above is a carved wooden sculpture of their deity, Aflamu, created in a stylized human form and painted with hints of black, red and white colors. Indeed, this figure protects the village from evil spirits in the jungle whose abilities to metamorphose into tree stumps or bush vines enable them to assail the Saramaccans while inhabiting these shapes thereby avoiding counterattacks. Their religion is centered around a supreme being (Gran Gadu) and the belief that jungles, rivers, snakes and most entities embody a spirit thus each village, house and person hosts a deity to which offerings are made.
