Curatorial Notes: This object, from Dar es Salaam, is the door to a morgue. The door was manufactured by the Shirazi peoples and was acquired in 1953 by Dr. Korabiewicz on an expedition to East Africa that was financed by the AMNH. This door is notable as it features an Arabic inscription alongside one edge that most likely refers to a Quaranic verse.

The Shirazi peoples are a Swahili speaking group living on the islands and coasts of Tanzania[1]. Traditionally, the term referred to people, that practiced Islam, who claimed descent from Persian traders and that settled the area during the thirteenth century. However, on the islands, it can more generally refer to people who claim indigenous identity. It was through the arrival of the Shirazi that Islam was introduced and diffused through this area of East Africa[2].

The Shirazi do not actively distinguish themselves from the population and, as such, ethnic consciousness is usually reinforced through customs that included segregated neighborhoods, separated mosques and graveyards[3]. A mortuary, like the one that this door comes from, could have been part of this system of practice that would have engendered cultural difference. For example, the Shirazi follow a number of unique funerary customs as prescribed by Islamic law. Generally, the body must be washed an odd number of times by a member of the deceased’s family; in the even that only strangers are present, the body may be scrubbed with sand. Occasionally, the deceased will be interred in a garment intended specifically for the grave though this is not necessary[4]. Islamic burial practices do not simply extend to the treatment of the dead but can also structure communitywide social practices. Specifically in Tanzania, every village shares a burial society that “help[s] people cope emotionally and financially with the calamity of death[5]”. Although there is no formal membership process or dues involved, it is expected that everyone in the community should contribute either money or items, such as food, when either a villager passes away.

Shirazi practice frequently manifests visually. The door is an example of an Islamic architectural style that was brought to East Africa. Furthermore, the Arabic writing on this object is a visual marker of the history of Islam in this region and the beliefs of the Shirazi peoples as well. In the Islamic world, script often was seen as a symbol[6] of Islam due to the religion’s prohibition on the depiction of figurative forms, such as people or animals and also because of the fact that “the creation of living forms is unique to God[7]”

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

Ivory horns were vital instruments for Mangbetu royalty; ivory has long been held as a highly prized material. The use of this precious ivory and the special labor involved in its production firmly linked ivory horns with the power of the king. Ivory horn production was delegated to exceptional smiths who used metals tools to carve the instrument from an animal tusk; these smiths also carved decorative images and motifs on the exterior of the horn, as seen on object 90.1/ 1794. Herbert Lang, head of the Museum’s 1915 Congo expedition during which these horns were collected, noted that many of these horns were adorned with an anthropomorphic carving on the mouthpiece, a decorative motif also found on Mangbetu harps. The length of the horns could be extended by attaching a wooden tube to the larger end of the instrument, a device that also deepened the sound of the horn. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 202)

Ivory horn orchestras were primarily employed during court dances, such as the mabolo dance, in which the king danced alone in front of his subjects order to display his skill. The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the king’s prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).

Curatorial Notes: This is a gold mask ornament of the Ashanti (or Asante) Kingdom, a conglomeration of Akan tribes who overthrew their powerful Akan neighbors, the Denkyira (Wasserman, 1961). The Akan people considered themselves one nation consisting of many dialects. The Ashanti are members of the Twi-speaking branch of the Akan, which was a major cultural and political power in Western Africa between 1670 to 1902 (Gifford,1998). Asante means "because of war," which the words asa means "war" and nti means "because of," due to the nation being created initially to fight the Denkyira kingdom (Kaminski, 2012).

A gold cast ornament of a human head was a significant representation and reflection of a decapitated enemy chief, symbolic of the Ashanti’s prominent connection with war (Ross, 1977). These amulet masks ranged from two to three inches. This gold mask ornament is 3 1/8 inches by 2 3/8 inches and was cast by pouring molten gold into a wax mold shaped as a human face with a beard, moustache, and diadem.

In the late fifteenth century, the Portuguese were the first to arrive and maintain a position on the coast to secure trade against competitors, and Africans antagonistic towards their fortification (Pike, 2017). The Dutch, who were friendly with the Ashanti, forced the Portuguese out. In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the Dutch sold their forts (and possessions) to the British, causing the Ashanti to lose their influence over the coast and its inhabitants.

Called “The Gold Coast” by the British, they imposed a colonial administration over the subjugating Akan political authority by overseeing chiefs who maintained traditional forms of power. The indirect rule was a form of colonial governance that emerged in the Victorian era in order to circumvent indigenous rebellion, also seen in the Maori of Aotearoa ( New Zealand) and the Ethiopian and Eritrean defeat of Italian forces.

Tensions amplified between the British and the Ashanti when many chiefs realized their power was increasingly tenuous and symbolic, as Britain seized more control of their means of production and local politics. Some chiefs punished those who had sought British protection from their own rulings (Wasserman, 1961).

The British subsequently invaded with an army of 2,500, accompanied by a great number of African auxiliaries (Pike, 2017). The British believed their invasion as the only effective method to stabilize, secure, and deal with the disputes between rivals groups that disturbed their trade. In contrast, the Ashanti believed the British’s action were deceptive and did not allow for a mutual agreement between both parties. The Ashanti faced defeat with the occupation and burning of their capital city, Kumasi, forcing the Ashanti to get rid of their Ansantehene (King) and accept protection of the crown (Driskell 1968).

On January 1, 1902, the Ashanti Nation dissolved and became a British colony which capitalized on their culturally, politically, and economically significant resources. After British attempts to create a global imperial presence in the Victorian era, exploitation of the West African gold industry was a way to resolve their own extensive debts.

In 1957 this west coastal region of Africa was established as the independent nation of Ghana (Edgerton, 1995). Many gold artifacts did not survive from the Ashanti Nation after their defeat. These gold pieces became heavily commodified both as symbols of European royal prestige and on the emerging private market. Their value transitioned from material representation of the Akan chiefly powers, to demonstration of European economic and political control. Therefore, many Ashanti gold objects were melted down and sold for profit, eroding a significant source of material culture of the Ashanti Kingdom. (Habashi, 2009).

Curatorial Notes: This is a mask worn by women in the Sande women's regional association across Liberia, often associated with the Vai, Kpelle and Mende cultures. This mask, known as a bundu, is worn during celebrations and ceremonies marking the transition of a girl into womanhood and gives the wearer the full rights and responsibility to participate in the Sande society. A spiritual force embodying the feminine ideal is conferred upon the young woman when she dons the mask; the sowo spirit is channeled through her in the mask and costume and in moments of civic importance, she herself becomes an embodiment of the sowo-- a difficult to attain image of female beauty and spiritual power.

The social responsibility of the woman who wears this bundu is to demonstrate the political and social strength of the women's associate through her careful conduct in the performance of the duties of the sowo. The mask "embodies and mediates contradictions between traditional feminine role models in the society and the actual political and economic power which women do have access to, if not consistently, then at least regularly and generally enough for that power to call forth a significant symbolic focus in the ideological realm." (Dubinskas 1976) This mask is worn to spiritually consecrate the vital role of women's collectives in the political and cultural life of their communities.

Curatorial Notes: This object is a cup carved by the Kuba people of the Congo region who are a coalition of at least eighteen different ethnic groups who have united through mechanisms of an elaborate political system. The Kuba system of governance features a number of figures, including a king, called the nyim, who holds the most authority, and also lesser councils and representatives who wield a certain amount of power[1]. The Kuba are agriculturalists and cultivate a number of different resources. These include beans, manioc, raffia, and various other palms. The palms and the sap they produce are used in the making of palm wine. The sap is usually placed in gourds and fermented until a mildly alcoholic and acidic liquid develops.[2]

This type of cup features prominently in the social lives of the Kuba people. Wealthy individuals often engaged in conspicuous consumption and would serve large quantities of palm wine to their guests. The consumption of palm wine is a social activity, not meant to be done alone[3]. Cups of this type provided a means for the Kuba to forge significant relationships and, by extension, community solidarity.

Drinking palm wine is an activity that is only undertaken in public by men. Although women do imbibe palm wine as well, they do so clandestinely. Female intoxication is considered deviant and women who are discovered to be drinking are socially castigated. This cup almost certainly was made for and used by a Kuba man[4].

These cups also speak to Kuba epistemologies beyond the role of palm wine as a social lubricant. Kuba cups, along with other aspects of their material culture and art “need to be understood in the context and development of the kingship.[5]” Bilotta, in her analysis, indicated that the “artistry and craftsmanship” of Kuba art, including cups, “was a tribute to prosperity and the institution of kingship[6]” and, as such, reproduced “royal statuses[7]”

Curatorial Notes: The Bambara of Mali are Mande-speaking people who live on the Bani and Upper Niger Rivers who are predominantly agrarian. The Bambara people credit Ci-Wara, a spiritual being, with teaching them agriculture; it is under Ci-Wara’s guidance, they say, that they became successful and able farmers.

This antelope headdress is worn by Bambara male dancers, Ci-Wara (translated to “farm animals”). It is often worn in pairs of one male and female antelope. The antelope symbolizes the farmer who tills his fields tirelessly (Siegmann 2009: 62). Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, “Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good
job” (Phillips 1995: 502).

The headdress exemplifies the Bambara people’s devotion to agricultural cultivation and fertility. “The organization of the Ci-wara performance is based on the Bamana respect for the power and efficiency of the union of male and female. As human reproduction is the result of the sexual union between man and woman, so agricultural fertility is attributed to the union between fire (the sun), an expression of the male principle, and earth and water, an expression of the female principle.” (Colleyn 2001: 204).

The horns of the antelope represent ears of millet and the zigzag design of the mane represents the sun. The costume worn by the performers consists of raffia fibers that extend from the base of the headdress to the ground and represent the rain needed for cultivation.The female antelope has a child on its back depicting the way mothers carry their children, but also calling attention to animal, plant, and human fertility (Siegmann 2009: 62). The headdresses hold a parallel between the penetration of the earth through farming and copulation (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

The headdress is associated with different purposes, however, as one observer noted, “like all other dances of the Bamana that of [Ci-Wara] was never static ….the development and change took place somewhat independently in each region and indeed in each village” (Lagamma 2002: 71). Likewise, each performance is unique and no one description will be able to convey the many tribal traditions.

It is used during agricultural competitions. During these competitions young farmers are galvanized by songs, music, and the masked dances. They seek to imitate the mythical animals that originally taught humans agriculture. During the competition the men work through a number of rows in a field and the first to completion is the winner and declared the Ci-Wara. The title brings much praise to the winner. Men are constantly invited to take up the contest, just as their ancestors had done before, and see how they measure up to the heroes of the past.

Curatorial Notes: The Bambara of Mali are Mande-speaking people who live on the Bani and Upper Niger Rivers who are predominantly agrarian. The Bambara people credit Ci-Wara, a spiritual being, with teaching them agriculture; it is under Ci-Wara’s guidance, they say, that they became successful and able farmers.

This antelope headdress is worn by Bambara male dancers, Ci-Wara (translated to “farm animals”). It is often worn in pairs of one male and female antelope. The antelope symbolizes the farmer who tills his fields tirelessly (Siegmann 2009: 62). Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, “Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good
job” (Phillips 1995: 502).

The headdress exemplifies the Bambara people’s devotion to agricultural cultivation and fertility. “The organization of the Ci-wara performance is based on the Bamana respect for the power and efficiency of the union of male and female. As human reproduction is the result of the sexual union between man and woman, so agricultural fertility is attributed to the union between fire (the sun), an expression of the male principle, and earth and water, an expression of the female principle.” (Colleyn 2001: 204).

The horns of the antelope represent ears of millet and the zigzag design of the mane represents the sun. The costume worn by the performers consists of raffia fibers that extend from the base of the headdress to the ground and represent the rain needed for cultivation.The female antelope has a child on its back depicting the way mothers carry their children, but also calling attention to animal, plant, and human fertility (Siegmann 2009: 62). The headdresses hold a parallel between the penetration of the earth through farming and copulation (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

The headdress is associated with different purposes, however, as one observer noted, “like all other dances of the Bamana that of [Ci-Wara] was never static ….the development and change took place somewhat independently in each region and indeed in each village” (Lagamma 2002: 71). Likewise, each performance is unique and no one description will be able to convey the many tribal traditions.

It is used during agricultural competitions. During these competitions young farmers are galvanized by songs, music, and the masked dances. They seek to imitate the mythical animals that originally taught humans agriculture. During the competition the men work through a number of rows in a field and the first to completion is the winner and declared the Ci-Wara. The title brings much praise to the winner. Men are constantly invited to take up the contest, just as their ancestors had done before, and see how they measure up to the heroes of the past.

It is also used during entertainment and ritual events. They appeared during festivals at the Ségou court. After colonial conquest they were also a part of the July 14th Bastille celebrations, French National Day.

Third, it is used during an annual two day celebration that takes place between November and April, according to the moon’s appearance. This celebration includes sacrifices and the remaking of cult objects. It also includes a parade through the village with stops
