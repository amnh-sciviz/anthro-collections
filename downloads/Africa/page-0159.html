Curatorial Notes: The Ait Atta are the largest Berber group in southern Morocco, a “supertribe” or “confederacy” of smaller descent groups that can be found scattered throughout the Middle Atlas Mountains and the southeastern Saharan region of Morocco. “Ait” means “people of,” and “Atta” is the semi-legendary “Daddy Atta” who was the shared ancestor of all Ait Atta peoples. Well into the mid-nineteenth-century, most Atta groups lived a life of semi-nomadism known as transhumance, where a small number of shepherds and goatherds in an Atta community seasonally departed their home communities grazing pastures further up into the mountains. Transhumance was accompanied by sedentary agriculture, which defined the lives of the non-transhuming majority of Ait Atta. Throughout the twentieth century, as tribal sociopolitical structures and nomadic movement were disrupted by colonial and postcolonial Moroccan governments, many distinctive aspects of precolonial Atta culture slowly became untenable. Today, very few Atta families transhume, and most Atta communities have become fully sedentary.

The direct effects of this transition on Atta culture are particularly notable in textile production. Women once wove woolen garments, tents, carpets, and bags from wool sheared from their own flocks of sheep and goats. Today, families maintain at best a few livestock, if any at all. Women therefore typically buy at least some wool for weaving in the marketplace, and occasionally use synthetic fibers in their looms. Because fewer women make their own woolen textile fibers, the process of wool-making and weaving is no longer the labor-intensive and communal effort it once was. Wool, however, still retains particular cultural prestige and importance for the Ait Atta. In addition to being the most practical for cold mountain nights, wool is also seen by the Atta as having plentiful baraka, loosely translated as “divine blessing.” The Ait Atta still have great respect for those women who excel at weaving, although as Cynthia Becker notes, “fewer and fewer young women are learning how to weave blankets and carpets; it is rapidly becoming an art that is restricted to the older generation” (Becker 2006: 32).

In wool-working, the first step is preparing the wool for the loom by straightening out the fibers and working out the kinks with iron pronged combs (90.2/ 5194, 90.2/ 5195). The iron combs are followed by a fine-toothed wooden wool card, such as this one, which makes the wool even more supple and smooth. This wool is then dyed, if desired, and mounted onto the loom.

David Montgomery Hart (1927-2001) was an American anthropologist, noted for his fieldwork among various Moroccan Berber tribes during the fifties and sixties. From 1959 to 1967, Hart was hired by Department of Anthropology at the American Museum of Natural History to gather film footage and tape recordings of Berber life, and to collect ethnographic artifacts on behalf of the museum to expand its African ethnology collections. Additionally, Hart played in integral role in the development and construction of the museum’s desert diorama in the permanent Hall of African Peoples exhibit.

The glasses are a set of five. The gold designs on the glasses is in a pattern of five. This is because five represents the hand, a common symbol of protection against the evil eye. Because of this many abstract designs have been created based on the hand, generally using the repetition of the number five. The evil eye is the idea that a negative or covetous glance can cause harm to befall you.
Popularized by the British in the late 1870s, tea drinking is an important ritual in Moroccan culture. Poorer families may have a simple aluminum barrahd while richer families may have more ornate teapots made of silver. They also may have accessories with the tea set such as a siniyya, a round table with feet that the teapot rests on. There are also rbaia, circular containers for tea, mint and sugar and a bahbur or samovar which is used to hold the water for tea making. Tea drinking is synonymous with hospitality and thus people are willing to spend more on an elaborate tea set or carry the extra weight of the teapot to make sure they are a generous host. The pressure of this also falls on the men to serve the tea although often the women may make the tea out of view of the guests.
Mint tea is widely considered Morocco’s national drink, in the north made with plenty of sugar and fresh mint to sweeten it, while in the south it is stronger and more bitter with only dried mint. In the north, it is brought to a boil once then the bitter water is discarded, then new water is added and brought to a boil, then more water along with the sugar. Meanwhile, in the south, water is not discarded and instead just added. This is because the north has abundant water, mint, and sugar while in the more arid south these are less available.
The tea is poured back and forth between the glass and the pot three times with sugar added for flavor. It is then poured into the glass for a final time from two feet or higher, this is to aerate the tea to bring out the flavors similar to a glass of wine. After the tea is poured for the guest they are meant to compliment the host on the tea. It is expected the guest drinks more than one but less than four glasses.

Curatorial Notes: The silham is a cape worn by Berbers and other Atlas groups of North Africa dating back to the Roman era. Solely known as a silham in Morocco, in other regions of North Africa this cape is called a burnous (also burnouse or bournous)--originating from the Arabic word burnus. In conjunction with a man’s turban and tunic, or tajellabiyt, the silham embraces imagery that “associates men with Islam and the qualities of modesty, honor, and dignity” (Becker 2006, 52).

The silham is circular with a large pointed hood and a hole for the head that is embroidered to strengthen the fabric. However, instead of wearing the silham as intended, many men prefer to wear the cape tossed over their shoulder. Worn as an outer garment over the tajellabiyt, the silham is made from undyed wool, camel hair, cotton, or a mixture of these materials, making the garment snow resistant. Reaching down to the wearer’s feet, with long sides that can be wrapped around the wearer’s body for warmth, the silham’s material and style make it useful as both an “item of clothing by day and a blanket by night” (Lynch and Strauss 2014, 56).

While men’s hooded gowns are similar in structure among Berber groups throughout North Africa, they are unique to each group by specific colors, styles, and patterns. The Ait Wawazgit of the Anti-Atlas weave their silham with black goat hair and decorate it with a red and white salamander pattern--”the Berber symbol of longevity” (Shoup 2013, 504). Another common motif is the decorative orange eye pattern, which is woven on the back of the silham to ward off the harmful forces of the “evil eye”: an envious and coveted gaze which is believed to cause “bad luck, illness, and even death” (Becker 2006, 48).

The Ait Atta are the largest Berber group in southern Morocco, one of many that can be found throughout the Middle Atlas Mountains and the southeastern Saharan region of Morocco. “Ait” means “people of,” and “Atta” is the “Daddy Atta” of legend who was the shared ancestor of all Ait Atta peoples. Well into the mid-nineteenth-century, most Atta groups lived a life of semi-nomadism known as transhumance, where a small number of shepherds and goatherds in an Atta community seasonally departed their home for grazing pastures up into the mountains. Transhumance was accompanied by sedentary agriculture, which defined the lives of the majority. Throughout the twentieth century, nomadic movement was disrupted by Moroccan politics, many distinctive aspects of precolonial Atta culture slowly became untenable. Today, very few Atta families transhume, and most have settled on specific land.
As they would transhumance, much of their living was in tents, and as such even though they have become mostly settled, tents remain an important part of the culture. Some families still have tents set up on their land in addition to their permanent residences. These tents were woven, put up and deconstructed by women and protected their families from the sun, sandstorms and chill. They were woven with goat hair on horizontal ground looms in bands around two feet wide and thirty two feet long. These large looms are outside, and the threads are pounded into place with a comb known as a taska. Each panel takes roughly three weeks to weave, and a mid-size tent takes seven taskas. As settlements became more common, these horizontal looms and the tents woven on them disappeared.
However, tents are still part of wedding ceremonies. The bridal tent is set up outside the groom’s home. Curatorial Notes: The Ait Atta are the largest Berber group in southern Morocco, one of many that can be found throughout the Middle Atlas Mountains and the southeastern Saharan region of Morocco. “Ait” means “people of,” and “Atta” is the “Daddy Atta” of legend who was the shared ancestor of all Ait Atta peoples. Well into the mid-nineteenth-century, most Atta groups lived a life of semi-nomadism known as transhumance, where a small number of shepherds and goatherds in an Atta community seasonally departed their home for grazing pastures up into the mountains. Transhumance was accompanied by sedentary agriculture, which defined the lives of the majority. Throughout the twentieth century, nomadic movement was disrupted by Moroccan politics, many distinctive aspects of precolonial Atta culture slowly became untenable. Today, very few Atta families transhume, and most have settled on specific land.
As they would transhumance, much of their living was in tents, and as such even though they have become mostly settled, tents remain an important part of the culture. Some families still have tents set up on their land in addition to their permanent residences. These tents were woven, put up and deconstructed by women and protected their families from the sun, sandstorms and chill. They were woven with goat hair on horizontal ground looms in bands around two feet wide and thirty two feet long. These large looms are outside, and the threads are pounded into place with a comb known as a taska. Each panel takes roughly three weeks to weave, and a mid-size tent takes seven taskas. As settlements became more common, these horizontal looms and the tents woven on them disappeared.
However, tents are still part of wedding ceremonies. The bridal tent is set up outside the groom’s home. The bride is driven near it, but the car stops a short distance away so the bride can ride a mule to the tent. The women in the groom's family are prepared to welcome the bride while the men in the wedding party playfully attempt to halt the progress of the bride’s mule. Thus, even though the bride is not far from the tent, it may take her over three hours to arrive. Before the bride enters her tent, she and the rest of the bridal party circle it three times counterclockwise to acknowledge the tent. Then she is given a bowl of milk to drink of which she takes a sip then showers the rest on the wedding guests and over the tent entrance to pay respects to the dangerous spiritual beings or jnoun who live there. Jnoun inhabit places of transition both real and spiritual; hence, doorways are inherently dangerous places. The tent entrance is both, symbolizing the bride’s transition from single to married life, and it is also a literal doorway. By circling and sprinkling the milk, it is meant to pacify the jnoun and purify the tent. Curatorial Notes: The tajellabiyt, a hooded, long-sleeved tunic made of undyed sheep’s wool, is a common outer garment worn by men across Morocco and throughout North Africa--although today, they are also worn by women. Originating from the Arabic word for the same garment, jellaba, the name became more recognizably “Berber” with a prefix and suffix. Underneath their tajellabiyt, men wear white or blue trousers and a long white cotton shirt--white representing “moral qualities and good fortune” (Becker 2006, 50).

Women present sons with their first tajellabiyt when they are around thirteen or fourteen to publicly mark their transition from boyhood to manhood--both metaphorically and physically. Adolescent boys who accompany their fathers to their herds in the desert are protected by the tajellabiyt from the harsh desert environment.

The traditional tajellabiyt was solely dark brown and knee length. However, after Spanish occupation as a result of the Rif War of 1921 - 1926--an armed conflict between colonial Spain and Berber tribes of the Rif mountain region--this type was mostly abandoned for the current variety. Today, the majority of Berber men wear tajellabiyts that fall midway between the knee and ankle. However, the traditional tajellabiyt is still worn by the Jbala and Ghmara people.

Before 1926, men wore woolen turbans with their tajellabiyt which left the crown of their head uncovered, displaying a scalplock. However, in 1922, political leader Abd al-Krim ordered all men to shave off their scalplocks in accordance with his goals of “modernization and Islamic reform” (Pennell 2004). Today men wear cotton turbans, called an arezziy, which cover the wearer’s shaven head. The arezziy carries social siginificance, symbolizing honor and respect for the wearer. The tajellabiyt, in combination with the turban and a protective cape called a silham, embraces imagery that “associates men with Islam and the qualities of modesty, honor, and dignity” (Becker 2006, 52).

Although these clothes are still worn today, beginning after the Rif War, more and more young men have switched to wearing European-influenced clothing or a combination of both European and traditional Berber clothes.

Today, very few Atta families transhume, and most Atta communities have become fully sedentary.

The Ait Atta were one of the last tribes to resist French colonization, fighting into the 1930s when most of the country had already surrendered by 1920. This flintlock gun, known as bu shfar, would have been one of the most commonly-used firearms by the resisting fighters. Though repeating rifles were more highly valued and easily used in war, the flintlock was more affordable for the majority of tribesmen. As Hart notes, “The two most visible marks of a man in ‘Atta eyes are the turban and the beard, and in [pre-colonial] days the rifle or flintlock gun made a third, while many Ait ‘Atta still carry curved daggers today” (Hart 1984: 85).

David Montgomery Hart (1927-2001) was an American anthropologist, noted for his fieldwork among various Moroccan Berber tribes during the fifties and sixties. From 1959 to 1967, Hart was hired by Department of Anthropology at the American Museum of Natural History to gather film footage and tape recordings of Berber life, and to collect ethnographic artifacts on behalf of the museum to expand its African ethnology collections. Additionally, Hart played in integral role in the development and construction of the museum’s desert diorama in the permanent Hall of African Peoples exhibit, in which this object can be found.

Curatorial Notes: The silham is a cape worn by Berbers and other Atlas groups of North Africa dating back to the Roman era. Solely known as a silham in Morocco, in other regions of North Africa this cape is called a burnous (also burnouse or bournous)--originating from the Arabic word burnus. In conjunction with a man’s turban and tunic, or tajellabiyt, the silham embraces imagery that “associates men with Islam and the qualities of modesty, honor, and dignity” (Becker 2006, 52).

The silham is circular with a large pointed hood and a hole for the head that is embroidered to strengthen the fabric. However, instead of wearing the silham as intended, many men prefer to wear the cape tossed over their shoulder. Worn as an outer garment over the tajellabiyt, the silham is made from undyed wool, camel hair, cotton, or a mixture of these materials, making the garment snow resistant. Reaching down to the wearer’s feet, with long sides that can be wrapped around the wearer’s body for warmth, the silham’s material and style make it useful as both an “item of clothing by day and a blanket by night” (Lynch and Strauss 2014, 56).

While men’s hooded gowns are similar in structure among Berber groups throughout North Africa, they are unique to each group by specific colors, styles, and patterns. The Ait Wawazgit of the Anti-Atlas weave their silham with black goat hair and decorate it with a red and white salamander pattern--”the Berber symbol of longevity” (Shoup 2013, 504). Another common motif is the decorative orange eye pattern, which is woven on the back of the silham to ward off the harmful forces of the “evil eye”: an envious and coveted gaze which is believed to cause “bad luck, illness, and even death” (Becker 2006, 48).
