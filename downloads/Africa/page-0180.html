Curatorial Notes: This object is a cup carved by the Kuba people of the Congo region who are a coalition of at least eighteen different ethnic groups who have united through mechanisms of an elaborate political system. The Kuba system of governance features a number of figures, including a king, called the nyim, who holds the most authority, and also lesser councils and representatives who wield a certain amount of power[1]. The Kuba are agriculturalists and cultivate a number of different resources. These include beans, manioc, raffia, and various other palms. The palms and the sap they produce are used in the making of palm wine. The sap is usually placed in gourds and fermented until a mildly alcoholic and acidic liquid develops.[2]

This type of cup features prominently in the social lives of the Kuba people. Wealthy individuals often engaged in conspicuous consumption and would serve large quantities of palm wine to their guests. The consumption of palm wine is a social activity, not meant to be done alone[3]. Cups of this type provided a means for the Kuba to forge significant relationships and, by extension, community solidarity.

Drinking palm wine is an activity that is only undertaken in public by men. Although women do imbibe palm wine as well, they do so clandestinely. Female intoxication is considered deviant and women who are discovered to be drinking are socially castigated. This cup almost certainly was made for and used by a Kuba man[4].

These cups also speak to Kuba epistemologies beyond the role of palm wine as a social lubricant. Kuba cups, along with other aspects of their material culture and art need to be understood in the context and development of the kingship.[5] Bilotta, in her analysis, indicated that the artistry and craftsmanship of Kuba art, including cups, was a tribute to prosperity and the institution of kingship[6] and, as such, reproduced royal statuses[7]

Curatorial Notes: This object is a cup carved by the Kuba people of the Congo region who are a coalition of at least eighteen different ethnic groups who have united through mechanisms of an elaborate political system. The Kuba system of governance features a number of figures, including a king, called the nyim, who holds the most authority, and also lesser councils and representatives who wield a certain amount of power[1]. The Kuba are agriculturalists and cultivate a number of different resources. These include beans, manioc, raffia, and various other palms. The palms and the sap they produce are used in the making of palm wine. The sap is usually placed in gourds and fermented until a mildly alcoholic and acidic liquid develops.[2]

This type of cup features prominently in the social lives of the Kuba people. Wealthy individuals often engaged in conspicuous consumption and would serve large quantities of palm wine to their guests. The consumption of palm wine is a social activity, not meant to be done alone[3]. Cups of this type provided a means for the Kuba to forge significant relationships and, by extension, community solidarity.

Drinking palm wine is an activity that is only undertaken in public by men. Although women do imbibe palm wine as well, they do so clandestinely. Female intoxication is considered deviant and women who are discovered to be drinking are socially castigated. This cup almost certainly was made for and used by a Kuba man[4].

These cups also speak to Kuba epistemologies beyond the role of palm wine as a social lubricant. Kuba cups, along with other aspects of their material culture and art need to be understood in the context and development of the kingship.[5] Bilotta, in her analysis, indicated that the artistry and craftsmanship of Kuba art, including cups, was a tribute to prosperity and the institution of kingship[6] and, as such, reproduced royal statuses[7]

Curatorial Notes: The Senufo people, occupying areas of Mali, Burkina Faso, and Cote dIvoire, were given this name by the French as an identifier of the language they spoke (Launay 1999:272). Between the 15th and 16th centuries, the Senufo conglomerated into a chiefdom (Shoup 2011:253), with their capital situated in Korhogo in the Cote dIvoire, which declared an overarching identity for the widely spread and diverse Senufo people. Today, the villages of the Senufo are matrilineally ruled with intense social stratification inhibiting intermarriage between the three main social groups: landowning farmers, craftsmen, and slaves or descendants of slaves (Shoup 2011:253). In total, the Senufo people speak four languages, each language associated with the Gur family of the Niger-Congo languages.

Through sculpture, masquerade masks, and other forms of art, the Senufo peoples of Cte dIvoire intertwine the symbolic significance of their artwork with rites and rituals, such as transitional hoeing competitions. These competitions, or kahamas, are comprised of young men ages 15-17 in the Junior Grade (Glaze 1976:129). In these competitions, young men work to transition into the Senior Grade, the next phase of their lives, by winning the title of sambali. Upon victory, the champion of the kahamasreceives a cultivator staff, representing the young mans esteem in his agricultural skills, as well as the important transition he makes from the Junior Grade to the Senior Grade. Carved from a single block of wood, these staffs usually display a Senufo woman, existing as a manifestation of the Senufo idealization of feminine beauty (Glaze 1986:32), or a red-hawk eagle with outstretched wings. However, here, the cultivator staff head is depicted as an airplane.

On a larger scale, this staff holds great significance for the family who earns it as well as it is passed down the paternal genealogical line. This demonstrates the staffs importance on not only a personal scale, but also on a familial scale, as it affects the reputation of the family who has claim to the staff.
The staff also carries great significance upon the death of a sambali, in which the transition from life to death is made. A second cultivator staff is brought to the funeral, where dancing and certain drumming rituals are performed. These are seen as ways to open the place of the dead for the person who has died.

Curatorial Notes: Across the continent, wire toys, such as this toy bicycle from Namibia, are an exuberant and efficient way of fabricating children's toys. These toys are made from a variety of recycled materials such as scrap metal, metal rods, rubber bands, tin cans, and many others (Deregowski). In the past, toys such as these tended to be made out of wood; however, with increasing availability of wire power cables from formal and informal electrical grids and other infrastructure projects, metal wire has become an increasingly popular option. The wire’s malleability allows for many different design options. Creating planes and toys out of scrap materials popular with children for their own recreational use, but it is also encouraged by schools with many different levels of competition (Donlon).

While these toys were originally exclusively for personal play, artisans have started to produce and sell wire toys to tourists. These toys tend to be more elaborate, using craft workshop techniques such as welding to create profitable, albeit less play-friendly finished products. These toys also tend to not be made from as many recycled materials with figures being “entirely created in formed wire represent[ing] a style largely, if not solely, market driven by tourism pressure in Southern Africa” (Donlon, 49). Not only is there a lack of recycled materials, but wire toys produced for the tourist market may also include not recycled materials, utilizing mass produced parts like toy car wheels (Donlon). Donlon states that these figures marketed towards tourists are a medium between the wooden toys of the past and the and the recycled toys that have come into popularity over the past years. On one end the wooden toys tend to be viewed as priceless by both locals and visitors. On the other hand, the wire toys used by kids are not as marketable to a tourist market. This creates room for artisans to create their own styles in order to appeal to tourists.
