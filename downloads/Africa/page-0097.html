Field Notes: ANDROGI (= cloth for women) also called ASIASI. A selection of pieces of bark cloth as worn (there are two new ones) by the women of OKONDO. The black ones are predominant. The queens were [sic, wear?] always a black one. I saw only once MTUMBANI in a red one. Today the queens seldom wear native dress. They have generally a large ill fitting shirt-like dress made of white and blue like NENSIMA. MTUMBANI prefers a short shirt reaching down the nabel [sic, navel?] and pagne below. All these and the following samples are painted by native artists without interference except that similar designs are usually only found on the walls of the houses inside as well as outside.

Acquisition Year: 1915 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: LANG, HERBERT
Keywords: ARROW
Category: WEAPONS AND AMMUNITION
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Field Notes: [illeg: Moboko?] The iron point ENGWAME, the shaft NEGOROKO = the quiver of basketrywork-- EBAMBAMBANGU--arrows of the BANGBA. The MANGBETU proper on the MADJE use shafts of wood of the stalks of a palm. The BANGBA are very proud of their arrows as they pretend they go as far as the bullets of a rifle. This quiver was given by OKONDO and contains a hundred arrows. Near the base they insert some hair of a GENET (NANDISIA).

Field Notes: EGAKU. The string with which women and some men tie up their head, also used for children. It is made by women from bast of the raffia palm. Photographs have been taken of the manner of braiding and two of the sticks = GADOKIRRA upon which the string is braided are in the collection No. lll3, lll4. The string is blackened by crushing the leaves and bark of a tree = EPADA, (see sample No. lll5) putting it into water and washing it in the maceration received. Without drying it the string is put into the mud for a day and in this way becomes very black. (This is the first time I hear of the addition of the leaves and bark of this tree to blacken objects.) The interpreter I took with me from MEDJE says now that the MEDJE also use the bark and leaves of a tree (but not the same). The same bark and leaves or rather the maceration of it is used for blackening pots. It is in this process I saw it first time employed. It is put on when the jars are still hot during the process of backing them. After blackening the string is cleaned of all the small outstanding pieces. No. 1112 is fastened to a form of wood so the perfectness and size may be seen at once and especially in order to smoothen out all possible knicks, as the form has about the circumference of a human head, the arrangement is perfect. See photographs. Nearly all women know how to braid the string.

Material: WOOD, PLANT FIBER, METAL
Dimensions: L:63 W:20 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1915 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: LANG, HERBERT
Keywords: BELLOWS
Category: EQUIPMENT
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Field Notes: MBITI. [One of] Two bellows carved in wood and covered with hide. The projecting tube is put into a cylindrical tube of dried clay mixed with broken stalks or fibres from grasses. This heavy tube only reaches the fire. The bellows are worked by a boy alternately by pulling on the string fixed in the center of the slightly tightened skin. They often work without many iron tools and use an assortment of roundish pebbles to beat the red hot iron, though for finishing their rather poor work they use a sort of hammer fixed to a split or forked stick. The AZANDE towards YAKULUKU are only very poor blacksmiths and hardly produce but some sort of hoes or lumbs [sic, lumps?] of molten iron used as money. The AZANDE towards POKO, NALA, near DUNGU on the contrary are just as experienced in working iron as the MANGBETU, as may be seen from the specimens. NOTE: From AZANDE east of YAKULUKU.

Keywords: TOBACCO
Share Object

Field Notes: EDOBO. Tabac [sic, tobacco] so prepared by the MANGBETU. They take off the green leaves and let them dry somewhat in the sun, crush them in the mortar and form of this mass similar cakes as specimen coll. which are continually laid out in the sun until dry. The entire leaf is eaten, without addition of anything. Their pipe consists of the hollowed stalk of a banana leaf which is still closed near the end. About a foot distant from its end is a small hole cut into the upper rounded portion of the stalk that unites with the tubular hollowed portion made with a stick or an iron. In this hole they put a piece of green banana leaf turned to a funnel. A few splinters passed through like needles made it keep its form. This is put in the hole as a bowl. A boy or woman or girl is generally attached to each of the more important persons. When ordered he puts only a little tabac into the bowl and awaiting the person he or she puts a glowing coal on top of the bowl. The bowl is only about 2 ctm. in diam. on top and of about 5 ctm. high. The other takes some long draughts drawing in all the smoke he possibly can, and opening his mouth he slowly exhales the smoke. They often cough. OKONDO himself does not smoke. But all the women, some of his women, are passionate smokers of hashish. The pipe is often passed around but there is seldom enough tabac in to last for three or four persons. The girls or boys that serve the pipe never would dare to smoke in the presence of other persons. These pi...pes are renewed every 4 or 5 days.Show More >>
