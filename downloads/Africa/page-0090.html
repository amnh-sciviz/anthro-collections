Field Notes: BAGASSA. Sticks to make fire still common among the AZANDE, the LOGO, BAKA, MONDO and related tribes. The process is simple and any native can produce fire or at least glowing dust within a minute or two. Even at the heaviest rains when seemingly no dry material may be found, within 15 minutes the first flames are playing a crackling between the materials the natives have rapidly collected. They first make a crosscut along the softer stick and spread some fibres of an old bark cloth upon a large leaf. The bark cloth nourishes the glowing dust and it has the same qualities as tinder. The soft stick is placed with the incision upon the bark cloth. The hard stick is put upon the incision given a rotating motion as they slide their palm between which this stick is held rapidly in opposite direction. Whenever their hands during these movements slide too far below, they dexterously move them up to recommence again. During this rotating operation fine dust is worked off and drops at the edges of the crosscut upon the fibres of bark cloth. The dust gradually blackens, fumes are discharged and the joy of all a glowing is at least produced easily and willfully nourished on the little pile of dust. At once the sticks are taken off and by careful management and continuous blowing they have no more difficulty to kindle a fire so essential to all these natives. They easily can go without food for a day, but a rain to pass a night in the open air without fire would render them incapable for... any further work the following day. Very often during an exhausting march they make fire in this manner to light their pipes and have a smoke. In this case they simply drop the glowing dust upon the tabacco [sic, tobacco] into their pipes.Show More >>

Field Notes: BAEDE. Buffalo horn used to extract blood. They dip it first into the water, make a few light scratches in the skin with a sharp knife, press it upon the skin, suck firmly the air out with their mouth at the tip and close it by putting some wax or soft resin with the tongue over the (hole) opening. They also use the webs of certain spiders for the same purpose. They carefully press the wax about the edges with their fingers and wait until it drops off after it has been filled with blood about 1/3 of an inch from below. The blood drops out in a dish. In common use, specially for general rheumatism and in all other ills.

Field Notes: KOLLUGWA. The fruit of a tree that grows much like lemons but towards the dry season (towards Dec.) becomes hard. The LOGO use it to make a sort of castanet. It is held in either hand. One ball is held firmly with the thumb in the palm, the string passes between the third and fourth fingers so the ball hangs downward on the cord that both holds together. Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu knife pictured above, acquired in the early 1900’s, is called a Nadada knife which is one of the three types of knives that is categorized under the umbrella groups of namambele and nedukpa (Treasures of the Africa Museum, 1995). The nadada is a ceremonial knife that is symbolizes potency and power. These knives are used to “endow” a family when the chief wishes to betrothe a new wife, and by offering the knife to the family, the chief thus shows consideration and prestige. The sickle shaped blade of the Nadada knife is typically made with iron while the shaft is made from ivory or wood. The handle of ceremonial knives may sometimes be shaped to represent the elongated head of a woman, which was a product of cranial binding – a practice that began during infancy of wrapping the child’s head tightly with cloth to give it an elongated appearance which was seen as beautiful and aristocratic. Though cranial binding less common in Mangbetu culture today, the effects of the binding can be seen in elders who have had their heads wrapped during their infancy (Schildkrout 2008: 9).

Known globally for their rich musical heritage and intricate garments and personal adornments, the Mangbetu people prize aesthetic achievement and exceptional craftsmanship. Both elements are incorporated into artisanal production, including the smelting of tools and weapons. Knives in Mangbetu culture were and are both functional items and prestigious ornaments (Knives of the Mangbetu: 2008).

The incorporation of artistic components into the everyday life of the Mangbetu drew attention from Western “exploration” (Slack, 2003) who set out on expeditions in what is now the Democratic Republic of Congo. The reports from the German Botanist Georg Schweinfurth, the first European explorer to reach the Mangbetu in 1870, (Smithsonian National Museum of African Art: 2018) of the elegant arts of the Mangbetu and their elaborate hairstyles and fashion, attracted many Western photographers and filmmakers in the first half of the 20th century. The Western Europeans exoticized the people through an ethnocentric lens, conjuring up myths that the Mangbetu were cannibalistic “savages.” (Gunsch, 2017) Colonial rule and the spread of Christian missionaries was often justified and buttressed by these pervasive racist myths created and perpetuated by Europeans, but in reality, cannibalism was restricted to a religious ritual similarly to the civilizations of the Mayans and the Aztecs. The Europeans nicknamed the Mangbetu the “Parisians of Africa” (American Museum Congo Expedition: 2003) because of their artistic talent and fashion, which was a strikingly beautiful aspect of the culture. The influence of Mangbetu art can be seen in representations of popular culture today as a long lasting impact of the culture and its representation of beauty.

Field Notes: ADUGOMWE NAPODRO. The flour of white sorghum. The usual way of preparation is to let the water come to ebullition and gradually stir the flour into. Used principally to make a sort of beer. The sorghum (grain) is then put into the water and after three days soaking is left to germination and thus ground upon the stones by women. This mass is left again two days in baskets in the houses and then spread out on mats and dried thus sometimes stored away for a while or at once ground again and put into the water and left gently boiling for about eight to ten hours. It is left cooling overnight and then filtered in a crude fashion and ready for consumption. It is not a liquid, but decidedly a liquid paste as all the flour is retained. Many LOGOS do eat very little but live practically on this sort of beverage that is often very intoxicating (if fermented) but certainly also nourishing. Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

Ivory horns were vital instruments for Mangbetu royalty; ivory has long been held as a highly prized material. The use of this precious ivory and the special labor involved in its production firmly linked ivory horns with the power of the king. Ivory horn production was delegated to exceptional smiths who used metals tools to carve the instrument from an animal tusk; these smiths also carved decorative images and motifs on the exterior of the horn, as seen on object 90.1/ 1794. Herbert Lang, head of the Museum’s 1915 Congo expedition during which these horns were collected, noted that many of these horns were adorned with an anthropomorphic carving on the mouthpiece, a decorative motif also found on Mangbetu harps. The length of the horns could be extended by attaching a wooden tube to the larger end of the instrument, a device that also deepened the sound of the horn. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 202)

Ivory horn orchestras were primarily employed during court dances, such as the mabolo dance, in which the king danced alone in front of his subjects order to display his skill. The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the king’s prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).

Field Notes: A spear of the LOGO. The lower point of iron is used for killing or rather to finish a wounded animal which had broken the wooden part of the spear near the head. Usually the larger mammals when the spear has entered their bodies, breaks off the staff. The man in pursuit takes up either the undamaged lance or what is left of it. Usually the natives set out armed with two or three spears. All mammals such as elephants, rhinoceros, buffalo, lion, etc., are successfully hunted with spears.
