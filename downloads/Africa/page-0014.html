Curatorial Notes: This is a mask worn by women in the Sande women's regional association across Liberia, often associated with the Vai and Mende cultures. This mask, known as a bundu, is worn during celebrations and ceremonies marking the transition of a girl into womanhood and gives the wearer the full rights and responsibility to participate in the Sande society. A spiritual force embodying the feminine ideal is conferred upon the young woman when she dons the mask; the sowo spirit is channeled through her in the mask and costume and in moments of civic importance, she herself becomes an embodiment of the sowo-- a difficult to attain image of female beauty and spiritual power.

The social responsibility of the woman who wears this bundu is to demonstrate the political and social strength of the women's associate through her careful conduct in the performance of the duties of the sowo. The mask "embodies and mediates contradictions between traditional feminine role models in the society and the actual political and economic power which women do have access to, if not consistently, then at least regularly and generally enough for that power to call forth a significant symbolic focus in the ideological realm." (Dubinskas 1976) This mask is worn to spiritually consecrate the vital role of women's collectives in the political and cultural life of their communities.
