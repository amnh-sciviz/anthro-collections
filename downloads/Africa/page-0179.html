Curatorial Notes: This allo, or “Qur’an Board” is a copying and teaching tool utilized by Muslim clerics throughout Islamic Africa. Owing to the traditional expense and rarity of paper, day-to-day writing is carried out on the allo, which takes on an emblematic function for literacy in general (Hassan 1990). It is associated with peoples from the diverse Malinke language group, who are known for introducing Islam to a wide assemblage of cultures in West Africa via both trade activities and political influence through the malinke-speaking empire of Mali (Greenberg 1947). Allo are also known to be utilized extensively by the Hausa people in the region. The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups on the continent (Hassan), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg).

Among the Hausa, the clerics who use this technology are called Malams, from Arabic mu’alim “scholar.” Malams oversee the Muslim education of a Hausa community. Both oral tradition and scriptural or textual authority are central features of Islamic education, especially surrounding the Qur’an. There are three types of traditional schools in which the Malams teach: primary (Makarantar allo), secondary (Makarantar ilmi), and Qur’anic (Tsangaya). The last are a form unique to the Hausa, devoted almost entirely to rote memorization, writing, and recitation. The name of the first type of school, Makarantar allo, is derived from the arabic Maqra’ah al-loh “school of the board/slate,” since the allo is traditionally the primary tool for acquiring literacy. Some have opted to replace Makarantar allo with western education materials, a process associated with increased uniformity.

Girls and boys are taught separately from a young age, reflecting traditional gender segregation in Hausa societies. Most girls drop out before puberty, justified either as preparation for marriage or to prevent further mixing. This still continues, despite the lasting cultural impact of the Sokoto Caliphate, with it’s campaign for universal Islamic education, including women. The Malam does not expect regular compensation for their teaching, and regards what they do as a service. However, gifts of unfixed value are traditionally given in the middle of the week by children on behalf of grateful parents. The Makarantar schools are significant parts of any community, and participate heavily in public and religious festivals and times of need. As a way to strengthen national identity, Muslim education attained a greater deal of attendance among the Hausa under colonial rule than it had beforehand. Today, most young Hausa attain this primary level of education. The difficulty of learning a foreign literacy in a culture where it is not standardized conveys all the more prestige. Completion of the Makarantar allo is commemorated Saukar ceremony, wherein a student feasts, recites from the Qur’an, and receives the allo zayyanna, a highly decorated version of the allo that functions like a diploma. This ceremony is believed to have also originated in North Africa (Hassan).

Dimensions: L:235 D:67 (in CM)
Subject: COCOA POD SHAPE
Acquisition Year: 1991 [GIFT]
Donor: FULLER, MORTIMER & SUZANNE
Keywords: COFFIN
Category: CONTAINERS

Curatorial Notes: This casket, also known as a ‘fantasy coffin’ is modeled in the shape of a cacao pod originating from Accra, Ghana and acquired by the Museum in 1991. A fantasy coffin can take any form, ranging from a sneaker or tropical fish, to cell phones and nude women. The shape is dependent on the personality or profession of the deceased (Tschumi, 2014), for example, a fisherman may commission a fishing boat .
Modern fantasy coffins can be purchased by any individual who can afford the commision; however, these decorated coffins were originally reserved for the chiefs. During a chief's initiation he would be carried in a litter uniquely shaped to represent his familys emblem. The Ga view the initiation and burial of a chief as complementary events and, later, chiefs were buried in similarly shaped coffins. It was not until the 1960s that the public began to purchase their own representative coffins, a movement led by artists like Kane Kwei and Ataa Oko (Tschumi, 2014).
The cacao pod coffin is an artistic piece with market value, but it also represents the religion and culture of the Greater Accra region. Traveling to the ancestral world, which exists parallel to the material world, requires a soul (saman) to undergo an extensive journey (Gildea, 1963, p. 209). This journey is marked by a funeral, a celebration that is an accumulation of a persons life. The more extravagant the celebration the more honor and prestige is attributed not only to the individual who has passed but also to the family (Wigley, 2016). The coffin aids the body and soul on this journey and the shape permits the soul to continue living a life with the person's occupation or passions in the ancestral world. Furthermore, maintaining a positive relationship with ancestors is of the utmost importance because spirits play an active role in influencing relatives lives (Dodds, 1976). Paying proper respects with a funeral is important in maintaining this positive relationship.
Recently, Accra-based artists have been experiencing international fame and museums, art galleries, and other institutions are ordering coffins to be made to be shown in exhibits or art shows. This particular coffin is believed to have been constructed in the late twentieth century and was commissioned for an exhibit at the Center of African Arts (Schildkrout, 1992). The American Museum of Natural History has used the Cacao Pod Coffin twice since its acquisition, first in the 2003 Chocolate temporary exhibition and 15 years later in the 2017 Margaret Mead Film Festival to accompany the film Paa Jo and the Lion- a film documenting the artist, Paa Jo, and his artistic endeavors abroad in the UK (Wigley, 2016).
