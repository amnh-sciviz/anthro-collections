Curatorial Notes: This type of amulet is is associated with traveling Muslim clerics throughout West Africa, called Malam among the Hausa people of northern Nigeria. Practitioners of Islamic mysticism hold written language to be an instrumental part of universal order, an idea compatible with older West African belief systems. In “magical” practice, objects containing hidden slips of writing, such as these amulets, possess or transfer the words’ power. Often the effect is framed in context of the Arabic terms zahir (visible reality) and batin (hidden meaning) (Roberts 2007). This concealment is central to the function of such writing, so that the concepts of secrecy and magic are heavily associated. The Hausa word asiri ‘magic’ is derived from the arabic sir ‘secret.' The earliest spread of Islam in continental Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques.

There may be several types of text employed in these amulets, from written prayer, to names of God, designs, singular words, symbols of undisclosed meaning, or verses from the Qur’an. Some text, such as the names of angels or certain seals, were originally borrowed from Hebrew Kabbalistic tradition. Mystical grids, called Hatimi (from the Arabic Khatim, “seal”), incorporate coded configurations of numbers and words with assigned numerical value known only to the cleric. They may bestow good fortune and wealth, protection, or harm. The words be stored in the home, worn, or written out on flat surfaces to be washed off and drunk, in a process called rubutun sha. Various rules of conduct are followed by the cleric, the craftsperson, and the patron, in order for the amulets to maintain efficacy. The charm must be made in private, the paper usually folded. A leatherworker (sometimes metalworker) then encases the paper, creating an object anywhere from 1-12 inches. The process of concealment, plus any designs added to the surface of the charm, enhances potency (Hassan 1990).

Curatorial Notes: The Kuba Kingdom was located in the Zaire region, south-east of the modern day Democratic Republic of the Congo. The Kuba Kingdom was the most renowned producer of raffia cloth in Central Africa (Cooksey 2011: 109). This region is bordered by three rivers; the Sankuru, Lulua and Kasai Rivers. The Kingdom comprised an association of many linguistically-linked groups, which prospered in Zaire between the seventeenth and nineteenth centuries. During this time raffia cloth symbolically represented a family's wealth, was used in ceremonies and funerals and as well as in home decoration (Cooksey 2011: 109). Raffia cloth is a skillfully woven material made from the fibers of the Raphia vinifera palm. Raffia cloths offer insight into the ideology of collective work, as work from all members of the clan is necessary to fabricate the cloth. The palms were gathered and split by the children, woven by the men, and embroidered by the women. The most prominent group of the Kuba Kingdom were the Bushoong (also known as the Bambai), translating to the “people of the cloth.” Raffia cloth was a vital part of Kuba identity and life. Every group within the Kuba Kingdom created distinctly different cloths (Glazer 1996).

Geometric embroidery can be either symmetrical or asymmetrical. Geometric designs were often passed down from older women to younger girls. Embroidery was done by multiple women within one family on the same cloth; therefore many skill levels can be seen in the finished product (Cooksey 2011: 109).

Decorated geometric raffia cloth were most frequently worn as skirts during ceremonies and funerals. At a funeral, the body and the coffin are shrouded in cloths. Kuba people believe that the deceased person must be dressed in raffia garments so that they would be recognized by family members and other members of the clan when they pass into the land of the dead (Glazer 1996: 107). Failure to properly dress the deceased in raffia cloths was believed to provoke mween, evil spirits (Cooksey 2011: 109). The mourning period after a death typically lasted three to nine months. Women stayed in the home during this time, sewing and embroidering to replenish the family's collection of cloths. More ornate designs meant more time was spent making the cloth, increasing the cloth’s value (Cooksey 2011: 128).

Cloths were dyed and embroidered by women. The dyeing process occurred before the decorative stitching and embroidery is applied. Un-dyed raffia cloth is a tan/ecru color. Dyes used for cloths are usually acquired from regional plants. Certain dyes were used for certain purposes, for example, red is the color of mourning so red cloths are associated with funerals (Glazer 1996: 28). The pieces of cloth were traditionally sewn together before being embroidered in order to create long strips of material that can be worn as skirts. Skirts were traditionally worn wrapped around the waist, layer over layer. Raffia skirts are usually bordered on three sides of the cloth by either fringe or fabric.
