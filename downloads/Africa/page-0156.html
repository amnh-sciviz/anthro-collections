Exhibition History
• AFRICAN REFLECTIONS: ART FROM NORTHEASTERN ZAIRE. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. June 1990 - February 1994
Exhibition Label: Wooden trumpet with cowrie shells, ivory and resinous material. The Bongo, northern neighbors of the Azande, carved large wooden trumpets that often had sculpted heads at the top. The practice of carving heads on musical instruments probably originated in the Sudan and spread southward to the peoples of north eastern Zaire.

Curatorial Notes: The Bambara of Mali are Mande-speaking people who live on the Bani and Upper Niger Rivers who are predominantly agrarian. The Bambara people credit Ci-Wara, a spiritual being, with teaching them agriculture; it is under Ci-Waras guidance, they say, that they became successful and able farmers.

This antelope headdress is worn by Bambara male dancers, Ci-Wara (translated to farm animals). It is often worn in pairs of one male and female antelope. The antelope symbolizes the farmer who tills his fields tirelessly (Siegmann 2009: 62). Animals are often used as metaphors by the Mande, Roan and dwarf antelope are considered graceful and strong; aardvarks are determined and consciences. These are values associated with farming, where strength, stamina, patients, and foresight are all necessary to do a good
job (Phillips 1995: 502).

The headdress exemplifies the Bambara peoples devotion to agricultural cultivation and fertility. The organization of the Ci-wara performance is based on the Bamana respect for the power and efficiency of the union of male and female. As human reproduction is the result of the sexual union between man and woman, so agricultural fertility is attributed to the union between fire (the sun), an expression of the male principle, and earth and water, an expression of the female principle. (Colleyn 2001: 204).

The horns of the antelope represent ears of millet and the zigzag design of the mane represents the sun. The costume worn by the performers consists of raffia fibers that extend from the base of the headdress to the ground and represent the rain needed for cultivation.The female antelope has a child on its back depicting the way mothers carry their children, but also calling attention to animal, plant, and human fertility (Siegmann 2009: 62). The headdresses hold a parallel between the penetration of the earth through farming and copulation (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

The headdress is associated with different purposes, however, as one observer noted, like all other dances of the Bamana that of [Ci-Wara] was never static .the development and change took place somewhat independently in each region and indeed in each village (Lagamma 2002: 71). Likewise, each performance is unique and no one description will be able to convey the many tribal traditions.

It is used during agricultural competitions. During these competitions young farmers are galvanized by songs, music, and the masked dances. They seek to imitate the mythical animals that originally taught humans agriculture. During the competition the men work through a number of rows in a field and the first to completion is the winner and declared the Ci-Wara. The title brings much praise to the winner. Men are constantly invited to take up the contest, just as their ancestors had done before, and see how they measure up to the heroes of the past.

It is also used during entertainment and ritual events. They appeared during festivals at the Sgou court. After colonial conquest they were also a part of the July 14th Bastille celebrations, French National Day.

Third, it is used during an annual two day celebration that takes place between November and April, according to the moons appearance. This celebration includes sacrifices and the remaking of cult objects. It also includes a parade through the village with stops
a spots where the union of the male and female masks are symbolized (Colleyn 2001: 204-205).

Curatorial Notes: Popularized by the British in the late 1870s, tea drinking is a ritualistic in Moroccan culture. In Moroccan culture tea has taken the place of alcohol in societies as alcohol is not partaken in Islamic communities. Teahouses provide places to gather, similar to bars. The tea is poured back and forth between the glass and the pot three times and it is poured into the glass from two feet or higher, this is to aerate the tea to bring out the flavors similar to a glass of wine. In the house, women make the tea, although if there are guests the man of the house will always serve the tea.

Poorer families may have a simple aluminum barrahd while richer families may have more ornate teapots made of silver. They also may have accessories with the tea set such as a siniyya, a round table with feet that the teapot and glasses rest on. There are also rbaia, circular containers for tea, mint and sugar and a bahbur or samovar which is used to hold the water for tea making. Tea drinking is synonymous with hospitality and thus they are willing to spend more on an elaborate tea set or carry the extra weight of the teapot to make sure they are a generous host. The pressure of this also falls on the men to serve the tea although often the women may make the tea out of view of the guests.

Mint tea is widely considered Morocco’s national drink, in the north made with plenty of sugar and fresh mint to sweeten it, while in the south it is stronger and more bitter with only dried mint. In the north, it is brought to a boil once then the bitter water is discarded, then new water is added and brought to a boil, then more water along with the sugar. Meanwhile, in the south, water is not discarded and instead just added. This is because the north has abundant water, mint, and sugar while in the more arid south these are less available.

The tea is poured back and forth between the glass and the pot three times with sugar added for flavor. It is then poured into the glass for a final time from two feet or higher, this is to aerate the tea to bring out the flavors similar to a glass of wine. After the tea is poured for the guest they are meant to compliment the host on the tea. It is expected the guest drinks more than one but less than four glasses.

Curatorial Notes: This leather-bound Hadith text and hide gafaka case were donated by Dr. Joseph Greenberg, prominent linguist and anthropologist who studied around the Hausa center of Kano from 1938-39. Hadith, a major source of guidance for Muslims, contextualize the sacred writings of the Qur’an (or “Koran”) by documenting the Prophet Muhammad's words and accounts of his daily practice, called the Sunna (“Hadith”). The handwritten copying of books, even those not considered scripture, is still said to be a pious act for highly trained scholars, and the resulting manuscripts are prized by the Hausa (Hassan 1990).

The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the fourteenth century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

The earliest spread of Islam in Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco (Greenberg). The Muslim cultures of the Maghreb (Northwestern Africa above the Sahara) have exerted a great deal of influence ever since. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. The religion was mostly restricted to urban areas, among a literate elite which preserved older styles of written Arabic. The calligraphy shown in this text may be a local variant derived from the Maghribi (“western”) style, indicated by a form of the letter fa (Greenberg, Hassan).

Curatorial Notes: This hide-bound Qur’an (older spelling "Koran") and gafaka case (Hassan 1990) were donated by Dr. Joseph Greenberg, prominent linguist and anthropologist who studied around the Hausa center of Kano from 1938-39. Hadith, a major source of guidance for Muslims, contextualize the sacred writings of the Qur’an (or “Koran”) by documenting the Prophet Muhammad's words and accounts of his daily practice, called the Sunna (“Hadith”). The handwritten copying of books, even those not considered scripture, is still said to be a pious act for highly trained scholars, and the resulting manuscripts are prized by the Hausa (Hassan).

The Hausa are one of the oldest and largest Islamic groups in Africa (Hassan), and occupy parts of Nigeria, Niger, Benin, and many other Sub-Saharan African nations. Islam first reached the Hausa city states through the kingdom of Mali in the 14th century, and was reinforced through the Songhai empire two centuries later (Greenberg 1947). Under the Fulani Sokoto Caliphate (1804-1903), Kano would become a semi-independent Emirate, before being absorbed into British colonial Nigeria. Although around half the Hausa population were polytheistic when British rule began, nearly eighty percent of Hausa people today identify as Muslim (Hassan).

The earliest spread of Islam in Africa can be traced to 11th century overland routes that brought trade from Morocco (Greenberg). The Muslim cultures of the Maghreb (Northwestern Africa above the Sahara) have exerted a great deal of influence ever since. Local merchants were the first people in western Africa to adopt Islam. In the following century, kings began to willingly convert, building Islam into an organized force in the region through royal sanction and the creation of mosques. The religion was mostly restricted to urban areas, among a literate elite which preserved older styles of written Arabic (Greenberg, Hassan).
