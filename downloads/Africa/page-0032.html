More ornate designs meant more time was spent making the cloth, increasing the cloths value (Cooksey 2011: 128).

Dyes used for cloths are usually acquired from regional plants. Certain dyes were used for certain purposes, for example, red is the color of mourning so red cloths are associated with funerals (Glazer 1996: 28). The pieces of cloth were traditionally sewn together before being embroidered in order to create long strips of material that can be worn as skirts. Skirts were traditionally worn wrapped around the waist, layer over layer. Raffia skirts are usually bordered on three sides of the cloth by either fringe or fabric.

Children gathered and split the palms either by hand or with a raffia rib comb. These strands were then soaked in warm water for an hour or more until they are silk-soft. Men in the Kuba Kingdom wove the cloths on a single heddle loom, which is a simple device, composed of a heddle and two horizontal bars, between which the palms are woven. A plain weave cloth has horizontal and vertical threads crossing over and under one another. An experienced weaver couldve completed a cloth in a few hours (Glazer: 27). The size of the cloth is determined by the length of the raffia fibers. After a cloth was woven, it was often softened by wetting and pounding with a mortar.

Cut pile raffia were often worn as wrappers. Cloth could be used to mark a house or family during event of significance, or given as gifts to establish relations of reciprocity. When raffia cloths were worn as skirts, they showed the wearers status, wealth and social class. The most luxuriously decorated cloths were the most expensive and valuable. Wealthy people and the king can afford to have these skirts, but not exclusively. If a non-elite person could afford an elaborate skirt, he/she would be entitled to wear it. Fancier cloth does not announce a persons title, but instead implies their social or economic class (Glazer 1996: 128/129).

The palms were gathered and split by the children, woven by the men, and embroidered by the women. The most prominent group of the Kuba Kingdom were the Bushoong (also known as the Bambai), translating to the people of the cloth. Raffia cloth was a vital part of Kuba identity and life. Every group within the Kuba Kingdom created distinctly different cloths (Glazer 1996).

Geometric embroidery can be either symmetrical or asymmetrical. Geometric designs were often passed down from older women to younger girls. Embroidery was done by multiple women within one family on the same cloth; therefore many skill levels can be seen in the finished product (Cooksey 2011: 109).

Decorated geometric raffia cloth were most frequently worn as skirts during ceremonies and funerals. At a funeral, the body and the coffin are shrouded in cloths. Kuba people believe that the deceased person must be dressed in raffia garments so that they would be recognized by family members and other members of the clan when they pass into the land of the dead (Glazer 1996: 107). Failure to properly dress the deceased in raffia cloths was believed to provoke mween, evil spirits (Cooksey 2011: 109). The mourning period after a death typically lasted three to nine months. Women stayed in the home during this time, sewing and embroidering to replenish the family's collection of cloths. More ornate designs meant more time was spent making the cloth, increasing the cloths value (Cooksey 2011: 128).

Cloths were dyed and embroidered by women. The dyeing process occurred before the decorative stitching and embroidery is applied. Un-dyed raffia cloth is a tan/ecru color. Dyes used for cloths are usually acquired from regional plants. Certain dyes were used for certain purposes, for example, red is the color of mourning so red cloths are associated with funerals (Glazer 1996: 28). The pieces of cloth were traditionally sewn together before being embroidered in order to create long strips of material that can be worn as skirts. Skirts were traditionally worn wrapped around the waist, layer over layer. Raffia skirts are usually bordered on three sides of the cloth by either fringe or fabric.

Curatorial Notes: This object is a cup carved by the Kuba people of the Congo region who are a coalition of at least eighteen different ethnic groups who have united through mechanisms of an elaborate political system. The Kuba system of governance features a number of figures, including a king, called the nyim, who holds the most authority, and also lesser councils and representatives who wield a certain amount of power[1]. The Kuba are agriculturalists and cultivate a number of different resources. These include beans, manioc, raffia, and various other palms. The palms and the sap they produce are used in the making of palm wine. The sap is usually placed in gourds and fermented until a mildly alcoholic and acidic liquid develops.
[2]
This type of cup features prominently in the social lives of the Kuba people. Wealthy individuals often engaged in conspicuous consumption and would serve large quantities of palm wine to their guests. The consumption of palm wine is a social activity, not meant to be done alone[3]. Cups of this type provided a means for the Kuba to forge significant relationships and, by extension, community solidarity.

Drinking palm wine is an activity that is only undertaken in public by men. Although women do imbibe palm wine as well, they do so clandestinely. Female intoxication is considered deviant and women who are discovered to be drinking are socially castigated. This cup almost certainly was made for and used by a Kuba man[4].

These cups also speak to Kuba epistemologies beyond the role of palm wine as a social lubricant. Kuba cups, along with other aspects of their material culture and art need to be understood in the context and development of the kingship.[5] Bilotta, in her analysis, indicated that the artistry and craftsmanship of Kuba art, including cups, was a tribute to prosperity and the institution of kingship[6] and, as such, reproduced royal statuses[7]

Curatorial Notes: The Kuba Kingdom was located in the Zaire region, south-east of the modern day Democratic Republic of the Congo. The Kuba Kingdom was the most renowned producer of raffia cloth in Central Africa (Cooksey 2011: 109). This region is bordered by three rivers; the Sankuru, Lulua and Kasai Rivers. The Kingdom comprised an association of many linguistically-linked groups, which prospered in Zaire between the seventeenth and nineteenth centuries. During this time raffia cloth symbolically represented a family's wealth, was used in ceremonies and funerals and as well as in home decoration (Cooksey 2011: 109). Raffia cloth is a skillfully woven material made from the fibers of the Raphia vinifera palm. Raffia cloths offer insight into the ideology of collective work, as work from all members of the clan is necessary to fabricate the cloth. The palms were gathered and split by the children, woven by the men, and embroidered by the women. The most prominent group of the Kuba Kingdom, the Bushoong, were also known as the Bambai, thepeople of the cloth. Raffia cloth was a vital part of Kuba identity and life. Every group within the Kuba Kingdom created distinctly different cloths (Glazer 1996).

Children gathered and split the palms either by hand or with a raffia rib comb. These strands were then soaked in warm water for an hour or more until they are silk-soft. Men in the Kuba Kingdom wove the cloths on a single heddle loom, which is a simple device, composed of a heddle and two horizontal bars, between which the palms are woven. A plain weave cloth has horizontal and vertical threads crossing over and under one another. An experienced weaver couldve completed a cloth in a few hours (Glazer: 27). The size of the cloth is determined by the length of the raffia fibers. After a cloth was woven, it was often softened by wetting and pounding with a mortar.

Cut pile raffia were often worn as wrappers. Cloth could be used to mark a house or family during event of significance, or given as gifts to establish relations of reciprocity. When raffia cloths were worn as skirts, they showed the wearers status, wealth and social class. The most luxuriously decorated cloths were the most expensive and valuable. Wealthy people and the king can afford to have these skirts, but not exclusively. If a non-elite person could afford an elaborate skirt, he/she would be entitled to wear it. Fancier cloth does not announce a persons title, but instead implies their social or economic class (Glazer 1996: 128/129).
