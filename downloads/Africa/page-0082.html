Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

The Azande in the north, or the Mangebele and the Matchaga Bantu-speaking peoples in the south likely introduced harps to the Mangbetu during the mid nineteenth century (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 198). Harp production among the Mangbetu proliferated during the colonial period, in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Individual musicians used them mainly as an accompaniment to particular songs, and they held relatively little musical importance in comparison to the ivory horns, slit drums, and iron bells employed in court music. They were actively collected by European traders and agents, who valued them more for their aesthetic qualities than as musical instruments. By the 1930s, harps had virtually disappeared from Mangbetu music. (Ross Miller 1992 : 51)

Standard material utilized in harp construction were wood, plant fiber, and animal hide. Animal pelts, such as leopard or okapi, as well as pangolin scales, and reptile skins were used decoratively on the hourglass or oval resonator. The addition of these decorative materials emphasizes the production of Mangbetu harps as art pieces rather than functioning musical instruments. There are stylistic similarities between Mangbetu and Azande harps, such as the traditional five strings, and anthropomorphic carvings along the stem of the instrument. (Perani and Smith 1998: 225). Mangbetu harps typically portray an elongated head that reflects a cultural expression of beauty that is achieved through cranial binding in infancy. The style in figurative expression can also be seen on some Azande harps. Mangbetu and Azande harps are differentiated by the location of the pegs; the Mangbetu are located on the right side, while the Azande are on the left. Further, Mangbetu harps do not have legs attached to the resonator, an anthropomorphic form employed only by the Azande. (Perani and Smith 1998: 225)

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities. Music, and musical instruments themselves, held symbolic significance when in employed in particular social settings.

This is a stick zither, called a nenzenze (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 214). Collected by Herbert Lang on the 1915 Congo expedition, Lang indicates that this instrument was Made by the Mabutu, seldom used by the Mangbetu. Zithers and small hand-held finger pianos called sanzas were also present in Mengbetu culture, but were introduced by neighboring peoples, like the Mabutu or the Zande. The Mengbetu and the Zande engaged in a rich historical practice of sharing music with the neighboring Zande, resulting in fluid musical influence and exchange.

Curatorial Notes: The Mangbetu are groups of linguistically and culturally related peoples in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Like many neighboring West African peoples, their rich musical heritage has an important role in their communities.

Ivory horns were vital instruments for Mangbetu royalty; ivory has long been held as a highly prized material. The use of this precious ivory and the special labor involved in its production firmly linked ivory horns with the power of the king. Ivory horn production was delegated to exceptional smiths who used metals tools to carve the instrument from an animal tusk; these smiths also carved decorative images and motifs on the exterior of the horn, as seen on object 90.1/ 1794. Herbert Lang, head of the Museums 1915 Congo expedition during which these horns were collected, noted that many of these horns were adorned with an anthropomorphic carving on the mouthpiece, a decorative motif also found on Mangbetu harps. The length of the horns could be extended by attaching a wooden tube to the larger end of the instrument, a device that also deepened the sound of the horn. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 202)

Ivory horn orchestras were primarily employed during court dances, such as the mabolo dance, in which the king danced alone in front of his subjects order to display his skill. The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the kings prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).

The mabolo dance was designed to affirm the kings prestige because skillful dancing is equated with intelligence (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 205). Large ivory horns were also used to announce certain royal events, such as the mabolo dance, as well as the arrival and departure of a king, and victories in war. Smaller ivory horns, such as object 90.1/3660, were also carried by women during royal dances. Ivory horns were used to honor chiefs during the colonial period. (Schildkrout and Keim 1990: 203).

Field Notes: NEIBONNO - Are bowls for smoking tobacco. They are inserted in the heavy midrib of a banana leaf through which a channel for the air is established by passing repeatedly a slender stick or reed through it. The thicker end receives the pipe, both ends are cut obliquely. Very often they use as bonds only a leaf to which they give the shape of a funnel. The very small quantity (a pinch) of tobacco is pressed together in the hand and laid into these primitive pipes with a little red coal. They take one or several long inhalations in succession, open their mouth and let the smoke pass out slowly. It is evident that they swallow large quantities as they cough nearly always immediately afterwards and though in fact they make a very sour face, they pretend it is excellent, and are very fond of smoking. They often ask a gift of tobacco. At the present time the European pipe is introduced and much appreciated. There are a few tobacco-plants near every house, therefore it is certain that in every village tobacco is planted in more or less great quantities. I never saw actual plantations, which are not in use with the Mangbetu, as even their bananas which here form the basis of the general food are only existing in irregular agglomerations without any particular order. When they smoke they usually pass the pipe along their friends or incidental companions each one taking one or several inhalations.
