Curatorial Notes: Both the Maasai and Kikuyu have experienced cultural diffusion from intermarriage, frequent raiding, and instable trade relations throughout time. The Kikuyu live relatively close to Maasai territory, however their agriculturalist livelihood is completely opposite of Maasai nomadic pastoralism. They reside in the highlands and live off a typical agriculturalist diet of maize, millet, sweet potatoes, etc. There is a Maasai variation of this leglet called entuala enkelu, which is a leg ornament worn by warriors while dancing.

The bell or leglet is worn on the warrior’s thigh, specifically the right leg, while dancing during specific occasions, such as circumcision rites. It is usually worn with other ornaments such as armlets and other leglets. The bell is made from iron, which is attached to a leather strap adorned with cowrie shells and outlined by glass beads. Scholars suggest that some leglets were made from recycled leather ear ornaments previously worn by woman. Women could offer warriors these ornaments or they could be transformed into a leglet to sell at a higher price for the tourist market (Klumpp 1994: 17).

Curatorial Notes: The gourd, or ‘gourd water bottle’ is from the Maasai people whose territory consists of areas along the Rift Valley in Kenya and Tanzania. According to museum archives, this gourd may have been acquired specifically in the Laikipia reserve. The Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist culture, relying mainly on cattle for subsistence and wealth (Somjee 2000: 98).

Although this object is listed as a water bottle, the Maasai predominately use this type of object to carry milk (Palsson 1990: 83). A Maasai warrior most likely carried this object while herding cattle and participating in activities corresponding to his age-grade ('moran'). In addition, this object was intended for Maasai men based on the lack of beaded ornamentation. A Maasai woman’s version, called Enkukuri, contains elaborate beaded straps and a slightly different shape (Klumpp 1987: 103).

Early European travelers often feared the moran for their notorious reputation for violence and frequent raids. A member of the moran would typically obtain a gourd filled with milk left especially for him from his mother, who is in charge of milking and setting aside portions of milk for the members of the household unit (Ibrahim 2001: 88). The gourds are cleaned out with a combination of charcoal, cow’s urine, and water (Tjader 1910: 257). Maasai values, such as the importance of community, are shown through the use of this object. For instance, a warrior may not finish a gourd of milk or drink from it alone (Kaj 1989: 18). This is to avoid any sense of individuality or self-indulgence.

The tubular shape of the gourd is convenient for the Maasai because it can be easily slung over the shoulder with the attached leather straps. These gourds must be portable because of their nomadic livelihood.

Curatorial Notes: Historically, Maasai warriors, part of the moran, have worn such a frontlet pictured here. The Maa term for this object is called errap and incorporates two coiled metal projections on either side of the band. The terminology also can stand for curved lines or scallops, resembling horns of the cattle that are seen in the outline of the band (Klumpp 1987: 101, 290). It can be made of horn, ivory, or metal; in this case, it is made of leather and iron with attached beaded ornamentation. The strong/weak or contrasting/complementary beaded color patterns symbolize the male/female gender roles in Maasai society. It should be noted that this ornament is worn differently than how it is displayed. A Maasai warrior would wear this ornament turned so that the projections are worn on the front and back of the upper arm.

The Maasai travel through subsequent stages in life based on both age and accomplishments. The moran is a specific age-set in between uncircumcised boys and junior elders. Maasai men are initiated into the moran between the ages of twelve and eighteen, depending on their age at the point of the four or five year cycle (Hodgson 1999: 126). Maasai warriors herd cattle together, drink milk together, and sleep in homesteads together. This lasts until the warriors participate in the eunoto rite of passage and become junior elders, or ilpayioni. It is at this point that they must marry and give up their elaborate dress. The required constant community with other men as a warrior diminishes as the new elder establishes his own homestead, individuality, and political power.

A major characteristic of the moran is their obsession with their appearance. Warriors are the most decorated of the Maasai men. Assembling ornaments is a communal process among the moran. Each age-set’s ornaments usually have a collective theme. However, they are not allowed to make them themselves; it is strictly reserved for Maasai women. Their girlfriends would often make the ornaments as gifts.. This frontlet or armband is an example of just a small part of the moran personal ornamentation. There are multiple forms of armbands worn by warriors and is particularly a common element of Maasai warrior dress.

Curatorial Notes: This particular armlet, from the Maasai, is made from buffalo horn and wound with iron wire. The armlet is known as Inkatarri enkaina in the Maa language and is described as worn by both men and women (Klumpp 1987: 100). The Maasai live in both Kenya and Tanzania, however this armlet is categorized as being collected from the then Kenya Colony in 1940. Armlets such as these are common portable objects worn by Maasai warriors, called the moran. This object is on display at the Hall of African Peoples, along with numerous other ornaments worn by Maasai warriors. These include a headdress, headband, and various types of armlets. Ornaments worn by Maasai men contain less beading than those worn by women. The moran are known for elaborately decorating themselves in ornaments made by Maasai women in order to show off and stand out amongst their group.

Like many other forms of Maasai art, material is derived from their cattle. Cattle is represented often either from skins or horn, or cattle patterns and symbolism found in other objects such as beaded neckpieces. Maasai are a nomadic pastoralist group and so cattle are an extremely important part of their livelihood and religion. The animals provide milk and blood for sustenance and are also sold or traded for other goods. There are societal and religious customs for consuming milk or blood from cattle and is a central part to Maasai lifestyle.

Curatorial Notes: The Tuareg are ethnically connected to the Berber peoples of North Africa but they maintain distinct social, cultural and political identities. The Tuaregs have historically lived a nomadic pastoralist lifestyle, which continues on in a significant capacity today. They have been moving constantly across borders of the North Africa Saharan regions of Niger, Mali, Morocco and Algeria. Traditionally the Tuareg people practiced Animism, however as Islam entered North Africa 7th century, Tuaregs converted to Islam. Today, most Tuaregs are Muslim, but there is still a significant role for animist practices in contemporary North African social life.

Since prehistoric times, the Tuareg have dominated the Sahara and have been using caravans to trade in the Trans-Saharan trade. Tuareg merchants were responsible for bringing gold from the southern edge of the Sahara into the northern part of Africa and Europe. However, around the 20th century, European trains and trucks began to subverted and seized control of the Trans-Saharan trade and the French army attempted to imperialize the Tuareg areas. The Tuaregs are known throughout African history to be great warriors and their fighting skills were especially evident in this time, as they defeated the French colonial army and maintained their independence.

This sword, called the Takouba, originated from the Tuareg group. Most of the tribe’s inhabitants live throughout the Sahara Desert and parts of North Africa. Many medieval Arabic writing has described the Tuareg as being great warriors and notorious highwaymen, enforcing heaving tolls on traders crossing the Trans-Sahara. Their warrior attire consisted of agher, a 1.5 meter high shield and the takouba, usually about one meter in length with a leather covered handle and a pommel made out of metal or copper. Predominantly a Muslim group, the Tuareg also often engraved religious texts onto their swords, a common Muslim practice from a belief religious text would bring warriors good fortune on the battleground.
