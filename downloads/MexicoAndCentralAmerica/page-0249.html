Curatorial Notes: Huichol used votive bowls to make offerings to the gods and these were considered to be effective ways to send prayers to them, when the gods drink from the bowls they "drink in" the prayers as well. This votive bowl is dedicated to Father Sun. The Norwegian explorer Carl Lumholtz collected it and over 5,500 other ethnological and archaeological items on AMNH-sponsored expeditions to northern Mexico between 1890 and 1897.

Curatorial Notes: The Huichol make small chairs of bamboo and twine. Such chairs, God's chairs, are brought out for festivities to help bring the gods to people. Often small objects, symbolic prayers, are hung on the chair or placed on the seat. This chair is part of a large collection, over 5,500 ethnological and archaeological items, made by the Norwegian explorer Carl Lumholtz during his 5 and 1/2 years' travel in northern Mexico starting in 1890. Published in Lumholtz' Unknown Mexico.

Curatorial Notes: Huichol figure of Elder Brother, the god of wind or air and hikuli (peyote) is made of solidified volcanic ash. The red, yellow and white stripes symbolize rain. The bird on his chest is the hawk called Piwami. The Norwegian explorer Carl Lumholtz collected this figure and over 5,500 other ethnological and archaeological items on AMNH-sponsored expeditions to northern Mexico between 1890 and 1897. In his Symbolism of the Huichol Indians, (AMNH Memoirs vol.III, pt.I, 1900) Lumholtz writes that this figure is "a reproduction of one that stands in the neighborhood of Pochotita, near Santa Catarina." Also published in National Geographic, June 2000.

Curatorial Notes: Hats that the Huichol braid and sew from palm leaf strips and decorate with small crosses and tassels of red flannel strips and tails of grey squirrels are worn by men on their pilgrimage to find the sacred hikuli (peyote) cacti for a religious ceremony. A narrow homemade ribbon under the wearer's chin helps keep the hat on his head. The Norwegian explorer Carl Lumholtz collected it and over 5,500 other ethnological and archaeological items on AMNH-sponsored expeditions to northern Mexico between 1890 and 1897. Published in Lumholtz' Unknown Mexico.
