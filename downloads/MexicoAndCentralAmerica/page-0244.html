Curatorial Notes: Cylinder Vase. Orange clay with red, white, brown, and black paint. Design features two seated figures with long stemmed lotus flowers and a glyph band below exterior rim. It shows a spectacular headdress. It appears to be composed of long feathers and a bunch of fluffy plumes mounted at the end of a long quill extending from the front of the head. The painting portrays an idealized Maya type, stocky and corpulent. The ring of glyphs around the tops is more decorative than meaningful. The inside rim is painted to match the outside, and a series of long brush strokes carry the color into the interior, an effective technique typical of many of these vessels.

SCULPTURE, FOUR HUMAN FIGURES, SCARIFICATION SCENE
MEXICAN & CENTRAL AMERICAN ARCHAEOLOGICAL COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 30.3/ 2559
Locale: WEST MEXICO, NAYART
Country: MEXICO
Period: LATE FORMATIVE/CLASSIC, circa B.C. 300 - A.D. 300
Material: CLAY, PIGMENT
Dimensions: L:13.8 W:8 H:9.2 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1989 [GIFT]
Donor: SCHNELL
Keywords: SCULPTURE
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: Pottery sculpture of a ritual bloodletting or scarification practice. The arranged scene includes four figures, one of whom is piercing the cheek of another. Bloodletting, particularly of the cheek or tongue, was a ritual activity often engaged in by the ruling elite to propitiate important ancestors and deities. Small scenes of daily life and ritual activity, such as this one, are found throughout West Mexico. The surface of this ceramic is decorated with red slip and black paint.
Exhibition History
• BODY ART: MARKS OF IDENTITY. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. November 1999 - May 2000
Exhibition Label: Scarification scene. Ceramic. 300 B.C.-A.D. 300. Cheek piercing was a rite practiced at West Mexican funerals and other important events. Carried out on both men and women, cheek piercing is related to the general Mesoamerican practice of offering blood to the deities and ancestors during ceremonies.
