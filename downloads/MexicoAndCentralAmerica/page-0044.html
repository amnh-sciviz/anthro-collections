Curatorial Notes: This large jade sculpture is in the form of an axe or adze, though it was most certainly used for ceremonial purposes. The adze takes on the form of a were-jaguar, or were-jaguar baby, characterized by the snarling mouth with fangs and feline nose. Were-jaguars are half-human, half-jaguar creatures that are often found in Olmec style art. Jaguars were considered to be very powerful animals and were often associated with the ruling elite, as well as with shamans who had to ability to transition between the human and spiritual world. This axe is named after Tiffany executive George Kunz, who donated the object to the American Museum of Natural History in 1891. The Kunz Axe is often attributed to the Olmec culture because of its stylistic features, but AMNH records indicate it was collected in the state of Oaxaca, Mexico.
