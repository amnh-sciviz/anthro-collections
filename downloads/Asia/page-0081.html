Exhibition History
• BODY ART: MARKS OF IDENTITY. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. November 1999 - May 2000
Exhibition Label: Shoes. Silk, cotton, linen, leather, metal, metallic threads, lacquer, glass, paper, wood, plastic. c. 1900. Bound feet were shaped to look like unopened lotus flower buds. To emphasize the delicacy and perceived beauty of their small feet, Chinese girls and women made "lotus shoes" of exquisite workmanship. Men were never supposed to see unbound feet, but were instead meant to gaze at the dazzling tiny shoes. Women even slept in soft-soled slippers to hide their feet from view at night.

Researchers' Remarks
This rod puppet clothed in silk is from the Hebei province in China. The use of silk material by a puppeteer often depended on the availability of the material, actual costs and other factors. The fact this puppet is clothed in silk could be emblematic of a period where Buddhist monasteries were in the business of accepting silk donations to be worn as ritual clothes or sold in the marketplace in exchange for funds. While rod puppets come in all shapes and sizes, this puppet is relatively small, only eleven to eighteen inches in length, which means itinerant puppeteers probably performed with it. To operate the puppet, a single performer deftly uses a central rod to control the body and two smaller poles to manipulate the hands. The puppeteer, the Chinese say, “In one breath, tells a thousand ancient tales, and with two hands creates the dance of a million soldiers.” This puppet represents the monk, Hsuan T’Sang [Xuanzang], from the story, Monkey-King: Journey to the West, who in the 7th century A.D. pilgrimaged to India in search of Buddhist manuscripts. Along his spiritual journey T’sang is confronted with different earthly temptations he must resists in order to complete the mission. The story is significant in China for its metaphorical qualities. The monk symbolizes the endurance of man and his ability to resist greed, delusion, and ill will, which other characters in the play – the Pig, the Monkey, and the Sandy Monk – attempt to corr.
