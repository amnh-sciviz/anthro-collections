Researchers' Remarks
This elaborately brocaded sleeveless women’s coat comes from the Sikkim culture and is composed of plain weave maroon wool alternating with narrow weft weave, a technique that involves using threads that run from side to side. Sikkim is the least populated and second smallest Indian state. It is bordered by Nepal, Tibet, Bhutan, and West Bengal, creating a diverse geographical setting as well as a varied cultural landscape. Due to the extreme climate conditions of these mountainous regions, wool and woolen cloth were and remain vital to area trade. At certain times throughout the past few centuries, the production and trade of wool has been so important that practical guidelines for its exchange are laid out in treaties along with new borders. Clothing in Tibet tends to be conservative, and while western styles have become common, traditional clothing is still valued and worn. This garment could be considered a Sikkim kho, a sleeveless, loose traditional dress fastened at the neck and tied using a scarf at the waist. The stripes of blues, greens, and red along with gold metallic brocaded shoulder bands show that this coat belonged to someone of high social status, as brocade was a luxury textile worn predominantly by the royal family, nobility, and the religious elite. Different materials serve different cultural needs: wool serves a utilitarian purpose, protecting the wearer from the elements, while silk is used for adornment and to denote status. MICHELE CARLUCCI. June 13, 2016.
