Researchers' Remarks
This rosary was collected from Bhutan in 1997, and it has two components—threads that are made out of cotton and beads that are made out of bones. Prayer beads have been an enduring object within the Buddhist traditions in Asia since they are a very personal religious article. The Sanskrit word for rosary is “Japamala” or “Mala,” which literally means “garland for recitation.” The string of this rosary is an important part due to its symbolism; it is made from nine threads, which represent eight people who take on the Buddha teachings and Vajradhara – the ultimate Buddha. The act of stringing the thread of the mala is central; several traditional texts and contemporary monks teach how to do this properly. The use of the cotton string is not only needed when creating the prayer bead but repeatedly throughout its lifetime, given that they are commonly restrung after repeated use. A mala is a very personal item, which would explain the need to restring if this were to be ripped. The materials and the number of beads in a rosary are determined according to the type of religious practice that is going to be performed. Some malas might be used to count prayers, others to count prostrations for divination and fortune telling. This particular rosary has bone beads carved as skulls that are meant to evoke meditation about death and fragility of life. XIMENA VIAL. June 13, 2016.

• BODY AND SPIRIT: TIBETAN MEDICAL PAINTINGS. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. January 2011 - July 2011
Exhibition Label: Too Much or Too Little? Pregnancy and childbirth, overwork, old age and other conditions can deplete ones physical resources and cause so-called anabolic or deficiency diseases, as shown in the top two rows. In Tibetan medicine, therapy for deficiency diseases could include rest, a diet that includes butter, beer and sugar or mild enemas and massage, all aimed at restoring strength in depleted patients. Other patients are said to suffer from diseases of excess, or so-called catabolic ills, such as constipation, lethargy and overeating. Therapy might include fasting followed by a light diet, exercise and, for some patients, more extreme measures like purgatives and bloodletting. Gentle Therapy: This patient is being treated with a compress for an unidentified disease of excess. He may also have been prescribed a diet of light and easily digested food, as well as other therapies. (5th row, 2nd image)

Exhibition History
• BODY AND SPIRIT: TIBETAN MEDICAL PAINTINGS. AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. January 2011 - July 2011
Exhibition Label: Treatment Points and Bloodletting. Front View. The painting maps the locations of points on the body used for bloodletting, minor surgery and moxibustion. In traditional Tibetan medicine, these three treatments are viewed as being relatively extreme and they are generally used only when milder therapies dont work. The moxibustion points are shown in red, bloodletting points in blue and surgical points in yellow. In moxibustion, cones or rolls of burning leaves are placed on the points. To withdraw blood, a point is opened with a sharp instrument such as a lancet. Surgery usually consists of lancing abscesses or infections and removing pus or cauterizing wounds. Blood can be taken from a number of points on the nose and mouth, including on the tongue and in the middle of the lower lip.

Exhibition Label: A Catalog of Disease. The painting details the causes of particular diseases and disease categories. It classifies diseases according to their humoral nature. Wind diseases are shown in the first section, indicated by the cock. The catalog of bile diseases begins with the image of a snake and that of phlegm diseases with a pig. Tumors are caused by an imbalance of all three humors and are associated with a disparate set of other factors, including overwork - such as carrying rocks, shown here - as well as indigestion, childbirth, harmful demons, sitting on damp ground and past deeds (bad karma).
