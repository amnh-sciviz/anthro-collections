Researchers' Remarks
Ice creepers of the Bering region are made from ivory, bone, or iron (Redenko 1961: 61; Bogoras 1904: 507). Based on museum collections, ivory ones were most common among Yupik and Chukchi. Both groups are sea mammal hunters or trade with them, making walrus ivory readily available. Ice creepers are used to walk on slippery ice, especially in the spring; the spikes grip the ice and help with traction (Smithsonian). They are lashed to the soles of boots at the arch of the foot. Attached using hide straps, they can be taken off once they are no longer needed. One known example of ice creeper use is wearing them while cutting a pathway for boats in sea ice (ibid.). Useful in daily life, ice creepers may also be invoked as a tool in love magic (Bogoras 1901: 478, 507). Ice creepers have been known to the region for at least 2000 years and have been found in archaeological excavations. (Smithsonian). LANI H. WYMAN, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY COHORT 2019. April 30, 2019.
