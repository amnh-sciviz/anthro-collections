Researchers' Remarks
In Manipur, weavers use materials such as silk, cotton and Santhak [a fiber derived from the inner bark of the Indian stinging nettle (Urtica dioica) and similar in texture to hemp fiber] to create textiles ranging from clothing to carpets. Manipuri looms would not have come in contact with wool fibers until perhaps as recently as the 1980s when weavers in the region began deconstructing Western textiles made of wool and using those fibers principally in the production of chadar, traditional Islamic women’s headscarves. [2 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
This loom came to the American Museum of Natural History through Indian Anthropologist and one-time head of the University of Calcutta’s Anthropology Department Panchanan Mitra and his research assistant J.K. Bose in 1934. Both individuals would have distinguished careers studying the native cultures of what would become eastern India and Bangladesh. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
Pang lyong looms are operated using a combination of the weavers hands and feet to manipulate the cloth warp to create patterns of varying complexity in a manner not unlike commercial looms used today. Looms akin to this one can be found throughout Asia and the world. [3 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
Peoples in the Indian region of Manipur, skirting the border between India and Myanmar and culturally inclusive of parts of Bangladesh, use a variety of looms to produce intricate textiles. This particular loom is known locally as a Pang Iyong, a loom where a shuttle, a device holding yarn or string, is thrown repeatedly through a tight warp of additional yarn or string. [1 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.

FIGURE, ELEPHANT WITH RIDER AND LOG
ASIAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 70.0/ 6345 AB
Country: SIAM
Material: WOOD (TEAK), PIGMENT, METAL
Dimensions: A) L:65 W:33 H:68 B) L:44.5 W:12 H:11 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1935 [GIFT]
Donor: DAVIS
Keywords: FIGURINE
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: This figure illustrates the kind of labor performed by tamed elephants in the rainforests of Thailand, deploying their immense strength to drag entire trees out of areas without roads. A century ago, more than sixty percent of Thailand was covered with forests full of teak and other valuable trees. An estimated 100,000 wild elephants lived in these forests at this time. Since elephants do not breed well in captivity, these worker elephants were either wild elephants that had been captured and tamed, or the offspring of tamed females and wild males. Currently it is estimated that there are fewer than 2,400 wild elephants left in Thailand. Logging is closely controlled in Thailand, and Thai organizations dedicated to elephant conservation are proliferating. Elephants draw large numbers of tourists to special centers where they can interact with and learn more about them.
