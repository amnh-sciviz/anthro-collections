Curatorial Notes: Traditionally, Tajik hunters in western Afghanistan used chireh textiles to camouflage or hide themselves from their prey. The zoomorphic motifs, intended to depict animals native to the Central Asian habitat, share many aesthetic similarities with those found on ancient architecture and ceramics. Hunters typically hung chirehs on bushes and gazed at their prey through small holes in the cloth. Others placed the textiles inside lodges, in accordance with pre-Islamic shamanist beliefs that the pictorial representations would appease the spirits of the hunted animals and some chireh were sewn into hooded masks. As functional objects, chireh are usually produced on cheap, mass-produced pieces of cotton and discarded after a few uses. Anthropologist Louis Dupree, who acquired this textile during his many years of acquaintance with Afghanistan, noted that it had been embroidered by a bride, in contrast with most chireh which were either painted or printed by a male artisan.
