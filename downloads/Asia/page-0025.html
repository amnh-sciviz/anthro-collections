Curatorial Notes: The most skillful hunter stood in the bow of the boat, where he delivered the first strike to the whale with his harpoon. The whale would dive, pulling the boat by the harpoon line. When the whale came up again to blow, the nearest hunters drove more harpoons into it. After the whale tired from a series of harpoon strikes, the hunters came in closer and killed the whale with lances like the one displayed here. All of the boats joined in towing the carcass to the village. This harpoon shaft is made of whale bone and the lashing around it is made of baleen.

Curatorial Notes: Carved from reindeer antler, this object may have been used as a charm by an inland Koryak hunter on a dangerous and potentially life-threatening hunt, or it may have been carved to barter for tobacco and other goods from Russian traders. At the time that this piece was made, Koryak hunters celebrated the bear hunt with dances and festivals, which included a “home-sending” ceremony, similar to the Ainu iomante bear ceremony. Today, the work of virtuoso Koryak carvers appears in art galleries and smaller pieces are sold as souvenirs.
