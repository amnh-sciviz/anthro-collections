SUNGKA BOARD
ASIAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 70.1/ 2361
Culture: VISAYAN
Locale: LEYTE ISLAND
Country: PHILIPPINES
Date: circa 1900 A.D.
Material: WOOD
Acquisition Year: 1905 [PURCHASE]
Donor: JESUP, MORRIS K.
Keywords: GAME BOARD, MANCALA GAME
Category: RECREATIONAL ARTIFACTS

Curatorial Notes: Sungka boards are used for mancala games, characterized by rows of holes and a proportionate number of identical playing counters. The sungka game has two players, each of them owning one row of holes and one enlarged storage hole at the far end of the board. The holes are filled with cowrie shells, seeds or stones. In the Philippines, they usually put seven counters in each hole. The purpose of the game is to gain the majority of the playing counters by spreading them one-by-one around the board and capturing where the move ends. The captured counters accumulate in the enlarged storage hole and the game ends when one player has no more counters with which to play. There are several sets of rules known for sungka and many more for mancala games in general. Sungka is commonly played by women and children. In South East Asia it is known to have been played by all parts of the population including royalty. This sungka board may have been made only for decorative purposes: the holes are small and it has a carved sheep forming the base.
