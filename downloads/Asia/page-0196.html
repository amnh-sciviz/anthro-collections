Researchers' Remarks
Textiles play a pivotal role for the Chin, who wear them as status symbols and for practical reasons. The Chin are spread over the rugged terrain of western Myanmar, North-Eastern India and Eastern Bangladesh and can be divided into 50 to 70 distinct groups. Backstrap looms, also referred to as loin looms or body-tension looms, are one of the oldest devices for weaving. They are made out of short, readily available sticks, which can easily be assembled where required. Weaving is predominantly and sometimes even exclusively women’s work, with men, depending on the tribe, executing certain tasks, such as spinning cotton or constructing parts of the loom. This backstrap loom represents a unique symbiosis of cotton warp and silk wefts. While cotton, together with flax and hemp, was grown locally, silk was being imported at the beginning of the 10th century from China and gradually used to create entire garments. The pattern in progress on this loom is a discrete diamond motif, which originated in the 19th century. When finished, this piece of cloth might have been sewn together to create a woman’s skirt. The backstrap loom was collected by the anthropologist F.K. Lehman during his expedition (commissioned by the AMNH) to Burma, now Myanmar, from 1957 to 1958 with the help of his local assistant Lian Uk. SOPHIA MUELLER. June 13, 2016.
