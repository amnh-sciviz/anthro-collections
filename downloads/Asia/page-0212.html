Curatorial Notes: The nomadic populations of Afghanistan have historically relied on their goats, sheep, and camels for survival. Nomadism is a way of life for many distinct ethnic groups. Most migrate seasonally, taking their herds to the mountains in the spring, and back to the lowlands in the winter with the camels transporting between 300-­400 pounds on their backs - tents, food, children - anything that might be needed in the winter or summer camp. Nomadic pride is reflected in the objects used to adorn these hard-working camels.

Scholar Louis Dupree, who collected this camel headdress in the 1960s, described how the nomadic maldar women on a fall migration “decorate their camels with brightly colored trappings and drape valued rugs or cloth over the saddle to create a kaleidoscopic impression that arouses the envy of townswomen in every village through which they pass.” (Louis Dupree, Afghanistan, 1976).

The nomadic lifestyle is increasingly difficult to sustain in present-day Afghanistan, where severe drought and residential development have reduced pastureland and diminished herd populations.
