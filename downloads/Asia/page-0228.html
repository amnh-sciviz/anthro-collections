Researchers' Remarks
Bells are one of the oldest pieces of continuously utilized technologies in the world. Bells have been used by those who herd animals for literally thousands of years and are still used throughout the world today. These bells, from the region of Kerman in southeastern Iran, assisted herders of either sheep or goats as hair from both is present in the regions carpets. [1 / 3]. These intricate rugs and carpets, which made Kerman famous around the world, all have their origins a variety of domesticated animals kept in the area. These animals include domesticated sheep domesticated sheep (Ovis aries), domesticated goats (Capra aegagrus hircus) and dromedary camels (Camelus dromedarius). [2 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
The finest Kerman rugs are made of goat wool known locally as kork. Kork, often called mohair in the West, are the fibers found closer to the goat’s body and are differentiated from taftik wool by its softness, which is an important attribute in woven goods. [3 / 3]. Curatorial Notes: In the 1980s, the wool pakol gained global popularity from its association with Afghani freedom fighters resisting the Soviet invasion. In fact, the pakol originated in the neighboring country of Pakistan where it replaced the turban in the early 20th century. This hat comes from Chitral, Pakistan where the best pakol are said to be produced today. A Chitral pakol is woven from Shu wool unique to a breed of sheep known as Kailey which can only be found in this region of Pakistan.

Pakols are a source of warmth in cold seasons. The thick brim can be rolled down to cover the ears, then rolled up in warmer weather when the hat is worn much like a western beret. Pakol are made in a range of colors including dark brown, camel, light brown, red, black, and grey and are sometimes adorned with peacock feathers and elaborately designed embroidery.
