Curatorial Notes: In the Thak Khola valley of Nepal, several different ethnic groups have historically traded raw wool and wool products. Carding is one of the first stages of wool production, and occurs after the wool has been collected from sheep and other fiber-producing animals. The wool is picked into clumps, which is sometimes prewashed to remove dirt and grease. The person carding holds a carder in each hand, with the pins facing together. A clump of wool is placed on one carder, which is held steady while the other carder is used to brush the wool. The repeated brushing action transfers the wool from one carder to the other while simultaneously breaking apart clumps and aligning fibers in the same direction. This is essential for subsequent production activities like spinning and weaving since poorly aligned fibers will generate low quality yarn. Carding also removes dirt and makes it easier for the person carding to pick out plant matter and other debris. Carding is also used to blend different wool types including fiber of various colors and from separate species. After carding, wool is usually spun into yarn, but unspun wool is used for felting.
