Researchers' Remarks
This fez, from the Druze culture of Lebanon, is part of a costume that would have been worn by a sheikh, a respected member of the religious elite. The Druze are a minority group living predominantly in Syria, Lebanon, Israel, and Jordan. In Lebanon, the small Druze population is concentrated in the mountainous regions surrounding Beirut. Although considered heterodox by some Muslims, the Druze religion has its basis in Islam. Its main tenet is monotheism, and no outsiders are permitted to convert. The Druze religion is secretive even to its members, as only religious elites have full access to its knowledge. This tradition of esotericism in addition to the practice of severe self-discipline and the rejection of physical pleasure has led some to consider the Druze religion a Sufi tradition. Wool has had an important place in Sufism, the Islamic mystical tradition. It symbolizes the insignificance of the physical world and human suffering, with the most devout Druze sheikhs generally wearing wool clothing– a wool abba (outer garment) over a black wool shirt and black wool pants. While extremely conservative or traditional Druzes might only wear hand-woven textiles, modernization has crept into the material culture. Wool is a suitable textile for the extreme climate conditions of the mountainous regions of Lebanon as they are subject to great fluctuations in weather conditions and contain a wide span of elevations. MICHELE CARLUCCI. June 13, 2016.

Researchers' Remarks
The backstrap loom, also known as the loin loom or body loom, revolutionized the way silk was produced and sold in Asia. Prior to the introduction of this technology, it could take an individual weeks, or even months to turn thread into the fabric needed for a single garment. Looms however, allowed work to be created much faster and in a wider array of patterns. This tool is not exclusive to any particular area of the world; in fact it is utilized by many different cultures across a wide span of time. After silk was brought to India through the Silk Road, this device became the most common instrument employed by the Nagaland culture in the northeast region of the country. Naga women were traditionally responsible for using the backstrap looms to create silk fabrics for both daily and ceremonial use. After harvesting the silk fibers from silkworms, women turn the thread into fabric through weaving. This loom consists of a continuous warp stretched between two parallel bamboo pieces, one piece is tied to a post or door and the other end held by a strap worn around the weaver’s lower back. Because the technique involves the physical body, women develop a close relationship with their craft. In the twenty-first century, there are many different methods that can be used to produce silk fabric. However, the Naga women continue to use the backstrap loom because it is low cost and deeply ingrained in their identity. DANIELLE ZWANG. June 13, 2016.
