Researchers' Remarks
This rug is from Erbent (near Ashgabat) Turkmenistan. These rugs, and rugs from surrounding areas, are highly sought after. Like many traditional rugs from this area, an identifier of a Turkmenistan rug is the dark boarder surrounding detailed patterns in the center. The spiral patterns seen here, also known as “rams horn” patterns are placed symmetrically throughout the rug. The rounded patterns present may be interpreted as a symbol representing the world and universe at large. This rug was made using a felting technique that requires wool to be agitated into a solid form. This involves the longer fibers to be laid out and layered in the shape of the initial rug, wet with warm water, and rolled up securely using a mat made of reeds. Once rolled up and secure many people help to felt the wool. Often the mat is carried on the ground by animals. Animal fibers have many plates on the hairs, and the more the fiber is agitated and dragged around, the stronger the bonds and tangles becomes between fibers creating a solid sheet of felt. Detailed patterns are then sewn in using a different color of felt. Felt rugs made in the Turkmenistan area in the past served primarily as a vessel. This type of rug is used today for many activities ranging from a table for outdoor meals, to prayer rugs. Color can be an identifier of a rug’s purpose. Lighter colored rugs are usually meant for religious purposes, so this rug was most likely not used for prayer. MELISSA CHASE. June 13, 2016.
