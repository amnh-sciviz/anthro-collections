Researchers' Remarks
This personal sized Yupik tobacco bag was collected along the Siberian Coast of the Bering Sea. Historically, it was worn on belts of both men and women. The squirrel hide most likely originates from a type of arctic ground squirrel and the sinew from a whale or reindeer. The reindeer fur demonstrates a possible trading connection with tundra based Reindeer Chukchi. Tobacco from the Russians was an essential and highly desirable trade commodity. At fairs, for example the Anui fair, tobacco from Russian officials was traded for native contributions including walrus, seal, fox, or reindeer. Lieutenant George Thornton Emmons sold his collections from 1894 to the AMNH, shortly before the Jesup expedition. JACQUELINE DEVEREAUX, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY COHORT 2019. April 26, 2019.

Researchers' Remarks
This wrench was likely made from walrus, seal, whale, or caribou bone, though its unique shape may indicate antler. The process of making this tool may have included soaking the bone in water for several days to weaken the outer layers for shaping. A stone drill was likely employed to drill the center hole. The bone returned to its natural hardness once dry. The wrench may have also been “fire-hardened” or packed in ashes for added durability, though this technique was more common for wooden tools and has debated efficiency. George T. Emmons catalogued this item as a men’s tool used for tasks similar to Western wrenches but does not give any more specificity. Few other Arctic wrenches survive, and most are dedicated for arrow-shaft bending or straightening (See Smithsonian Collections object 82A00003, for example). Its center hole resembles the shape of these arrow shaft wrenches, but the four protruding handles of this object greatly differ from the single stemmed shaft wrench. The delicacy and balance of this Yupik object might not have supported the repeated single spot pressure exerted on the arrow shaft by the drilled hole, indicating that this wrench may not have been used for such a purpose. It is possible that this wrench, with its four prongs and centered, squared hole, was used as a handle to turn an axle working to shorten, lengthen, or tighten the ropes of sails or fishing nets. It may have also been used as a grip for superior handling, like a k. JACALYN MALSICH, COLUMBIA MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY COHORT 2019. May 3, 2019.
