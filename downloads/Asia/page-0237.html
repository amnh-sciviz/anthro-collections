Researchers' Remarks
The Fluffing Bow and Cotton Working Board from Ban Nong Kha, Tambon Tha Tum, Mahasarakham province, located in North-Eastern Thailand, is used for the first step in the creation of cotton thread. While men nearly exclusively make the tools used to process cotton and silk, women do the actual processing with the tools. They remove the seeds from the raw cotton, and then gin, spin, dye and weave the cotton threads on backstrap looms. Historically, the way people wore and produced textiles defined gender roles. Besides being the primary means of decorating Tai homes and surroundings, they also marked the division of labor, signaled changes in status and liminal periods in rites of passage, and commemorated important occasions. Due to the increased global demands for textiles, the textile industry in Thailand has undergone considerable change. When this instrument was collected the village Ban Nong Kha produced most of its own textiles. By the 21st century, weaving often occurs under exceedingly unhealthy and exploitative conditions in sweatshops and workshops. The Fluffing Bow and Cotton Working Board were collected by Professor Charles F. Keyes and his wife Jane Keyes. Both have been involved in research in Thailand for over half a century. The village of BA:N NO:NG KHA in Tambon Tha Tum, Mahasarakham province, is where both of them shared a house with a man and his wife. SOPHIA MUELLER. June 13, 2016.
