Curatorial Notes: The Stag Mask is worn by the Tibetan Buddhist Monk to perform a Cham “Stag Dance” as part of a religious festival. When performed by several dancers, the stag dance banishes bad spirits in preparation for the New Year. A solitary dancer evokes the Buddhist story in which Saint Milarepa saves a deer from a hunter and his dog, enacting the deer’s gratitude to Milarepa for saving his life. The deer makes many other appearances in Tibetan Buddhist art and fables as a symbol of harmony, faithfulness, and peace. Berthold Laufer collected this mask from the Tantric Buddhist temple in Beijing in the early 20th century; similar masks are worn in performances today, both in Tibet and internationally.

Researchers' Remarks
This piece is one of six vajras, which in Sanskrit means “adamant”, “diamond”, or “thunderbolt.” In Tibetan Buddhist tradition they are a “magical weapon and common symbol of power, indestructibility, and immutability”. There are several types of vajras used in tantric rituals, varying in both size and the number of “points” or prongs, usually ranging from one to nine on each side. The piece of red cotton clothing tied to this vajra is not common, it might have been attached claiming protection, or the color red could be alluding to the Padma family and its wisdom. Amitabha is a Buddha of the Padma family whose color is red the color of fire, passion and heat. Though is rare to see this type of attachments in vajras, is common to encounter the use of pieces of cloth to liven up dance performances in Tibetan region. The color red was not chosen by chance; it is used along various motifs in Buddhist traditions like monks’ robes, prayer flags, bracelets and offerings in different ceremonies. Given the fact that the red cloth is not common in vajras and the wood is not a usual material, this piece demonstrates how objects are by no means static and their uses vary from culture to culture. This piece is from the Jacob H. Schiff expedition to China and collected by Berthold Laufer, where he was to make a comprehensive ethnographic collection and to conduct scholarly research on the history and culture of a sophistica. Curatorial Notes: This toy, collected in China in the early 20th century, represents Camelus bactrianus or two-humped camel. Wild Bactrian camels inhabit the Gobi and Taklamakan deserts, which arc across Mongolia and northwestern China. One-humped Dromedary camels are found primarily in the Middle East and the African Sahara. Bactrian camels were so common a form of transport in northernmost China that camel images are often found among the votive offerings placed in 5th to 8th century tombs to serve the deceased in the afterlife. They seem to have been popular toys in Beijing around 1900.

Curatorial Notes: The cloth toy presents the tiger as a guardian, protective and harmless. It’s bright colors and large eyes are inviting and innocent while the toy’s wide grin conceals the animal’s notoriously sharp teeth. Tiger images in China and throughout East Asia are often deployed as powerful protectors against malevolent unseen forces. Although this tiger was collected in 1903, similar toys were produced throughout the 20th century and into the 21st.
Publication History
• ASIA: TRADITIONS AND TREASURES. FAIRSERVIS, WALTER A, JR. EXHIBITION CATALOG. 1981
Researchers' Remarks
The cloth toy tells one side of the tiger tale—the tiger as a guardian, protective and harmless. The toy, made of cotton and accented with pigment and beads, stands no more than 5 inches tall; it is brightly colored with large eyes that are inviting and innocent and a wide grin that conceals the animal’s notoriously sharp teeth. The guardian motif has long been embedded within Chinese folklore and mythology, as tigers are thought to protect against unwanted spirits, demons, and illness. Tiger parts, such as teeth, bones, meat, fur, and whiskers, and are highly sought after for use in medicinal remedies and as protective talismans. While the toy was collected in 1903 during an expedition intended to “illustrate the popular customs and beliefs of the Chinese, their industries, and their mode of life,” at the time, cloth tigers remain a popular form of folk art across China today.The toy tiger was collected in 1903 by anthropologist Dr. Berthold Laufer as he led the Schiff Expedition through Eastern Asia. During his career, Laufer worked for the American Museum of Natural History, lectured at Columbia University, and was as the curator of Asian Anthropology at the Field Museum. ALLISON CUTLER. June 13, 2016.
