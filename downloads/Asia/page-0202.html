Researchers' Remarks
The Apatani of the Ziro Valley, located in the North-Eastern Indian district of Arunachal Pradesh, have retained their distinctive traditions due to being topographically cut off from the other populated areas in the region by a mountain range. Apatani women are renowned for their highly sophisticated weaving techniques. Even though they could have grown cotton themselves, they usually traded the raw material with their Dafla neighbors. While occasionally cotton was bartered in exchange for swords and knives, Apatani women could obtain cotton supplies in exchange for their services as weavers. As such, they would procure the raw cotton, gin it, and then spin it. The thread on the spindle is colorless, yet it would be dyed with natural materials (Apatanis are associated with the color blue) and subsequently weft into various cloths. Due to increased access to the markets in Assam, Apatani often employ mercerized cotton, blended polyester, and machine-spun woolen and acrylic yarns to produce their traditional textiles. The spindle was gathered by the Austrian anthropologist and archaeologist C. Fürer-Haimendorf during his expeditions in 1944 and 1945. C. Fürer-Haimendorf is renown for his many publications and research in India. SOPHIA MUELLER. June 13, 2016.
