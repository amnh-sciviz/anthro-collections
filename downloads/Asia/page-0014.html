Researchers' Remarks
Pieced together from brightly colored fragments of silk and cloth, this wedding jacket from the Nanai people of southern Siberia reflects the blending of different Asian cultural styles. As a material, silk inspired a network of trade routes known as the Silk Road. Throughout the east, these networks permitted the sharing of ideas and material goods including silk garments like the bridegroom’s coat. The spirit of the Silk Road lives on in this extraordinary textile. Looking closely, the influence of Chinese imperial power that extended over the Nanai people can be seen in design. Traditionally, the Nanai were a fishing culture. As many of their villages were located on the banks of the region’s rivers, they employed fish bone and even skin to make their clothing. During the seventh century CE, the material culture of the Nanai began to reflect the distinct Chinese style. After the imperial emperors began occupying the area, the Nanai people began absorb their taste for certain motifs and luxury fabrics like silk. Those who could afford such a product traded for the material and used it for garments like the bridegroom’s coat. [1/2]. DANIELLE ZWANG. June 13, 2016.
In particular, this wedding jacket is filled with the Chinese influences. The wedding coat captures the spirit of the dragon robes worn by emperors of the Qing dynasty (1644–1911). Possibly recycled for old robes, the patches form scales on the back to create a dragon robe. In Chinese mythology, the dragon was very important. However the Nanai did not completely abandon their heritage. Instead, they incorporated Nanai motifs and fabric at the hem. Like the Silk Road, these garments display the intersection of cultures in Asia. [2/2]. DANIELLE ZWANG. June 13, 2016.

Researchers' Remarks
This bridal robe belongs to the Nanai which are located near the Amur River in the Russian Far East. The location explains why the styles of the embroidery in the garment are imbued with Chinese spirit, which the Nanai appropriated and kept on developing independently. The needlework done by women in this beautiful cotton garment is a tradition regarded as a great merit. The use of cotton became popular amongst the Nanai on the second half of the 19th century; before they mainly used fish skin. The designs on the robe are cocks, which are a very iconic image in clothing within the Nanai culture. The birds represent the soul and traditionally an unborn child or infant is said to be a reincarnated soul. Due to this connection the illustrated birds represent the unborn children of the bride on wedding robes. Most of the designs are in the back of the dress because according to their legends these designs are supposed to protect a woman from what is not visible to her eyes. Another reason can be the fact that a woman never faces their guest directly in traditional society. The Nanai bridal dress was collected in the Jesup North Pacific Expedition by Berthold Laufer who conducted ethnographic research on the Amur area. This expedition was led by Franz Boas. XIMENA VIAL.
