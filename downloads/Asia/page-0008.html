Researchers' Remarks
These undecorated summer boots from Siberia are attributed to Maritime Chukchi. Reaching to the knee, this pair used to be worn by the whaling-crews or by the -miners. Made mainly of seal hide which is favored for its thickness and oiliness, stitched with sinew as well, they are nearly impervious to water. With soles made of seal hide with the hair side in, grass insoles would be used since individuals would wear summer boots without socks. Instead of being merely regarded as regular clothing, boots had two other uses. Every summer, thousands of boots would be bartered for other commodities. A boot could also participate in different ceremonies, for example, being suspended as a substitute for the so-called “divining-stone” during divination and in the ceremony for a new-born. Researchers' Remarks
The qelutviaq is a single-stringed instrument carved from wood. It is held under the chin like a violin or across the lap, and played by either plucking the sinew string or using the wooden bow. As the instrument cannot be tuned, it likely produced an imprecise, atonal sound that reflected Chukchi musicality, and throat-singing techniques that emulated the sounds of nature. Accounts from the mid-19th century describe its use by Chukchi men in performance while in the company of a group of English crewmen, and the use of native drums by the crewmen. (Krejci 2010: 54) Although this specific instrument was collected from a Yupik community, similar instruments appear throughout native communities of Eastern Russia, Siberia, Alaska and Canada, suggesting its widespread movement across the area. Researchers' Remarks
Bogros observed the wearer of this shade steering the craft the wearer owned on the Bering Sea while the harpooner in the boat wore a similar eye, shade AMNH60/3590. The top portion of the shade, facing the sun is decorated with an orca in the center flanked by two walruses. The walruses are swimming toward the wearer and the orca is swimming away. The differences in direction may reference walruses swimming north in the spring. Bogoras makes note of connection to masks which likely relates to desires to attract and catch walrus through representations on objects. This also likely connects to complex ideas surrounding physical and supernatural sight. When wearing a shade the wearer would have his field of vision limited but also be able to see without the glare of the sun in his eyes. COLLEEN SWIFT, MA MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY STUDENT. April 30, 2019.

Researchers' Remarks
Before the introduction of shotguns, Chukchee and the neighboring Yupik of Eastern Siberia used bolas as hunting weapons to capture low flying birds. This hunting gear was hand made with strings of sinew tied to ivory or bone balls, or stones that were used for weights. As birds flew over, hunters twirled the bola and threw it into the flock, turning like a wheel until they entangled the birds' wings and necks bringing them to the ground. Hunters would wear bolas coiled on their heads or around their waists for easy transport. On lakes or sea, they would use bolas with weights made of woods since they would float. ALEXANDRA FANELLI.
