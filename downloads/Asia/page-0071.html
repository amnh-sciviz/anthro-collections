Curatorial Notes: Although the fearsome tiger (Panthera tigris) has vanished from most of the Chinese landscape, it remains an important symbol of power, prowess, and grandeur. In this painting, as in many Chinese portrayals, the tiger is depicted as a king of animals, with the four stripes on the tiger’s forehead forming the character wang or “king.” The inscription attributes the painting to T’ang Yin, a prominent scholar and painter, and one of the “Four Masters of the Ming Period,” but this is unlikely because having taken the pen name of “Eldest Tiger” (Bo Hu), T’ang seems never to have painted a tiger. Berthold Laufer, who collected this painting at the turn of the last century, made a careful study of the paintings he purchased from dealers and aficionados but in this instance, the young collector was probably naïve.
