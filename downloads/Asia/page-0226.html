Curatorial Notes: Called Mata ni Pachedi, or “sacred cloth of the Mother Goddess,” this block-printed cotton textile is used by the Vaghris, an underprivileged community in the Indian state of Gujarat, for religious worship. Forbidden from entering temples, the Vaghris attached four or five of these textiles to create a canopied tent as a temporary shrine for various Hindu rituals in honor of the Mother Goddess. These textiles always feature a manifestation of the goddess as their central figure, in this instance, the goddess Meladi who rides a black male goat. Images of other deities, symbolic animals and plants surround her to visually depict a myth.

Mata ni Pachedi are printed on inexpensive cotton cloth from the factories Gujarat, the largest producers of industrial cotton in India since the 19th century. Affordable cotton allowed the Vaghris to maintain their religious identity in spite of their social and economic limitations, and the production of Mata ni Pachedi flourished. Vaghri printers created the elaborate imagery on the textile using wood block prints that allow for consistency and efficiency. The textile always features a central image of a goddess, surrounded by smaller figures and decorative motifs. The deep reds, saturated blacks and bright whites are achieved by a complex process of dyeing and washing. Today, collectors prize these cloths for their appeal as folk art.
