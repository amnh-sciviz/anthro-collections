Researchers' Remarks
While kimono once were worn as everyday attire, it has become popular practice today to don kimono as formal wear for special occasions by people in Japan during the last several decades. On her wedding day the bride wears this robe as an uchikake, an overrobe, under which she wears layers of other kimono. Red, gold, and silver and the butterfly motif indicate good fortune in her future life as a wife and a harmonious marriage. The bride’s uchikake uses brocade—either a satin weave in metallic threads over a plain weave or silk crepe dyed after weaving. After the ceremony concludes, she changes into a different robe, signifying her change from young single girl to married woman. LAUREN ACKERLEY, RESEARCHER NOTES. May 19, 2015.

Curatorial Notes: The Siberian Tiger has disappeared from South Korean mountains but tigers are present in South Korean folklore and folk art as approachable icons. In the early 20th century, woodblock prints like this one would have either been hung on the house wall or folded up and carried on one’s person as protective talismans. The ideographs signify wishes for peace and good fortune while an “official” is trapped inside the spiral at the top of the tiger’s curled tail. The configuration of “insects” and “metal” inside the square on the right and the amulet writing on the tiger’s chest have magical intentions. The original block belonged to Dr. Cho Cha-ryong (Zozayong) whose writing and lectures encouraged Koreans to look with fresh eyes on folk representations of Korean tigers. This print was collected in the 1980s but this same image is reproduced in a variety of media in the early 20th century.
Researchers' Remarks
Tigers have disappeared from the wild in South Korea, yet they remain at home in Korean folklore. These traditions have depicted tigers as august and sometimes-silly icons with practical uses. People often hung tiger prints on their doors to ward off inauspicious forces at the start of the year. The Chinese ideograms on this print’s upper-left and right may signify fortune; its other ideograms may have served magical ends. This print came from a woodblock owned by Dr. Cho-cha Yong, founder-director of Seoul’s Emille Museum. With his collection of madcap tiger faces, Cho awakened interest in Korean tiger images and their anonymous producers. Dr. Laurel Kendall, anthropologist of Korea and Division Chair and curator of AMNH’s Anthropology Division, acquired this in 1988. RJ PETTERSEN, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY SOCIOLOGY STUDENT. May 15, 2015.
The Siberian Tiger has disappeared from South Korea’s shrinking wilderness, yet tigers remain at home in Korean folk traditions. Over the centuries, mass-produced amulets like the tiger woodblock print, well-known tales like “The Tiger and the Persimmon,” and modern mascots like Hodori have reanimated tigers as august, fearsome, and sometimes-silly icons: as symbolic housecats that exist not for aesthetic appreciation, but for practical use. The creator and provenance of this print’s original woodblock may be lost to time, but the print’s practical function is clear. At the start of the new year, people often hung prints—and especially tiger prints—on their front doors in order to protect their homes from natural disasters and other inauspicious fo. RJ PETTERSEN, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY SOCIOLOGY STUDENT. May 15, 2015.
