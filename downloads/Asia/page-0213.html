Curatorial Notes: For more than a thousand years, Chinese emperors wore robes decorated with bold dragon designs. Under the Qing dynasty (1644-1911 C.E.) law, only the emperor and his immediate family were allowed to wear the five-clawed dragon, or long, embroidered on the robe shown here. Those of lower rank had to settle for dragons with fewer claws-though by the end of the dynasty, these rules were often ignored. Some dragon robes were made for the theater (like this one), with features exaggerated for colorful effect on the stage.
Exhibition History
MYTHIC CREATURES: DRAGONS, UNICORNS & MERMAIDS AMNH, NEW YORK, NY. May 2007
Exhibition Label: WOMANS ROBE. Silk, dye, metallic thread, copper alloy. Beijing, China. 1800s. For more than a thousand years, Chinese emperors wore robes decorated with bold dragon designs. Under Qing dynasty (16441911) laws, only the emperor and his immediate family were allowed to wear the five-clawed dragon, or long, embroidered on the robe shown here. Those of lower rank had to settle for dragons with fewer clawsthough by the end of the dynasty, these rules were often ignored. Some dragon robes were made for the theater, with features exaggerated for colorful effect on the stage.
