Researchers' Remarks
This Chukchi shaman’s coat was collected by Bogoras at Mariinsky Post, Siberia in 1902 (Fig. 287 in Bogoras 1907). It is most likely made from reindeer hide, sinew, and fur, which serves as the coat’s lining and trim. Chukchi shamans ornamented their coats with white fringe and amulets and allowed them to become worn to increase their value (Bogoras 1907: 458). Its characteristic form identifies its owner as a male shaman; female shamans rarely wore such garments. Unlike in other forms of Siberian shamanism, this coat would not have been worn during ceremonies since Chukchi shamanistic rituals primarily took place in the dark, hot inner rooms of houses (Bogoras 1907: 457). Instead, it was most likely worn outdoors by a shaman, possibly Scratching Woman (Bogoras 1907: Fig. 287). Still, because Chukchi shamans held positions of elevated status in their communities, this coat would also have denoted the social standing of its wearer. MICHAELA WARSHAW, SHAMAN'S COAT RESEARCH NOTE (2019). April 28, 2019.
