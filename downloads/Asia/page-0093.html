Researchers' Remarks
These wool clippers came to the American Museum of Natural History in 1920 as part of a large donation of Himalayan materials funded by American financier J.P. Morgan. The objects were collected by Moravian missionary Herman B. Marx in Ladakh, a region now located in the contemporary Indian state of Jammu and Kashmir. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
Goat wool, particularly that which comes from Kashmir in northern India where these wool clippers were collected, is considered to be of particularly high quality and was often reserved for high end products. Today, products sold as “Cashmere” continue to be made from goat wool and fetch higher prices than most other animal fiber products. While production of Cashmere happens on a global scale today, traditionally, Cashmere starts as the softer hair found closer to the skin of the Changthangi or Pashmina breed of goat. [2 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
High altitude living would likely not have been possible without animal fibers. In the Himalayas, domesticated sheep (Ovis aries), domesticated goats (Capra aegagrus hircus) and domesticated yaks (Bos grunniens) are the predominant sources of animal hair for wool production. Clippers such as these are the first an important part of the process of creating woolen fiber. [1 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
Goats usually have their hair cut twice annually. While the process of shearing animals has been largely mechanized since the advent of electricity, for thousands of years, rudimentary tools no more complicated than a pair of scissors were all that was available to small scale herders and farmers. In many parts of Asia and the world, objects like these still serve as the primary means of transforming fur into wool. [3 / 3]. DOUGLAS RAY, COLUMBIA UNIVERSITY, MUSEUM ANTHROPOLOGY GRADUATE STUDENT. May 11, 2015.
