Curatorial Notes: Whistle of gourd decorated with native cotton yarn, scleria seeds, deer hooves and parrot feathers. These whistles are made by men, both men and women play them at log races, young boys play them at any time. They are worn bandolier style from the cotton or palm fiber carrying cord. Collected with 318 other objects by AMNH anthropologist Laila Williamson on a Museum expedition to the Shavante in 1992.

WHISTLE
SOUTH AMERICAN ETHNOGRAPHIC COLLECTION
 
Catalog No: 40.1/ 5588
Culture: SHAVANTE
Locale: MATO GROSSO, PIMENTEL BARBOSA RESERVATION
Country: BRAZIL
Date: 1960s-1970s
Material: INSECT (COCOON), SEED (A'E), FEATHER (EMU), CORD (COTTON), BEESWAX/ CRUSHED ROOT MIXTURE
Dimensions: L:62 W:16 (in CM)
Acquisition Year: 1992 [EXPEDITION]
Donor: WILLIAMSON, LAILA
Keywords: CEREMONIAL WHISTLE
Category: SOUND DEVICES
Share Object
Add Object to Portfolio
Curatorial Notes: This ceremonial whistle, sibhí, is an insect cocoon with one end cut open. It is decorated with two emu feathers and hangs from a cotton cord. Men make these whistles and play them outside the village, to make the sound of forest spirits, during the boys' initiation ceremony (wai'a). Women and uninitiated boys are not allowed to see them. Suptó, the chief of the Shavante village Pimentel Barbosa, gave two of these whistles to AMNH anthropologist Laila Williamson who as a non-Shavante and non-Indian woman was allowed to receive them. He gave them to her outside the village when she was leaving the Shavante in May 1992.
