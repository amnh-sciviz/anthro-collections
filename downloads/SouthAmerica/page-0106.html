Curatorial Notes: A mask resembling a giant face covered with many-colored parrot and flamingo feathers is the most elaborate mask the Tapirapé make. Called upé anchunga, this type of mask represents the spirit of an enemy killed in battle in the past and was owned by a specific Tapirapé man. Part of a masked dance outfit, a mask like this one was danced on ceremonial occasions in the village plaza. Originally purchased at a Brazilian Indian Service depository in Brasilia by Eugene J. Manzione of Brooklyn, this and another mask were confiscated by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service which donated the material to AMNH in 1988.

Curatorial Notes: This headband of native cotton is covered with parrot and cracid feathers, from it hang long, many-colored macaw and black curassow feathers as well as human hair. Rikbaktsá men wear this elaborately constructed headdress at the back of the head on ceremonial occasions. Donated to AMNH in 1988 by Richard Melnyk, the owner of an Amazonian Indian arts store in Manaus, Brazil. The headdress is on display in the Hall of South American Peoples.
